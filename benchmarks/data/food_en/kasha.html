Buckwheat grains

In the English language, kasha is a term for the pseudocereal buckwheat. In Central and Eastern Europe, especially Russia and Poland, kasha is a dish made of any kind of grains boiled in water or milk, possibly with additives, i.e., a porridge.

The largest gross consumption per capita is in Russia, with {{convert|15|kg|abbr=on}} per year, and Ukraine, with {{convert|12|kg|abbr=on}} per year.<ref>No 8 &#91;008&#93; 26 жовтня, 2007; [www.agro-business.com.ua/.../1655-2013-06-25-11]</ref> The share of buckwheat in the total consumption of cereals in Russia is 20%.<ref>Russian Market of Buckwheat in 2009 - September 2010</ref>

This English-language usage probably originated with Jewish immigrants, as did the form {{lang|yi|קאַשי}} kashi (technically plural, literally translated as "porridges").<ref>Steinmetz, Sol. Dictionary of Jewish Usage: A Guide to the Use of Jewish Terms. p. 42. ISBN 0-7425-4387-0.</ref>

The word generally refers to roasted whole-grain buckwheat of buckwheat groats.{{citation needed|date=February 2014}} However, in Slavic Europe, it refers to porridge in general and can be made from buckwheat or any cereal wheat, barley, oats, millet and rye. At least 1,000 years old, kasha is one of the oldest known dishes in Central European and Eastern European cuisine.<ref>Molokhovets, Elena (1998). Classic Russian Cooking. Indiana University Press. p. 331.</ref><ref>http://www.lesliebeck.com/ingredients/kasha</ref&gt;

In Russian, buckwheat is referred to formally as {{lang|ru|гречиха}} (grechikha) and buckwheat grain and buckwheat groats as {{lang|ru|гречневая крупа}} (grechnevaya krupa). Informally buckwheat grain and buckwheat groats are called {{lang|ru|гречка}} (grechka), and the porridge made from buckwheat groats is known as {{lang|ru|гречневая каша}} (grechnevaya kasha). In Polish, buckwheat porridge is referred to as {{lang|pl|kasza gryczana}}. Annual (2013) per capita consumption of groats in Poland is approx. {{convert|1.56|kg|abbr=on}} per year ({{convert|130|g|abbr=on}} a month).<ref>Biuletyn Informacyjny ARR 4/2013, Handel Wewnętrzny 4/2013 IBRKK</ref>

In Russian culture

Kasha is commemorated in the Russian saying "щи да каша{{spaced ndash}}пища наша" (shchi da kasha{{spaced ndash}}pishcha nasha) literally "shchi and kasha are our food" or, more loosely, "cabbage soup and porridge are our kind of food".

Kasha can be used at any meal, either as a dish in itself, or a side dish.

In Jewish culture

Retrieved May 30, 2007.</ref> and is sometimes included in matzah-ball soup.{{citation needed|date=February 2014}}
