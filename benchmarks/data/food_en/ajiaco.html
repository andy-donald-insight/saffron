Ajiaco is one of the city's most representative dishes in Bogotá, Colombia

Ajiaco ({{IPA-es|aˈxjako}}) is a soup common to <ref name="cuban">{{cite web|url=http://www.tasteofcuba.com/ajiacocubano.html |title=Cuban Ajiaco Recipe |publisher=Tasteofcuba.com |date= |accessdate=2014-06-03}}</ref> Colombia.<ref name="nytimes">http://www.nytimes.com/2011/11/02/dining/from-colombia-the-ultimate-one-pot-meal-a-good-appetite.html?_r=0</ref&gt;

In the Colombian capital of Bogotá, ajiaco is a popular dish typically made with chicken, three varieties of potatoes, and the Galinsoga parviflora herb, commonly referred to in Colombia as guascas.<ref name="mycolombian">{{cite web|url=http://www.mycolombianrecipes.com/ajiaco-bogotano-colombian-chicken-and |title=Ajiaco Bogotano (Colombian Chicken and Potato Soup) |publisher=Mycolombianrecipes.com |date=2009-03-19 |accessdate=2014-06-03}}</ref><ref>[1]</ref> In Cuba, ajiaco is a hearty stew made from beef, pork, chicken, vegetables, and a variety of starchy roots and tubers classified as viandas.<ref>Garth, Hanna. 2013 Food and Identity in the Caribbean. Bloomsbury Press.</ref><ref name="cuban"/>

The exact origin of this dish has been debated by scholars.<ref name="cuban"/> In his book Lexicografia Antillana, former president of Cuba Alfredo Zayas y Alfonso stated that the word "ajiaco" derived from "aji", the native Taíno word for "hot pepper."<ref name="ecured">{{cite web|url=http://www.ecured.cu/index.php/Ajiaco_cobrero |title=Ajiaco cobrero |language=Spanish|publisher=Ecured.cu |date= |accessdate=2014-06-03}}</ref> Cuban ethnologist Fernando Ortiz stated that ajiaco was a meal typical of the Taíno, and was an appropriate metaphor for Cuba being a melting pot.<ref name="ecured"/> In the Cuban city of Camagüey, the San Juan festival begins with the making and serving of ajiaco.<ref name="lacalle">{{cite web|url=http://www.lacalle.cu/index.php/2013/06/25/el-ajiaco-de-camaguey/ |title=El ajiaco de Camagüey |publisher=Lacalle.cu |date=2013-06-25 |accessdate=2014-06-03}}</ref> La Calle magazine of Cuba stated that the inhabitants of the village of Santa María de Puerto del Príncipe began the tradition of making ajiaco using their own cooking ingredients, donations from passersby, surplus from farmers, and surplus slave provisions.<ref name="lacalle"/> Ajiaco is believed to have become popular in Cuba during the 16th century, particularly among rural Cubans, although it was occasionally enjoyed by the upper class.<ref name="cubanow">http://www.cubanow.net/articles/emblematic-dish-ajiaco</ref&gt;

In Peru, ajiaco is a quite different dish of potatoes cooked with garlic, a mix of dried yellow and red chilies (aji mirasol and aji panca), hierba buena, and huacatay, generally accompanied by rice and stewed chicken or rabbit.
