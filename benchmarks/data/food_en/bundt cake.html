David Dalquist | course = Dessert | type = Cake | served = | main_ingredient = Flour, sugar, eggs | variations = | calories = | other = }} A Bundt cake {{IPAc-en|b|ʌ|n|t}} is a cake that is baked in a Bundt pan, shaping it into a distinctive ring shape. The shape is inspired by a traditional European cake known as {{lang|de|Gugelhupf}}, but Bundt cakes are not generally associated with any single recipe. The style of mold in North America was popularized in the 1950s and 60s, after cookware manufacturer Nordic Ware trademarked the name "Bundt" and began producing Bundt pans from cast aluminum. Publicity from Pillsbury saw the cakes gain widespread popularity.

Etymology

The Bundt cake derives in part from a European brioche-like cake called {{lang|de|Gugelhupf}} which was particularly popular among Jewish communities in parts of Germany, Austria and Poland.<ref name=oxford>{{cite book |title=The Oxford Companion to Food |publisher = Oxford University Press |year = 2006 |isbn = 978-0-19-280681-9 |page = 441 |edition = 2nd |last = Davidson |first = Alan }}</ref> In the north of Germany {{lang|de|Gugelhupf}} is traditionally known as {{lang|de|Bundkuchen}} ({{IPA-de|ˈbʊntkuːxn}}), a name formed by joining the two words {{lang|de|Kuchen}} (cake) and {{lang|de|Bund}}.<ref name=wpost>{{cite news |url=http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A27813-2005Jan21.html |newspaper = The Washington Post |title = Who Brought the Bundt Cake? |date = 22 January 2005 |last = Pfrengle |first = Hermann |accessdate = 19 December 2012 }}</ref>

Opinions differ as to the significance of the word {{lang|de|Bund}}. One possibility is that it means "bunch" or "bundle", and refers to the way the dough is bundled around the tubed center of the pan.<ref name=wpost/> Another source suggests that it describes the banded appearance given to the cake by the fluted sides of the pan, similar to a tied sheaf or bundle of wheat.<ref name=franklin>{{cite book |title = 300 Years of Kitchen Collectibles |last = Campbell Franklin |first = Linda |publisher = Krause Publications |pages = 187–8 |edition = 5th |ISBN = 978-0-87349-365-9 |year = 2003 }}</ref> Some authors have suggested that {{lang|de|Bund}} instead refers to a group of people, and that {{lang|de|Bundkuchen}} is so called because of its suitability for parties and gatherings.<ref name=wpostobit>{{cite news |title = Bundt Pan Creator H. David Dalquist, 86 |url = http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/articles/A51960-2005Jan5.html |date = 6 January 2005 |accessdate = 19 December 2012 |agency = Associated Press |newspaper = The Washington Post }}</ref><ref name=oklahoman>{{cite news |title = Bundt pan fans; Fluted cakes popular for six decades |newspaper = The Oklahoman |last = Dowell |first = Sharon |date = 17 May 2006 }}</ref>

Uses of the word "bund" to describe cakes outside of Europe can be found in Jewish-American cookbooks from around the start of the 20th century.<ref name=babette>{{cite book |chapter=Plain Bund, or Napf Kuchen |title= Aunt Babette's Cook Book |url = http://digital.lib.msu.edu/projects/cookbooks/html/books/book_41.cfm |publisher = Block Pub and Print Co |place = Cincinnati |year = 1889 |pages = 326–327 |oclc = 3903063 }}</ref><ref name=meier>{{cite book |title=The Art of German Cooking and Baking |chapter = Bund-Kuchen |last = Meier |first = Lina |year = 1909 |publisher = Wetzel Bros Printing Co |place = Milwaukee |page = 337 |oclc = 13378934 }}</ref> The alternative spelling "bundte" also appears in a recipe as early as 1901.<ref name=kander>{{cite book |chapter = Bundte Kuchen |last = Kander |first = Lizzie Black |title = The Settlement Cook Book |year = 1901 |publisher = American Crayon Co |place = Sandusky, OH |accessdate = 19 December 2012 |url = http://www.wisconsinhistory.org/turningpoints/search.asp?id=910 }}</ref>

Design

Bundt-style pans in silicone and metal

Bundt cakes do not conform to any single recipe; instead their characterizing feature is their shape. A Bundt pan generally has fluted or grooved sides, but its most defining design element is the central tube or "chimney" which leaves a cylindrical hole through the center of the cake. The design means that more of the mixture touches the surface of the pan than in a simple round pan, helping to provide faster and more even heat distribution during cooking.<ref name=wpost/><ref name=fante>{{cite web |url = http://www.fantes.com/tube-pans.html |title = Tube Pans |accessdate = 20 December 2012 |publisher = Fante's }}</ref> The shape is similar to that of the earlier European {{lang|de|Gugelhupf}} or {{lang|de|Bundkuchen}}. A {{lang|de|Gugelhupf}} differs from contemporary Bundt-style cakes in that it follows a particular yeast-based recipe, with fruit and nuts, and is often deeper in shape and more decorative.<ref name=oxford/> Also similar in shape is the Eastern European Babka, dating from early 18th century Poland.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.twojaeuropa.pl/1441/il-bab--ciastko-o-niezwyklym-smaku-i-historii|title=Il Babà - ciastko o niezwykłym smaku i historii|date=17 March 2010|last=Berny|first=Iwona|language=Polish|trans_title=Babka: a cake with a unique flavor and history}}</ref> While Babka is associated with Jewish culture, Bundt cake is firmly set in Christian tradition and is traditionally baked for Christmas and Easter. Today, there is no recipe for "Bundt cake". Anything can be baked in a Bundt-style pan, and is. Recipes range from Pine Nut and Chili cakes to ice cream and fruit concoctions. And, Bundt-style pan design has expanded beyond the original fluted ring to today's designs of skylines, octopus and cathedrals, all with the requisite hole in the center of the pan<ref name=BundtBook/> made by Nordic Ware and others. Since a toroidal cake is rather difficult to frost, Bundt cakes are typically either dusted with powdered sugar, drizzle-glazed, or served undecorated. Recipes specifically designed for Bundt pans often have a baked-in filling; Bundt pound cakes are also common.{{cn|date=November 2013}}

Since the name "Bundt" is a trademark, similar pans are often sold as "fluted tube pans" or given other similar descriptive titles.<ref name=fante/> The trademark holder Nordic Ware only produces Bundt pans in aluminum, but similar fluted pans are available in other materials.<ref>File:Napfkuchen-Form.jpg</ref>

Rise to popularity

Rainbow Bundt cake, partially sliced

The people credited with popularizing the Bundt cake are American businessman H. David Dalquist and his brother Mark S. Dalquist {{cn|date=November 2013}}, who co-founded cookware company Nordic Ware based in St. Louis Park, Minnesota. In the late 1940s,<ref name=BundtBook>{{cite book |title= Bundt Cake Bliss |last = Short |first = Susanna |publisher = Minnesota Historical Society Press |place = St. Paul |year = 2007 |chapter=And the Bundy was Born! |pages = 1–4 |ISBN = 978-0-87351-585-6 }}</ref> Rose Joshua and Fannie Schanfield, friends and members of the Minneapolis Jewish-American Hadassah Society approached Dalquist asking if he could produce a modern version of a traditional cast iron {{lang|de|Gugelhupf}} dish.<ref name=wpost/> Dalquist and company engineer Don Nygren designed a cast aluminum version which Nordic Ware then made a small production run of in 1950. In order to successfully trademark the pans, a "t" was added to the word "Bund".<ref name=oklahoman/> A number of the original Bundt pans now reside in the Smithsonian collection.<ref name=startribune>{{cite news |first = Matt |last = McKinney |url = http://nl.newsbank.com/nl-search/we/Archives?p_product=MN&p_theme=mn&p_action=search&p_maxdocs=200&s_dispstring=(Smithsonian%20gobbles%20up%20Bundt%20Pan%27)&p_field_advanced-0=&p_text_advanced-0=(%22Smithsonian%20gobbles%20up%20Bundt%20Pan%27%22)&xcal_numdocs=20&p_perpage=10&p_sort=YMD_date:D&xcal_useweights=no |title = Smithsonian Gobbles up Bundt Pan |newspaper = Star Tribune |place = Minneapolis |date = 24 February 2007 |accessdate = 2007-03-01 }}</ref>

Initially, the Bundt pan sold so poorly that Nordic Ware considered discontinuing it.<ref name=startribune/> The product received a boost when it was mentioned in the New Good Housekeeping Cookbook in 1963,<ref name=anderson>{{cite book |title = American Century Cookbook: The Most Popular Recipes of the 20th Century |publisher = Clarkson Potter |place = New York |year = 1997 |page = 458 |ISBN = 978-0-517-70576-6 |last = Anderson |first = Jean }}</ref><ref name=nghcb>{{cite book |title = The New Good Housekeeping Cookbook |publisher = Harcourt, Brace & World |year = 1963 |place = New York |oclc = 378017 }}</ref> but did not gain real popularity until 1966, when a Bundt cake called the "Tunnel of Fudge", baked by Ella Helfrich, took second place at the annual Pillsbury Bake-Off and won its baker $5,000.<ref name=BundtBook/><ref name=anderson/> The resulting publicity resulted in more than 200,000 requests<ref name=BundtBook/> to Pillsbury for Bundt pans and soon led to the Bundt pan surpassing the tin Jell-O mold as the most-sold pan in the United States.{{citation needed|date=December 2012}} In the 1970s Pillsbury licensed the name Bundt from Nordic Ware and for a while sold a range of Bundt cake mixes.<ref name=oklahoman/>

To date more than 60 million Bundt pans have been sold by Nordic Ware across North America.<ref name=nordic>{{cite web |url=http://www.nordicware.com/about |title=About Us |publisher = Nordic Ware |accessdate = 19 December 2012 }}</ref>
