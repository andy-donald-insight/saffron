{{Infobox prepared food | name = Bath bun | image = | caption = | alternate_name = | country = United Kingdom | region = Somerset | creator = | course = | type = Sweet roll | served = | main_ingredient = Sugar, candied fruit peel, currants or raisins or sultanas | variations = | calories = | other = }} The Bath bun is a sweet roll made from a milk-based yeast dough.<ref name="Ayto2012">{{cite book|author=John Ayto|title=The Diner's Dictionary: Word Origins of Food and Drink|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=NoicAQAAQBAJ&pg=PA23|date=18 October 2012|publisher=Oxford University Press|isbn=978-0-19-964024-9|page=23}}</ref> It has a lump of sugar baked in the bottom and more crushed sugar sprinkled on top after baking.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.enjoyengland.com/ideas/food-and-drink/news-bites/edible-england/bath-bun.aspx|title=The Bath Bun|last=Sutton|first=Henry|publisher=Enjoy England|accessdate=27 March 2010}}</ref> Variations in ingredients include candied fruit peel, currants or larger raisins or sultanas.

The original 18th-century recipe used a brioche or rich egg and butter dough which was then covered with caraway seeds,<ref name="Davidson">Davidson, Alan, "Bun" in Oxford Companion to Food, Oxford: Oxford University Press], 1999), p.&nbsp;114. ISBN 0-19-211579-0</ref> coated in several layers of sugar similar to French drag√©e.<ref name="visitbath">{{cite web|url=http://visitbath.co.uk/site/eating-and-drinking/local-specialities-and-british-favourites/local-bath-delicacies|title=Local Bath Delicacies|publisher=Visit Bath|accessdate=27 March 2010}}</ref>

The bun's creation is attributed to William Oliver in the 18th century.<ref>Bender, David A. "Bath bun." In A Dictionary of Food and Nutrition. : Oxford University Press, http://www.oxfordreference.com</ref&gt; Oliver also created the Bath Oliver dry biscuit after the bun proved too fattening for his rheumatic patients.<ref>Biography of Dr Oliver</ref> The bun may also have descended from the 18th century 'Bath cake'.

References to Bath buns date from 1763,<ref name="AboutBrit">{{cite web|url=http://www.aboutbritain.com/towns/bath.asp|title=Bath|publisher=About Britain|accessdate=27 March 2010| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20100323103622/http://www.aboutbritain.com/towns/bath.asp| archivedate= 23 March 2010 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref> and Jane Austen wrote in a letter of 'disordering my stomach with Bath Bunns' in 1801.<ref name="Ayto2012"/> The buns are still produced in the Bath area of England.<ref name="AboutBrit"/>

Although this is disputed, the 18th century 'Bath cake' may also have been the forerunner of the Sally Lunn bun, which also originates from Bath.<ref name="visitbath"/><ref name="Davidson"/>
