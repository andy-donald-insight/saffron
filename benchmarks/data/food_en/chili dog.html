Chili dog is the generic name for a hot dog served in a bun and topped with some sort of meat sauce, often chili con carne (usually with beans) or some other ground meat sauce. Often other toppings are also added, such as cheese, onions, and mustard. The style has multiple regional variations in the United States, many calling for specific and unique sauce ingredients, types of hot dogs, or types of buns and referred to regionally under region-specific names. Many regional variations were developed independently by Greek immigrant restaurateurs.

Regional variations

Texas wiener

{{seealso|Texas Tommy (hot dog)}} {{Infobox prepared food | name = Texas hot dog | image = | caption = | alternate_name = Texas chili dog, Texas hot, Texas wiener | country = United States | region = New Jersey | creator = | course = | type = Hot dog | served = | main_ingredient = Hot dog bun, hot dog, chili con carne or hot sauce | variations = | calories = | other = }} In New Jersey and Pennsylvania, the Texas hot dog, Texas chili dog, Texas hot, or Texas wiener is a hot dog with chili or hot sauce; it is served in variations with assorted condiments. The Texas wiener was created in Paterson, New Jersey before 1920<ref>{{cite book|last=Stern|first=Jane and Michael|title=Roadfood |year=2002|publisher=Broadway Books|isbn=0-7679-0809-0|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=roCHEntpvMMC&lpg=PP1&dq=Roadfood&pg=PA98|page=98}}</ref> and in Altoona, Pennsylvania by Peter "George" Koufougeorgas in 1918<ref>{{cite web|last=Mincin|first=Jimmy|title=Hot doggin' it|url=http://www.altoonamirror.com/page/content.detail/id/515838.html?showlayout=0|work=Altoona Mirror|date=February 5, 2009}}</ref> and originally called Texas Hot Wieners. The "Texas" reference is to the chili sauce used on the dogs, which actually has a stronger Greek cuisine influence due to the ethnicity of the cooks who invented it. It is considered a unique regional hot dog style. From its origins, the invention spread to the Pennsylvania cities of Scranton and Philadelphia.<ref>{{cite web|last=Krall|first=Hawk|title=Hot Dog Of The Week: Texas Wieners|url=http://www.seriouseats.com/2009/07/hot-dog-of-the-week-texas-weiner.html|publisher=Serious Eats|date=July 17, 2009}}</ref> Altoona's original Texas Hot Dogs shop is still open today in downtown Altoona on 12th street.

Hot wiener

{{Main|Hot wiener}}

Hot wieners

In Rhode Island the hot wiener or New York System wiener is a staple of the food culture and is served at "New York System" restaurants. The traditional wiener is made with a small, thin hot dog made of veal and pork, giving it a different taste from a traditional beef hot dog, served in a steamed bun, and topped with celery salt, yellow mustard, chopped onions, and a seasoned meat sauce.

Michigan hot dog

{{Main|Michigan hot dog}}

In the North Country of New York State, a Michigan hot dog, or "Michigan", is a steamed hot dog on a steamed bun topped with a meaty sauce, generally referred to as "Michigan sauce."

Cheese coney

{{Main|Cincinnati chili}}

Cheese coneys

In the Greater Cincinnati area, Cheese coneys or Coney Islands (without the cheese) are hot dogs in buns topped with Cincinnati chili (a Greek-inspired meat sauce), onions, mustard, and cheese.

Carolina style

{{Main|Carolina style}} In North Carolina, hot dogs topped with chili, onions, and either mustard or slaw are referred to as "Carolina style", which is also used to refer to hamburgers with similar toppings.

Half-smoke

{{Main|Half-smoke}} In Washington, D.C. the half-smoke is similar to a hot dog, but usually larger, spicier, and with more coarsely-ground meat, the sausage is often half-pork and half-beef, smoked, and served with herbs, onion, and chili sauce.
