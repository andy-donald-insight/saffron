Rēwena parāoa (literally, "leavened bread,"<ref name="metge102">{{cite book|last=Metge|first=Joan|title=Rautahi: The Maori of New Zealand|year=2004|publisher=Psychology Press|page=102|url=http://books.google.com/?id=gMv7fFg8AJIC&pg=PA107#v=onepage&q=rewena&f=false|isbn=9780415330572}}</ref> also spelled rewana) is a traditional Māori sourdough potato bread.

Etymology

Rēwena means both "bread made with potato yeast" and "the process of fermentation that causes bread to rise."<ref>{{cite web|title=Rewena|url=http://www.maoridictionary.co.nz/index.cfm?dictionaryKeywords=rewena&search.x=0&search.y=0&n=1&idiom=&phrase=&proverb=&loan=|work=Maori Dictionary|publisher=Te Whanake|accessdate=3 March 2013}}</ref> The Maori word for potato, 'riwai'<ref>{{cite web|title=Riwai|url=http://www.maoridictionary.co.nz/index.cfm?dictionaryKeywords=riwai&search.x=0&search.y=0&n=1&idiom=&phrase=&proverb=&loan=|work=Maori Dictionary|publisher=Te Whanake|accessdate=3 March 2013}}</ref> (sometimes spelled 'rewa'), is the root word of rēwena.<ref name="matarikifestival1">{{cite web|title=New World Rewena Paraoa Baking Competition|url=http://www.matarikifestival.org.nz/competitions/baking.asp|work=Matariki Festival Competitions|accessdate=6 March 2013}}</ref>

Preparation

Rēwena bread uses a pre-ferment starter, also called a ‘bug.’<ref>{{cite news|title=How to make the best Maori bread in town|url=http://www.nzherald.co.nz/lifestyle/news/article.cfm?c_id=6&objectid=10453825|accessdate=6 March 2013|newspaper=The New Zealand Herald|date=26 July 2007}}</ref> It is created by boiling and mashing potatoes, then adding flour and sugar.<ref>{{cite book|last=McLean|first=Mervyn|title=To Tatau Waka|year=2004|publisher=Auckland UniversityPress|page=48|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=gvc9n_xOWMUC&q=rewena#v=snippet&q=rewena&f=false}}</ref> Kumara, or sweet potatoes, may also be used.<ref>{{cite web|first=Kaitime|title=Paraoa Parai / Rewena Bread|url=http://www.kaitime.co.nz/index.php/recipes/desertsabreads/150-paraoa-parai--rewena-bread|work=Kaitime|accessdate=6 March 2013}}</ref> The mixture is then allowed to ferment from one to several days, depending on the ambient temperature and humidity.<ref name="olsen2007">{{cite web|last=Olsen|first=Nigel|title=Rewena Bread- the beginning|url=http://curiouskai.blogspot.com/2007/01/rewena-bread-beginning.html|work=Curious Kai: The Curious New Zealand Food Blog|accessdate=3 March 2013}}</ref> As with most sourdough breads, the starter can be maintained and used indefinitely, as long as the yeast is kept alive with regular feeding.<ref name="olsen2007"/> The potato starter and fermentation lends rēwena bread its characteristic sweet and sour taste.<ref name="olsen2007"/> The starter is then mixed with flour and water, kneaded, and baked- frequently in a round loaf.

Cultural significance

Rēwena may also be used to break the Maori taboo associated with visiting a cemetery by crumbling the bread over hands in lieu of washing with water.<ref>{{cite book|last=Albala|first=Ken|title=Food Cultures of the World Encyclopedia|year=2011|publisher=ABC-CLIO|page=173|url=http://books.google.com/?id=NTo6c_PJWRgC&pg=RA2-PA173&dq=rewena+urupa#v=onepage&q=rewena%20urupa&f=false|isbn=9780313376269}}</ref>

The "New World Rēwena Paraoa Baking Competition" is held during the Matariki Festival each year in New Zealand.<ref name="matarikifestival1"/>
