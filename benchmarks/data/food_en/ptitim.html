Ptitim ({{lang-he|פתיתים}}) is an Israeli toasted pasta shaped like rice or little balls. Outside of Israel, it is known as Israeli couscous or Jerusalem couscous.<ref name="Haaretz" /> In Israel it is sometimes colloquially and jokingly called "Ben-Gurion rice" (Hebrew: אורז בן-גוריון órez Ben-Gurion).

History

Ptitim was invented during the austerity period in Israel (from 1949 to 1959).<ref name="Haaretz"/> Israel's first prime minister, David Ben-Gurion, asked Eugen Proper{{sic|hide=y}}, one of the founders of the Osem food company, to quickly devise a wheat-based substitute to rice.<ref name="Haaretz">{{cite web |url=http://www.haaretz.com/ben-gurion-s-rice-1.245490 |author=Doram Gaunt |publisher=Haaretz |title=Ben-Gurion's Rice |accessdate=2012-06-05}}</ref> Consequently, it was nicknamed "Ben-Gurion's rice" by the people.<ref name="Haaretz"/> The company took up the challenge and developed ptitim, which is made of hard wheat flour and roasted in an oven.<ref name="Haaretz"/> The product was instantly a success, after which ptitim made in the shape of small, dense balls (which the company termed "couscous") was added to the original rice-shaped ptitim.

Preparation

Ptitim is popular among Israeli children, who eat it plain, or mixed with fried onion and tomato paste.<ref name="Haaretz"/> Ptitim are now produced in ring, star, and heart shapes for added appeal.<ref name="Haaretz"/> For health conscious consumers,<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.jpost.com/Business/BusinessNews/Article.aspx?id=27130 |author=Sharon Wrobel |publisher=The Jerusalem Post |title=Half of Israeli households buy low-fat products |accessdate=2012-06-05}}</ref> there are also whole-wheat and spelt flour ptitim.<ref name="Haaretz"/>

While considered a children's food in Israel, ptitim is sometimes treated as an ingredient for "trendy delicacies".<ref name="Haaretz"/> In the United States, it can be found on the menus of contemporary American chefs, and can be bought in gourmet markets.<ref name="JPOST">{{cite web |url=http://www.jpost.com/LandedPages/PrintArticle.aspx?id=60905 |author=Faye Levy |publisher=The Jerusalem Post |title=Petit ptitim |accessdate=2012-06-05}}</ref>

Ptitim can be used in many different types of dishes, both hot and cold. They retain their shape and texture even when reheated, and they do not clump together.<ref name="JPOST"/>

Commonly, ptitim are prepared with sautéed onions or garlic (vegetables, meat, chicken or sausages can also be added); the ptitim are then added, and fried for a short time before adding water.<ref name="Haaretz"/> Ptitim can also be used as a substitute for pasta or rice. They can also go in soup, can be baked, served in a pie, or made as a risotto.<ref name="Haaretz"/> American chef Charlie Trotter has produced a recipe for ptitim with spinach, artichoke, and Kalamata olives.<ref name="Haaretz"/>

Similar products

Pearl-shaped ptitim is similar to the Levantine pearled couscous known as maftoul or mograbieh in Palestine, Jordan, Syria and Lebanon.<ref name=GourmetSleuth>{{cite web|url=http://www.gourmetsleuth.com/Dictionary/I/Israeli-Couscous-5716.aspx|title=Israeli Couscous|publisher=GourmetSleuth.com|accessdate=2012-06-05}}</ref> It is also similar to the Kabyle Abazine and the Sardinian Fregula. Rice-shaped ptitim are similar to orzo.<ref name=GourmetSleuth/> Ptitim is not the same as Mograbia (aka Maftoul, Pearl Couscous etc.), though the two can be used similarly. Mograbia is a coated couscous; Ptitim is an extruded paste. Note as well that the word "Maftoul" is sometimes incorrectly used in America to refer to Israeli couscous.
