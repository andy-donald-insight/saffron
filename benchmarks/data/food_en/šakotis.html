nontraditional--> Lithuanian šakotis, Polish sękacz ("tree cake"; literally "branchy"<ref name="Lankauskas">{{cite book|last=Lankauskas|first=Gediminas|title=The Land of Weddings and Rain: Nation and Modernity in Post-Socialist Lithuania|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=GkriBQAAQBAJ&pg=PA208|year=2015|publisher=U of Toronto P|isbn=9781442612563|pages=208–11}}</ref>) is a Polish-Lithuanian traditional spit cake. It is a cake made of butter, eggs, flour, sugar, and cream, cooked on a rotating spit in an oven or over an open fire.

History

The cake became popular during the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth (1569–1791). Its origins are attributed to either the Italian Queen Bona Sforza of Poland or the Baltic tribe of Yotvingians. The Yotvingians settled in the early and high Middle Ages in Podlasie, while Bona Sforza is known to have implemented many agriculture, infrastructure and manufacture reforms.<ref>Klaus Klöppel: Polnische Ostseeküste: Danzig, Masuren, Baedeker, ISBN 3-8297-1171-9{{dead link|date=November 2011}}</ref>

Its name means "branched tree" or "tree with many branches" due to its distinctive shape (it is often conical, like a pine tree, and with the drips as branches). It is baked in a time- and labor-intensive process,<ref>{{cite book|last=Bremzen|first=Anya von|title=Det goda sovjetiska köket: Minnen, mat och längtan|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=ZMaKAwAAQBAJ&pg=PT242|year=2014|publisher=2244|language=Swedish|isbn=9789186729592|page=242}}</ref> by painting layers of dough onto a rotating spit in a special open oven or over an open fire.

It can be decorated with chocolate and flower ornaments, but it is often served plain. Šakotis is one of the most important desserts in Lithuanian celebrations, especially at weddings or other special occasions such as Christmas.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Baker|first1=Mark|last2=Presser|first2=Brandon|first3=Peter|last3=Dragicevich|first4=Simon|last4=Richmond|first5=Andy |last5=Symington|title=Lonely Planet Estonia, Latvia & Lithuania|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=ZZgzte6Y9yQC&pg=RA2-PA43|year=2012|publisher=Lonely Planet|isbn=9781743213049|page=43}}</ref>

It was the sweet chosen to represent Lithuania in the Café Europe initiative of the Austrian presidency of the European Union, on Europe Day 2006.

In May 2015, in Druskininkai, Lithuania the record of the biggest šakotis was broken with {{convert|372|cm|ft}} height and {{convert|85.8|kg|lb}} weight.<ref>Lietuvoje pasiektas rekordas: iškeptas didžiausias pasaulyje šakotis</ref>
