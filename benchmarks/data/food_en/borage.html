Borago officinalis

Borage ({{IPAc-en|ˈ|b|ʌr|ɪ-|dʒ}},<ref>{{OED|borage}}</ref> Borago officinalis), also known as a starflower, is an annual herb in the flowering plant family Boraginaceae. It is native to the Mediterranean region and has naturalized in many other locales.<ref>Altervista Flora Italiana, Borragine comune, gurkört, Borago officinalis L. includes photos, drawings, and European distribution map</ref> It grows satisfactorily in gardens in the UK climate, remaining in the garden from year to year by self-seeding. The leaves are edible and the plant is grown in gardens for that purpose in some parts of Europe. The plant is also commercially cultivated for borage seed oil extracted from its seeds.

Description

Borago officinalis grows to a height of {{convert|60|-|100|cm|ft|abbr=on}}, and is bristly or hairy all over the stems and leaves; the leaves are alternate, simple, and {{convert|5|-|15|cm|in|abbr=on}} long. The flowers are complete, perfect with five narrow, triangular-pointed petals. Flowers are most often blue, although pink flowers are sometimes observed. White flowered types are also cultivated. The blue flower is genetically dominant over the white flower.<ref name="springerlink.com">{{cite web|url=http://www.springerlink.com/content/4576pw551p5qbml6/fulltext.pdf |title=Geitonogamy: a mechanism responsible for high selfing rates in borage (Borago officinalis L.) - Springer |publisher=Springerlink.com |date= |accessdate=2013-09-13}}</ref> The flowers arise along scorpioid cymes to form large floral displays with multiple flowers blooming simultaneously, suggesting that borage has a high degree of geitonogamy (intra-plant pollination).<ref name="springerlink.com"/> It has an indeterminate growth habit which may lead to prolific spreading. In temperate climate such as in the UK, its flowering season is relatively long, from June to September. In milder climates, borage will bloom continuously for most of the year.

Characteristics and uses

A white flower cultivar

Two blossoms, the younger one is pink, the older blue

Traditionally borage was cultivated for culinary and medicinal uses, although today commercial cultivation is mainly as an oilseed.

Seed oil

Borage seed oil contains 17-28% of gamma-linolenic acid (GLA) and is the richest known source.<ref>National Non-Food Crops Centre. NNFCC Crop Factsheet: Borage, Retrieved on 16 Feb 2011</ref> The seed oil content is between 26-38%{{clarification needed|reason=26-38% of what? Seed dry weight?|date=November 2015}} and in addition to GLA contains{{clarification needed|reason= what contains?, in addition to what?|date=November 2015}} the fatty acids palmitic acid (10-11%), stearic acid (3.5-4.5%), oleic acid (16-20%), linoleic acid (35-38%), eicosenoic acid (3.5-5.5%), erucic acid (1.5-3.5%), and nervonic acid (1.5%). The oil is often marketed as "starflower oil" or "borage oil" for uses as a GLA supplement, although healthy adults will typically produce ample GLA through dietary linoleic acid.

Culinary

Borage is used as either a fresh vegetable or a dried herb. As a fresh vegetable, borage, with a cucumber-like taste, is often used in salads or as a garnish.<ref name="encyclopedia-of-spices">{{cite web |url = http://www.theepicentre.com/Spices/borage.html |title = Borage |year = 2009 |work = Encyclopedia of spices |publisher = The Epicentre |accessdate = 2010-12-01}}</ref> The flower, which contains the non-toxic pyrrolizidine alkaloid (PA) thesinine,{{Citation needed|date=December 2010}} has a sweet honey-like taste and is one of the few truly blue-colored edible substances,{{Citation needed|date=December 2010}} is often used to decorate desserts.<ref name="encyclopedia-of-spices"/> It is notable that the leaves have been found to contain small amounts (2-10 ppm of dried herb) of the liver-toxic PAs intermedine, lycopsamine, amabiline and supinine.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.wildflowerfinder.org.uk/Flowers/B/Borage/Borage.htm |title=Borage Wildflower Finder |publisher=Wildflowerfinder.org.uk |date= |accessdate=2013-09-13}}</ref> Leaves contain mainly the toxic lycopsamine also amabiline and the non-toxic saturated PA thesinine. PAs are also present in borage seed oil, but may be removed depending on method of processing.<ref name=SK>Borage at Sloan-Kettering website</ref><ref>{{cite journal | author = Dodson, Craig D.; Stermitz, Frank R. | title = Pyrrolizidine alkaloids from borage (Borago officinalis) seeds and flowers | journal = Journal of Natural Products | year = 1986 | volume = 49 | issue = 4 | pages = 727–728 | doi = 10.1021/np50046a045}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal | title = TLC detection of pyrrolizidine alkaloids in oil extracted from the seeds of Borago officinalis | author = Parvais, O.; Vander Stricht, B.; Vanhaelen-Fastre, R.; Vanhaelen, M. | journal = Journal of Planar Chromatography--Modern TLC | year = 1994 | volume = 7 | issue = 1 | pages = 80–82}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal | author = Wretensjoe, Inger; Karlberg, Bo. | title = Pyrrolizidine alkaloid content in crude and processed borage oil from different processing stages | journal = Journal of the American Oil Chemists' Society | year = 2003 | volume = 80 | issue = 10 | pages = 963–970 | doi = 10.1007/s11746-003-0804-z}}</ref><ref>{{cite book | author = Awang V. C. | title = The Information Base for safety assessment of Botanicals | editor = Eskinazi D. | work = Botanical Medicine | year = 1999}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal | author = Langer T., Franz Ch. | title = Pyrrolizidine alkaloids in commercial samples of borage seed oil products by GC-MS | journal = Scientia Pharmaceutica | year = 1997 | volume = 65 | issue = 4 | pages = 321–328}}</ref> The German Federal Institute for Risk Assessment has advised that honey from borage contains PAs, transferred to the honey through pollen collected at borage plants, and advise that commercial honey production could select for raw honey with limited PA content to prevent contamination.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.bfr.bund.de/cm/343/fragen-und-antworten-zu-pyrrolizidinalkaloiden-in-lebensmitteln.pdf |title=Fragen und Antworten zu Pyrrolizidinalkaloiden in Lebensmitteln |language=German |author=<!--Staff writer(s); no by-line.--> |date=4 August 2014 |publisher=Federal Institute for Risk Assessment |access-date=12 November 2015 |format=PDF}}</ref>

Vegetable use of borage is common in Germany, in the Spanish regions of Aragón and Navarra, in the Greek island of Crete and in the northern Italian region of Liguria. Although often used in soups, one of the better known German borage recipes is the Green Sauce (Grüne Soße) made in Frankfurt. In Italian Liguria, borage is commonly used as a filling of the traditional pasta ravioli and pansoti. It is used to flavour pickled gherkins in Poland.{{Citation needed|date=December 2008}}

Borage is also traditionally used as a garnish in the Pimms Cup cocktail,<ref name="encyclopedia-of-spices"/> but is nowadays often replaced by a long sliver of cucumber peel or by mint. It is also one of the key "Botanical" flavourings in Gilpin's Westmorland Extra Dry Gin.

Herbal medicine

Aragonese cuisine. Borage boiled and sautéed with garlic, served with potatoes.

Traditionally, Borago officinalis has been used in hyperactive gastrointestinal, respiratory and cardiovascular disorders,<ref>Gilani A.H., Bashir S., Khan A.-u. "Pharmacological basis for the use of Borago officinalis in gastrointestinal, respiratory and cardiovascular disorders", Journal of Ethnopharmacology 114 (3), pp 393–399, 2007.</ref> such as gastrointestinal (colic, cramps, diarrhea), airways (asthma, bronchitis), cardiovascular, (cardiotonic, antihypertensive and blood purifier), urinary (diuretic and kidney/bladder disorders).<ref>Gilani A.H. "Focused Conference Group: P16 - Natural products: Past and future? Pharmacological use of borago officinalis", Basic and Clinical Pharmacology and Toxicology. Conference: 16th World Congress of Basic and Clinical Pharmacology. WorldPharma 2010 Copenhagen Denmark. Publication: (var. pagings). 107 (pp 301), 2010. Date of Publication: July 2010.</ref>

Naturopathic practitioners use borage for regulation of metabolism and the hormonal system, and consider it to be a good remedy for PMS and menopause symptoms such as the hot flash.<ref>Gupta M., Singh S. Borago officinalis Linn. "An important medicinal plant of Mediterranean region: Review." International Journal of Pharmaceutical Sciences Review and Research. 5 (1) (pp 27-34), 2010.</ref> The flowers can be prepared in infusion.

One case of status epilepticus has been reported that was associated with borage oil ingestion.<ref>{{cite journal | author = Al-Khamees W.A., Schwartz M.D., Alrashdi S., Algren A.D., Morgan B.W. | title = Status Epilepticus Associated with Borage Oil Ingestion | journal = Journal of Medical Toxicology | volume = 7 | issue = 2 | pages = 154–157 | year = 2011 | doi=10.1007/s13181-011-0135-9| pmid = 21387119 | last2 = Schwartz | last3 = Alrashdi | last4 = Algren | last5 = Morgan | pmc = 3724443 }} After taking 1.5 to 3g of borage oil daily for a week; level of GLA in blood was high.</ref>

A methanol extract of borage has shown strong amoebicidal activity in vitro. The 50% inhibitory concentration ({{LD50}}) of the extract against Entamoeba histolytica was 33&nbsp;µg/mL.<ref>{{cite journal | author = Leos-Rivas C., Verde-Star M.J., Torres L.O., Oranday-Cardenas A., Rivas-Morales C., Barron-Gonzalez M.P., Morales-Vallarta M.R., Cruz-Vega D.E. | title = In vitro amoebicidal activity of borage (Borago officinalis) extract on entamoeba histolytica | journal = Journal of Medicinal Food | volume = 14 | issue = 7–8 | pages = 866–869 | year = 2011| pmid = 21476887 | last2 = Verde-Star | last3 = Torres | last4 = Oranday-Cardenas | last5 = Rivas-Morales | last6 = Barron-Gonzalez | last7 = Morales-Vallarta | last8 = Cruz-Vega | doi = 10.1089/jmf.2010.0164 }}</ref>

Companion planting

Borage is used in companion planting.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://back2theland.com/articles/14766.html |title=Gardening Borage a Companion Plant |publisher=Back2theland.com |date=2009-05-23 |accessdate=2013-09-13}}</ref> It is said to protect or nurse legumes, spinach, brassicas, and even strawberries.<ref>{{cite web|author=Long Island Adventures LLC |url=http://www.n8ture.com/herbalcompanion.html |title=Borage: Herbal Companion |website=N8ture |date= |accessdate=2013-09-13}}</ref> It is also said to be a good companion plant to tomatoes because it confuses the mother moths of tomato hornworms or manduca looking for a place to lay their eggs.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.gardenguides.com/80544-use-borage.html |title=Use Borage |publisher=Gardenguides.com |date= |accessdate=2013-09-13}}</ref> Claims that it improves tomato growth<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.ghorganics.com/page2.html |title=GH Organics |publisher=GH Organics |date= |accessdate=2013-09-13}}</ref> and makes them taste better<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.gardenguides.com/444-borage-borago-officinalis.html |title=Borage Garden Guide |publisher=Gardenguides.com |date= |accessdate=2013-09-13}}</ref> remain unsubstantiated.
