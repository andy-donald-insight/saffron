A sufganiyah ({{lang-he|סופגנייה}} or סופגניה; plural, sufganiyot: סופגניות) is a round jelly doughnut eaten in Israel and around the world on the Jewish festival of Hanukkah. The doughnut is deep-fried, filled with jelly or custard, and then topped with powdered sugar.<ref name="Roden" /> At Hanukkah, Jewish people observe the custom of eating fried foods in commemoration of the miracle associated with the Temple oil.

History

The Hebrew word sufganiyah and Arabic word sfenj derive from the words for sponge (sfog, {{lang-he|ספוג}};<ref>{{cite book |title=The New Even Shoshan Dictionary |volume= II |last= |first= |authorlink= |coauthors= |year= |publisher= |location= |isbn= |page=932 |pages= |url= |accessdate=}}</ref> isfanj, {{lang-ar|إسْفَنْج}}).

There is a long North African history besides the Jewish tradition of associating sfenj (the smaller, deep-fried donuts) with Hanukkah. In Israel, where Central and East European Jews mingled with North African Jews, the Yiddish ponchkes (similar to the German Berliner, the Polish pączki, or the Russian ponchik) became part of this tradition.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://forward.com/articles/15088/polishing-off-your-delicious-ponchkes-/|title=Polishing off Your Delicious Ponchkes |last=Philologos |date=2009-02-04 |accessdate=2012-12-09 |publisher=The Forward}}</ref>

The ponchke-style sufganiyah was originally made from two circles of dough surrounding a jelly filling, stuck together and fried in one piece.<ref name="Roden">{{cite book |title=The Book of Jewish Food: An Odyssey from Samarkand to New York |last=Roden |first=Claudia |authorlink= |coauthors= |year=1996 |publisher=Alfred A. Knopf |location=New York |isbn= |page= |pages= |url= |accessdate=}}</ref> Although this method is still practiced, an easier technique commonly used today is to deep-fry whole balls of dough, similar to the preparation of sfenj, and then inject them with a filling through a baker's syringe (or a special industrial machine). This method has resulted in the modern Sufganiyot being identical to the German Berliner.

Elegantly-styled "mini" sufganiyot

Bakeries and grocery stores build excitement for the approaching holiday by selling sufganiyot individually and by the box; they have become a favorite for school and office parties. Angel Bakeries, the largest bakery in Israel, reportedly fries up more than 250,000 sufganiyot every day during the eight-day Hanukkah festival. Each batch uses 100 kilograms of dough and makes 1,600 sufganiyot.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.food-lists.com/lists/archives/clipping-cooking/2003/12/1071940462.php |title=Sufganiyot |last=Mietkiewicz |first=Mark |date=2003-12-20 |accessdate=2010-04-06 |publisher=Food-Lists.com}}</ref> Local newspapers add to the excitement by sending out food critics each year to rate the "best sufganiyah in town."

As a result of the national hubbub, some purveyors have elevated the basic filling recipe to an art form. The least expensive version (priced at about $0.30) is filled with plain red jelly, while more expensive versions (priced at up to $2 per sufganiyah) are piped with dulce de leche, chocolate cream, vanilla cream, cappuccino,<ref name="dough"/> and even araq.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://israelity.com/2008/12/18/fancy-shmancy-donuts-from-roladin/ |title=Fancy shmancy donuts from Roladin|date=18 December 2008 |accessdate=28 November 2011 |publisher=Israelity}}</ref> In recent years, Israeli bakeries began downsizing sufganiyot to appeal to health-conscious consumers. The usual {{convert|100|g}} size, packing 400 to 600 calories (1700 to 2500 kJ),<ref name="dough">{{cite web |url=http://www.jpost.com/Home/Article.aspx?id=162844 |title=Love Me Dough |author=Minsberg, Tali and Lidman, Melanie |date=2009-12-10 |accessdate=2009-12-17 |publisher=The Jerusalem Post}}</ref> now appears in {{convert|50|g}} size with different fillings and toppings, earning the name "mini".<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.ynetnews.com/articles/0,7340,L-3335737,00.html |title=Hanukkah: Doughnuts go healthy |last=Yefet |first=Orna |date=2006-12-04 |accessdate=2010-04-06 |publisher=Ynetnews}}</ref>
