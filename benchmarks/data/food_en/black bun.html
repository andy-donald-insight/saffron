{{EngvarB|date=December 2013}} {{Use dmy dates|date=December 2013}} {{Infobox prepared food | name = Black bun | image = | imagesize = | caption = A black bun cut open, showing fruit cake interior | alternate_name = Scotch bun, Scotch Christmas bun | country = Scotland | creator = | type = Fruit cake | served = | main_ingredient = pastry, raisins, currants, almonds, citrus peel, allspice, ginger, cinnamon and pepper | variations = | calories = | other = }} Black bun is a type of fruit cake completely covered with pastry. It is Scottish in origin, originally eaten on Twelfth Night but now enjoyed at Hogmanay. The cake mixture typically contains raisins, currants, almonds, citrus peel, allspice, ginger, cinnamon and black pepper. It had originally been introduced following the return of Mary, Queen of Scots from France, but its original use at Twelfth Night ended with the Scottish Reformation. It was subsequently used for first-footing over Hogmanay.

Description

Black bun is a fruit cake wrapped in pastry. The cake itself is similar to a traditional Christmas cake or Christmas pudding mixture, including ingredients such as raisins and currants along with spices such as cinnamon, black pepper and allspice.<ref>{{cite web|last=Hollywood|first=Paul|title=Black bun|url=http://www.bbc.co.uk/food/recipes/black_bun_66755|publisher=BBC Food|accessdate=21 December 2013}}</ref><ref name=delicious>{{cite web|title=Scottish black bun recipe|url=http://www.deliciousmagazine.co.uk/recipes/scottish-black-bun|publisher=Delicious|accessdate=21 December 2013}}</ref> It has been called a much bigger version of a Garibaldi biscuit, and it has been suggested that the origin of that biscuit may have been influenced by the black bun because the inventor of the biscuit, John Carr, was Scottish.<ref>{{cite book|last=McGinn|first=Clark|title=The Ultimate Guide To Being Scottish|year=2008|publisher=Luath|location=Edinburgh|isbn=9781906307813|page=44|url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=e7JD-WoRojoC&pg=PA44&dq=%22black+bun%22&hl=en&sa=X&ei=Uai1UsARpq3tBtijgdAC&ved=0CEUQ6AEwATgU#v=onepage&q=%22black%20bun%22&f=false}}</ref>

Outside of Scotland, the black bun is also eaten in the Appalachia region of the United States.<ref>{{cite book|last=Sohn|first=Mark F.|title=Appalachian Home Cooking|year=2005|publisher=University Press of Kentucky|location=Lexington, Ky.|isbn=9780813191539|url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=pnt2W4KY94IC&pg=PT192&dq=%22black+bun%22&hl=en&sa=X&ei=eam1Uq70I7TB7Aax84GoCQ&ved=0CEAQ6AEwADge#v=onepage&q=%22black%20bun%22&f=false}}</ref> In 2013, a recipe was demonstrated by Paul Hollywood on a Christmas special of The Great British Bake Off.<ref>{{cite news|last=O'Donovan|first=Gerard|title=Great British Bake Off Christmas Special, BBC Two, review|url=http://www.telegraph.co.uk/culture/tvandradio/tv-and-radio-reviews/10520489/Great-British-Bake-Off-Christmas-Special-BBC-Two-review.html|accessdate=21 December 2013|newspaper=The Daily Telegraph|date=17 December 2013}}</ref>

Origins

The cake was originated as a Scottish King cake for use on Twelfth Night on 5 January – the eve of Epiphany, and the end of the Twelve Days of Christmas.<ref>{{cite book|last=Crump|first=William D.|title=The Christmas Encyclopedia|year=2013|publisher=McFarland & Company, Inc.|location=Jefferson, North Carolina|isbn=9780786468270|page=211|url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=MdGoAAAAQBAJ&pg=PA211&dq=%22black+bun%22+twelfth+night&hl=en&sa=X&ei=sqq1UsXFKtSS7AaQs4DgBQ&ved=0CG0Q6AEwCA#v=onepage&q=%22black%20bun%22%20twelfth%20night&f=false}}</ref> It was introduced following the return of Mary, Queen of Scots from France, and the tradition was that a bean was hidden in the cake – whoever found it became the King for the evening. It has been recorded that Mary herself participated in such games, and in 1563 she dressed her childhood companion Mary Fleming in royal robes and jewellery after Fleming became Queen for the evening. This shocked the English Ambassador, who wrote "The Queen of the Bean was that day in a gown of cloth of silver, her head, her neck, her shoulders, the rest of her whole body, so beset with stones, that more in our whole jewel house was not to be found."<ref name=hogcompanion>{{cite book|last=Douglas|first=Hugh|title=The Hogmanay Companion|year=1999|publisher=Neil Wilson|location=Glasgow|isbn=9781897784938|url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=u7ynDvIQQ1EC&pg=PT21&dq=%22black+bun%22+twelfth+night+bean&hl=en&sa=X&ei=B6y1UuzhEOes7QbQ74GYDQ&ved=0CEcQ6AEwAQ#v=onepage&q=%22black%20bun%22%20twelfth%20night%20bean&f=false}}</ref> Following the Scottish Reformation in 1560, the celebration of Christmas was outlawed in Scotland and the use of a King cake at that time ended.<ref name=hogcompanion/>

The black bun type of cake in its modern usage dates from the early nineteenth century, previously called Scotch bun and Scotch Christmas bun. The term "black bun" was first recorded in 1898, and may have been a result of Robert Louis Stevenson referring to the cake as "a black substance inimical to life".<ref>{{cite book|last=Ayto|first=John|title=The Diner's Dictionary|year=2012|publisher=Oxford University Press|location=Oxford|isbn=9780199640249|page=33|edition=2nd|url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=NoicAQAAQBAJ&pg=PA33&dq=%22black+bun%22&hl=en&sa=X&ei=i6a1UsmlM4bd7QbBh4HYAg&ved=0CEEQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&q=%22black%20bun%22&f=false}}</ref>

The cake is now commonly used as a Hogmanay custom, where people visit their neighbours after midnight to celebrate the New Year. This was called first-foot, and the gift of a black bun was meant to symbolise that the receiving family would not go hungry during the forthcoming year.<ref name=delicious/> It was also used a traditional cake to serve to those visiting homes as part of Hogmanay, to be consumed with whisky.<ref>{{cite news|last=Smith|first=Patricia|title=Hogmanay organizers promise bigger, better Scottish New Year's party|url=http://www.ottawacitizen.com/life/Hogmanay+organizers+promise+bigger+better+Scottish+Year+party/9279442/story.html|accessdate=21 December 2013|newspaper=Ottawa Citizen|date=12 December 2013}}</ref>
