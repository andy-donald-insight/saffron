Greek pizza can refer to either of two styles of pizza.<ref>http://library.thinkquest.org/06aug/02299/pizza.htm</ref&gt;

One usage refers to a pizza with typically (or stereotypically) Greek ingredients as toppings.<ref>{{cite book |title=James McNair's New Pizza: Foolproof Techniques and Fabulous Recipes |last=McNair |first=James |authorlink= |coauthors= |year=2000 |publisher=Chronicle Books |location= |isbn=0811823644 |page=43 |pages= |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=VFmVuSOK_WMC&pg=PA43&dq=Greek+style+pizza&hl=en&sa=X&ei=jmW3ULWYFYS7qAGEhIHYBQ&ved=0CDsQ6AEwAQ#v=onepage&q=Greek%20style%20pizza&f=false |accessdate=November 2012}}</ref> These include toppings such as feta cheese, olives (e.g., Kalamata), onion, tomato, green bell pepper, gyros meat<ref>Pizza menu noting: 7. Greek Isle Black olives, feta, fresh tomatoes, onions, gyro meat, light oregano, mozzarella</ref> and spinach, but often lack any tomato sauce.<ref>{{cite book | url=http://books.google.com/books?id=EQgAAAAAMBAJ&pg=PA73&dq=Greek+style+pizza&hl=en&sa=X&ei=jmW3ULWYFYS7qAGEhIHYBQ&ved=0CE0Q6AEwBw#v=onepage&q=Greek%20style%20pizza&f=false | title=Pizza! An American Pie | publisher=Vegetarian Times | date=May 1992 | accessdate=November 29, 2012 | author=Cheney, Susan Jane}}</ref> These pizzas can be found across the United States but are not as popular in Greece itself.

The other usage refers mainly more to a style of pizza crust and its preparation, rather than its toppings.<ref name="SeriousEats">{{cite book |title=Serious Eats: A Comprehensive Guide to Making & Eating Delicious Food Wherever You Are |last=Levine |first=Ed |authorlink= |coauthors= |year=2011 |publisher=Random House Digital, Inc. |location= |isbn=030772087X |page=99 |pages= |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=E8vhbo4so_0C&pg=PA99&dq=Greek+style+pizza&hl=en&sa=X&ei=jmW3ULWYFYS7qAGEhIHYBQ&ved=0CEEQ6AEwAw#v=onepage&q=Greek%20style%20pizza&f=false |accessdate=November 2012}}</ref> This style is baked in a pan, instead of directly on the bricks of the pizza oven, (as is more traditional for Italian pizza).<ref name="SeriousEats"/> The pan used is a shallow pan, in-contrast to the deep pans used in Chicago-style deep dish pizza. The pan is heavily oiled with olive oil.<ref>http://www.yummly.com/recipes/greek-style-pizza-crust</ref&gt; It has a crust that is usually chewy and puffy, almost like focaccia bread but not as thick.<ref>http://slice.seriouseats.com/archives/2011/08/the-pizza-lab-how-to-make-new-england-greek-style-pizza-at-home.html</ref&gt; The crust is also rather oily, due to the heavily oiled pan used for the cooking process.<ref name="SeriousEats"/><ref>http://www.seriouseats.com/recipes/2011/08/new-england-greek-style-pizza.html</ref&gt; Due to the use of a larger tomato paste-to-crushed tomato ratio the sauce is typical tangy and thick with a strong taste of oregano.<ref>http://chowhound.chow.com/topics/404325</ref&gt; The sauce amounts are greater, relative to the light amounts of cheese placed on the pizza. The cheese itself is typically, (but not always), a blend of mozzarella and cheddar.

In restaurants

This style of pizza may be referred to as "Greek pizza" even when it has non-Greek toppings, since it is typical of pizzerias owned by Greek immigrants. (These pizzas are similar to the pizzas served in Italian-style restaurants in Greece itself.) These establishments often also sell Greek specialties, such as Greek salads and gyros, and tend to brand themselves as "Pizza and Pasta" or as a "House of Pizza"; a code signifying that it is not an Italian restaurant, but a Greek restaurant serving Italian-style food. In the United States, the latter usage is common in New England cuisine.<ref name="SeriousEats"/>
