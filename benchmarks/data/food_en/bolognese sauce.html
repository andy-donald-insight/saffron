{{Use dmy dates|date=July 2014}} {{Infobox prepared food |name = Bolognese sauce |image = |caption = Bolognese sauce used for tagliatelle al ragù |alternate_name = |country = Bologna, Italy |region = |creator = |course = |type = Sauce |served = |main_ingredient = Ground meat (beef or veal, sometimes pork), onions, celery, carrot, tomato paste, wine |variations = |calories = |other = }} Bolognese sauce {{IPAc-en|b|ɒ|l|ə|ˈ|n|j|eɪ|z}}, known in Italian as ragù (alla) bolognese, or simply ragù, is a meat-based sauce originating from Bologna, Italy. In Italian cuisine, it is customarily used to dress "tagliatelle al ragù" and to prepare "lasagne alla bolognese". In the absence of tagliatelle, it can also be used with other broad, flat pasta shapes, such as pappardelle or fettuccine. Genuine ragù alla bolognese is a slowly cooked sauce, and its preparation involves several techniques, including sweating, sautéing and braising. Ingredients include a characteristic soffritto of onion, celery and carrot, different types of minced or finely chopped beef, often alongside small amounts of fatty pork. Red wine and a small amount of tomato concentrate or tomatoes are added, and the dish is then gently simmered at length to produce a thick sauce.

The earliest documented recipe of an Italian meat-based sauce (ragù) served with pasta comes from late 18th century Imola, near Bologna<!--Note: 18th not 19th - a century before Artusi's recipe-->. A recipe for a meat sauce for pasta that is specifically described as being "bolognese" appeared in Pellegrino Artusi's cookbook of 1891. The ragù alla bolognese that is now traditionally associated with tagliatelle and lasagne is somewhat different from Artusi's recipe. Many traditional variations currently exist. In 1982 the Italian Academy of Cuisine registered a recipe for authentic ragù alla bolognese with the Bologna Chamber of Commerce (incorporating some fresh pancetta and a little milk). In Italy, ragù alla bolognese is often referred to simply as ragù; and in Bologna, tagliatella.

Outside Italy, the phrase "Bolognese sauce" often refers to a tomato-based sauce to which mince (beef or pork) has been added; such sauces typically bear little resemblance to ragù alla bolognese. Whereas in Italy ragù is not used with spaghetti,<ref>{{cite web|title=Ragu alla Bolognese – Authentic recipe|url=http://culinariaitalia.wordpress.com/2008/06/29/ragu-alla-bolognese-authentic-recipe/|publisher=Culinaria Italia|accessdate=15 February 2013}}</ref> so-called spaghetti bolognese has become a popular dish in many other parts of the world.

Origin and history

The earliest documented recipe for a meat-based sauce (ragù) served with pasta comes from late 18th century Imola, near Bologna.<!--Note: 18th not 19th - a century before Artusi's recipe--><ref name=Kasper>Kasper, Lynne Rossetto, The Splendid Table, Morrow, ISBN 0-688-08963-1</ref> Pellegrino Artusi published a recipe for a meat sauce characterized as being bolognese in his cookbook published in 1891.<ref name=Artusi>Artusi, Pellegrino, Science in the Kitchen and the Art of Eating Well (English translation), recipe 87, University of Toronto Press, ISBN 0-8020-8704-3</ref> Artusi's recipe, which he called Maccheroni alla bolognese, is thought to derive from the mid 19th century when he spent considerable time in Bologna (maccheroni being a generic term for pasta, both dried and fresh<ref name="DeVita2009">{{cite book|last=De Vita|first=Oretta Zanini|title=Encyclopedia of Pasta|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=D5nXAbSifIgC|accessdate=17 February 2013|date=15 September 2009|publisher=University of California Press|isbn=978-0-520-94471-8|page=162|others=Translated by Fant, Maureen B}}</ref>). The recipe only partially resembles the ragù alla bolognese that is traditionally associated with tagliatelle.<ref name="CVCI-dictionary">{{cite web|title=Tagliatelle al ragù alla Bolognese: the dictionary|url=http://itchefs-gvci.com/?option=com_content&view=article&id=461&Itemid=904|publisher=GVCI - Gruppo virtuale cuochi italiani|accessdate=17 February 2013|author=Scarpato, Rosario|year=2010}}</ref> The sauce called for predominantly lean veal filet along with pancetta, butter, onion, and carrot. The meats and vegetables were to be finely minced, cooked with butter until the meats browned, then covered and cooked with broth. Artusi commented that the taste could be made even more pleasant by adding small pieces of dried mushroom, a few slices of truffle, or chicken liver cooked with the meat and diced. As a final touch, he also suggested adding half a glass of cream to the sauce when it was completely done to make it taste even smoother. Artusi recommended serving this sauce with a medium size pasta ("horse teeth") made from durum wheat. The pasta was to be made fresh, cooked until it was firm, and then flavored with the sauce and Parmigiano cheese.<ref name="Artusi" />

Evolution and variations

Tagliatelle al ragu Bolognese as served in Bologna.

In the century-plus since Artusi recorded and subsequently published his recipe for Maccheroni alla bolognese, what is now ragù alla bolognese has evolved with the cuisine of the region. Most notable is the preferred choice of pasta, which today is widely recognized as fresh tagliatelle. Another reflection of the evolution of the cuisine over the past 150 years is the addition of tomato, either as a puree or as a concentrated paste, to the common mix of ingredients. Similarly, both wine and milk appear today in the list of ingredients in many of the contemporary recipes, and beef has mostly displaced veal as the dominant meat.

In 1982, the Italian Academy of Cuisine (Accademia Italiana della Cucina), an organization dedicated to preserving the culinary heritage of Italy, recorded and deposited a recipe for "classic Bolognese ragù" with the Bologna Chamber of Commerce (La Camera di Commercio di Bologna).<ref>{{cite web|author=Gruppo Virtuale Cuochi Italiani|title=The Classic Bolognese Ragù according the Accademia Italiana della Cucina |url=http://www.itchefs-gvci.com/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=587&Itemid=976|publisher=itchefs|accessdate=14 November 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|author=Gruppo Virtuale Cuochi Italiani|title=Il classico Ragù alla Bolognese secondo l’Accademia Italiana della Cucina|url=http://www.gvci.org/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=572&Itemid=975|publisher=itchefs|accessdate=14 November 2012|language=Italian}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=Come fare le Tagliatelle con ragù alla bolognese |url=http://www.newsfood.com/q/44376a8e/tagliatelle-con-rag-alla-bolognese-parte-1-il-rag/ |publisher=Newsfood.com |accessdate=14 November 2012 |author=Carlo Alberto Tozzola |author2=Bologna Cooking School |language=Italian}}</ref> A version of the academy's recipe for American kitchens was also published.<ref name=Kasper /> The academy's recipe confines the ingredients to beef cut from the plate section (cartella di manzo), fresh unsmoked pancetta (pancetta di maiale distesa), onions, carrot, celery, passata (or tomato purée), meat broth, dry wine (red or white, not sparkling), milk, salt and pepper. The option of adding a small amount of cream at the end of the preparation is recommended.

Nowadays, there are many variations of the recipe even among native Italian chefs,<ref name=Marcella>Hazan, Marcella Essentials of Classic Italian Cooking, Knopf, ISBN 0-394-58404-X</ref><ref>http://www.itchefs-gvci.com/?option=com_content&view=article&id=457&Itemid=900, retrieved 2 March 2012</ref><ref name=Caramella>http://www.itchefs-gvci.com/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=585&Itemid=975, retrieved 2 March 2012</ref><ref name=Clark>http://www.internationalcookingschool.com/recipes-la-cucina-ragu-bolognese.php, retrieved 2 March 2012</ref> and the repertoire has been further broadened by some American chefs known for their expertise in Italian cuisine.<ref>Bertolli, Paul, Cooking by Hand, Potter, ISBN 0-609-60893-2</ref><ref>http://www.foodnetwork.com/recipes/mario-batali/tagliatelle-al-ragu-recipe/index.html , retrieved 2 March 2012</ref>

Ragù alla bolognese is a complex sauce which involves various cooking techniques, including sweating, sautéing and braising. As such, it lends itself well to interpretation and adaptation by professional chefs and home cooks alike. Common sources of differences include which meats to use (beef, pork or veal) and their relative quantities, the possible inclusion of either cured meats or offal, which fats are used in the sauté phases (rendered pork fat, butter, olive or vegetable oil), what form of tomato is employed (fresh, canned or paste), the makeup of the cooking liquids (wine, milk, tomato juices, or broth) and their specific sequence of addition.

The numerous variations among recipes for ragù alla bolognese have led many to search for the definitive, authentic recipe.<ref name=Cloake>{{cite web| last=Cloake |first=Felicity |title=How to make perfect bolognese |url=http://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/wordofmouth/2010/nov/25/how-to-make-perfect-bolognese |work=The Guardian |date=25 November 2010 |accessdate=2 March 2012}}</ref> Some have suggested the recipe registered by the Accademia Italiana della Cucina in 1982 as the "most authentic".<ref>http://www.itchefs-gvci.com/?option=com_content&view=article&id=461&Itemid=904 ; retrieved 2 March 2012</ref> However, this would be inconsistent with the academy's own beliefs and statements about remaining faithful to tradition in documenting and preserving Italy's culinary heritage.<ref>http://www.accademiaitalianacucina.it/en_index.php ; retrieved 2 March 2012</ref><ref>Accademia Italian dell Cucina, La Cucina – The Regional Cooking of Italy, Rizzoli, 2009, ISBN 978—0-8478-3147-0 (English translation of La Cucina del Bel Paese)</ref> Prominent Italian chef Mario Caramella stated, "In Italy, there are several traditional recipes of tagliatelle al ragù alla bolognese with more or less slight variations".<ref name=Caramella /> The noted chef, culinary teacher, food writer, and authority on the cuisine of Bologna, Mary Beth Clark, claims "There are as many versions of Bolognese ragù as there are versions of tomato sauce and pizza!"<ref name=Clark /> According to UK cookbook author and food writer Felicity Cloake, "The fact is that there is no definitive recipe for a bolognese meat sauce, but to be worthy of the name, it should respect the traditions of the area",<ref name=Cloake /> a view that is consistent with that often expressed by the Italian Academy of Cuisine.

The many variations tend to be based on a common theme. For instance, garlic is absent from all of the recipes referenced above, as are herbs other than a parsimonious use of bay leaves by some. Seasoning is limited to salt, pepper and the occasional pinch of nutmeg. In all of the recipes meats dominate as the principal ingredient, while tomatoes, in one form or another, are only an auxiliary ingredient.

Traditional service and use

In Bologna ragù alla bolognese is customarily paired and served with tagliatelle, made with eggs and northern Italy’s soft wheat flour. Acceptable alternatives to fresh tagliatelle include other broad flat pasta shapes, such as pappardelle or fettuccine, and tube shapes, such as rigatoni and penne.<ref>Hazan, Giuliano, The Classic Pasta Cookbook, Dorling Kindersley, ISBN 978-1-56458-292-8</ref>

Ragù alla bolognese along with béchamel is also used by many Italian chefs to prepare traditional baked lasagna in Bolognese style.<ref name=Marcella />

International Day of Italian Cuisines (IDIC) 2010

Gruppo Virtuale Cuochi Italiani (GVCI), an international organization and network of culinary professionals dedicated to authentic Italian cuisine, annually organizes and promotes an "International Day of Italian Cuisines" (IDIC). In 2010 tagliatelle al ragu alla bolognese was the official dish for IDIC.<ref>http://www.itchefs-gvci.com/index.php?option=com_content&view=category&layout=blog&id=142&Itemid=1130, retrieved 2 March 2012</ref> The event, held on 17 January 2010, included participation by 450 professional chefs in 50 countries who prepared the signature dish according to "an authentic" recipe provided by chef Mario Caramella.<ref name=Caramella /> Media coverage was broad internationally,<ref>http://www.itchefs-gvci.com/index.php?option=com_content&view=category&layout=blog&id=150&Itemid=951, retrieved 2 March 2012</ref> but reports often incorrectly identified the recipe followed as that of l'Accademia Italiana della Cucina, and some included stock photographs of spaghetti Bolognese.<ref name=Cloake /><ref>{{cite news |last=Squires |first=Nick |title=Spaghetti bolognese recipe: Italian chefs show world the correct way |url=http://www.telegraph.co.uk/foodanddrink/foodanddrinknews/7017565/Spaghetti-bolognese-recipe-Italian-chefs-show-world-the-correct-way.html |work=The Daily Telegraph |location=London |date=18 January 2010 |accessdate=2 March 2012}}</ref>

Spaghetti bolognese

<!-- linked from redirects spaghetti bolognese, spaghetti alla bolognese, Spaghetti alla Bolognese and Spag Bol -->

Spaghetti bolognese with thyme and basil

Spaghetti bolognese (also known as spaghetti alla bolognese, and colloquially in Commonwealth countries as spag bol) is a pasta dish found outside of Italy, consisting in spaghetti served with a ground meat sauce. Spaghetti are never served in Bologna<ref>{{cite web|last1=Pizzimenti|first1=Chiara|title=Spaghetti bolognese a chi?|url=http://www.lacucinaitaliana.it/news/in-primo-piano/spaghetti-bolognese-a-chi/|website=www.lacucinaitaliana.it|publisher=La Cucina Italiana|accessdate=11 November 2015|language=Italian}}</ref> or anywhere else in Italy{{Dubious|date=November 2015}} with a meat-based sauce (ragù), since this kind of pasta, having a smooth surface, is not able to absorb the meat-based sauce.

The dish is very popular in France, the Benelux, Iran, Libya, Brazil, Portugal, Germany, Austria, Hungary, Sweden, Finland, Denmark and Norway. It is called {{lang|sv|spaghetti med köttfärssås}} in Swedish; {{lang|fi|spagetti ja jauhelihakastike}} in Finnish; {{lang|dk|spaghetti med kødsovs}} in Danish;<ref>{{cite web |url = http://www.food.dtu.dk/Nyheder/Ern%C3%A6ring.aspx?guid=%7B948B4EE7-1FF0-45C6-8E3C-8D1A43C07CA0%7D |title = dtu.dk, "Danskernes måltider: Kylling og kødsovs på middagsbordet". 18 January 2012 |work = http://www.food.dtu.dk }}</ref> {{lang|no|spagetti med kjøttdeig}} in Norwegian; {{lang|hu|bolognai spagetti}} in Hungarian; {{lang|pt|esparguete à bolonhesa}} in Portuguese; or simply bolognese. Most of these dishes have ground beef or pork and grated cheese as the main ingredient, other ingredients can vary or even be omitted.

A version of this dish is popular in the United Kingdom, New Zealand and Australia where it is often referred to as spag bol or spag bog.<ref>{{cite book |title = The Concise New Partridge Dictionary of Slang and Uncoventional English |publisher = Routledge |year = 2008 |author = Eric Partridge, Tom Dalzell, Terry Victor }}</ref> In the United States, the dish is often called simply "spaghetti", or "spaghetti and meat sauce"; the sauce (a tomato-and-ground-beef or pork) is typically called "spaghetti sauce",<ref>{{cite web |url = http://www.bhg.com/recipe/pasta/spaghetti-sauce/ |title = Spaghetti Sauce |work = Better Homes & Gardens }}</ref> and the term "bolognese" is rarely applied. In Iran, this dish is simply referred to as "macaroni" and usually contains Turmeric as the main seasoning of the ground beef, as well as diced, skin-less potatoes in the spaghetti sauce. Many Iranians make Tahdig out of the bottom of the spaghetti as it is simmering.
