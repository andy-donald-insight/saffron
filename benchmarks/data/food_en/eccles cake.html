Name and origin

Eccles cakes are named after the English town of Eccles. It is not known who invented the recipe, but James Birch is credited with being the first person to sell Eccles cakes on a commercial basis, which he sold from his shop at the corner of Vicarage Road and St Mary's Road (now known as Church Street) in the town centre, in 1793.<ref>{{cite web | title = The history behind (and recipe for) Eccles Cakes | publisher = Salford City Council | url = http://www.salford.gov.uk/living/yourcom/salfordlife/aboutsalford/salfordlocalhistory/localhistory-eccles/ecclescakes.htm | accessdate = 2007-04-10}}</ref>

Eccles cakes do not have Protected Geographical Status, so may be manufactured anywhere and still labelled as "Eccles" cakes.<ref>{{cite news |title=Cumberland sausage wins protection |first=Lewis |last=Smith |url=http://www.independent.co.uk/life-style/food-and-drink/news/cumberland-sausage-wins-protection-2245348.html |newspaper=The Independent |date=18 March 2011 |accessdate=6 July 2011 |location=London}}</ref>

Similar pastries

A Chorley cake (left) and an Eccles cake (right)

The Chorley cake (from Chorley in Lancashire) is flatter, made with shortcrust pastry rather than flaky pastry and is devoid of sugar topping.<ref>{{cite web |title=Chorley Cakes and Lancashire Cheese|url=http://www.visitlancashire.com/downloads/Chorley%20Cakes%20and%20Lancashrie%20Cheese.pdf |website=Visit Lancashire|accessdate=16 April 2015}} <!--See also Lancashire Recipes: Chorley Cakesparent web-page--></ref>

The Blackburn cake is similarly named for the city of Blackburn, using stewed apples in place of currants.<ref>{{cite book |last=Wilson |first=Sean |title=the Great Northern Cookbook |year=2012 |publisher=Hodder & Stoughton |isbn=978-1-4447-6113-9}}</ref>

The Banbury cake is an oval cake from Banbury.<ref>{{cite book |last=Little |first=Brian |title=Banbury: A History |year=2003 |publisher=Phillimore & Co |isbn=1-86077-242-0 |page=27}}</ref>

Fire hazard

UK fire brigades have reported fires started by Eccles cakes overcooked in microwave ovens. The sugar contained in the cakes is alleged to be flammable.<ref>{{cite news |title=Fire brigade issue warning after rise in kitchen blazes caused by overheating Eccles Cakes |url=http://www.dailymail.co.uk/news/article-2329640/Rise-house-fires-caused-overheating-Eccles-cakes-microwave.html|newspaper=The Daily Mail |first=Sam |last=Webb |date=23 May 2013}}</ref>
