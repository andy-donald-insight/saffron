Brown bread is a designation often given to breads made with significant amounts of whole grain flour, usually wheat, and sometimes dark-colored ingredients such as molasses or coffee. In Canada and the United Kingdom it simply refers to wholemeal or whole wheat bread, except in the Maritimes, where it implies bread made with molasses.

Whole wheat flours that contain raw wheat germ, instead of toasted germ, have higher levels of glutathione, and thus are said to result in lower loaf volumes.<ref>{{cite book |author=Paula I. Figoni |title=How Baking Works: Exploring the Fundamentals of Baking Science |publisher=Wiley |location=New York |year=2010 |pages=147–150 |isbn=0-470-39267-3 |accessdate=April 17, 2011 |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=XqKF7PqV02cC&pg=PA147#v=onepage&q&f=false |quote=Glutathione is also found in whole wheat flour, in particular in the wheat germ. ... Toasted wheat germ will not have the same high glutathione activity as raw wheat germ, since glutathione is inactivated by heat. ... If glutathione is not first destroyed, bread dough softens and becomes slack, and oven spring decreases. The result is lower loaf volume and coarser texture.}}</ref>

History

Crust of a brown bread

In Ireland, during the Famine, prior to 1848, brown bread was handed out to the poor.<ref>{{cite book |editor=John Saunders |title=The People's journal |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=s1QAAAAAYAAJ&pg=PA200#v=onepage&q&f=false |volume=IV |year=1848 |publisher=The People's Journal Office |location=London |page=200 |accessdate=April 15, 2011 |quote=I passed along and beheld a dense mass of wretched starving people,---men, women, and children, gathered in front of a shed, from which rations of brown bread and stirabout were served out to the poor.}}</ref> In England, brown bread was made from brown meal.<ref name="percy.p14.processes.flour.manufacture">{{cite book |author=Percy A. Amos |title=Processes of flour manufacture |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=KXLzrbmw8u0C&pg=PA14#v=onepage&q&f=false |year=1912 |publisher=Longman, Green, and Co. |location=New York, Bombay, and Calcutta |page=14 |accessdate=April 12, 2011 |quote=By allowing the germ and all but the outer, coarser layers of broad bran to mix in with the flour, we get the sweet-tasting brown meal producing the brown bread so much in favour amongst sections of the community.}}</ref> Around and prior to the year 1845, brown meal was considered a less desirable grain product, and was priced accordingly. However, by 1865, due to recently discovered health benefits of bran, brown meal's London price had increased to a point often greater than that of fine flour.<ref>{{cite book |author=Edward Smith |title=Practical dietary for families, schools, and the laboring classes |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=BuhSZOIY4G4C&pg=PA36#v=onepage&q&f=false |year=1865 |publisher=Walton and Maberly |location=London |page=36 |accessdate=April 15, 2011 |quote=}}</ref>

Colour

The brown color of whole grain breads is caused by cerealine, a discovery attributed to M. Mège Mouriès of France.<ref name="youmans.pg277.handbook.household.science" /><ref name="watt.the.chemist.monthly.journal.pgs488.539">{{cite book |editor=John and Charles Watt |volume=IV |title=The Chemist: a monthly journal of Chemical and Physical Science |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=QhM4AAAAMAAJ&pg=PA488#v=onepage&q&f=false|year=1857 |publisher=Alexander Watt |location=London |pages=488–539 |quote=... cerealine ... }}</ref> Cerealine, considered by Mouriès an active principal or ferment similar in action to diastase,<ref name="watt.the.chemist.monthly.journal.pgs488.539" /> came from the cereal layer of rectangular cells that millers considered a part of bran: later it was alternatively called the aleurone layer.<ref>{{cite book |author=Helen Woodard Atwater |title=Bread and the principles of bread making |url=http://books.google.com/books?&id=eGHVTs6VSEcC&oi=fnd&pg=PA9#v=onepage&q&f=false|year=1900 |publisher=Government Printing Office |location=Washington |page=9 |quote=}}</ref> In a statement attributed to Mouriès, if the cerealine is neutralized, white bread can be made from bran-containing flour.<ref name="youmans.pg277.handbook.household.science" />

Boston brown bread

New England or Boston brown bread is a type of dark, slightly sweet steamed bread (usually a quick bread) popular in New England. It is cooked by steam in a can, or cylindrical pan.

Boston brown bread's colour comes from a mixture of flours, usually a mix of several of the following: cornmeal, rye, whole wheat, graham flour, and from the addition of sweeteners like molasses and maple syrup. Leavening most often comes from baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) though a few recipes use yeast. Raisins are often added. The batter is poured into a can, and steamed in a kettle. While most variations are quick breads, and can be made in less than an hour, several commercial brands are available. Brown bread is somewhat seasonal, being served mostly in fall and winter, and is frequently served with baked beans.

Brown bread is closely related to an earlier bread known as "Rye n Injun" (from "Indian" cornmeal) or "thirded" bread from its use of rye, cornmeal and wheat flours. Unlike modern Boston brown bread, thirded bread is generally yeast-raised and baked rather than steamed.
