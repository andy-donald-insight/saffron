Glycyrrhiza glabra - MHNT

Liquorice, or licorice, ({{IPAc-en|ˈ|l|ɪ|k|ᵊ|r|ɪ|ʃ}} {{respell|LIK|(ə-)rish}} or {{IPAc-en|ˈ|l|ɪ|k|ᵊ|r|ɪ|s}} {{respell|LIK|(ə-)ris)}}<ref>licorice. Merriam-Webster's Medical Dictionary, © 2007 Merriam-Webster, Inc.</ref> is the root of Glycyrrhiza glabra from which a sweet flavour can be extracted. The liquorice plant is a herbaceous perennial legume native to southern Europe, India, and parts of Asia. It is not botanically related to anise, star anise, or fennel, which are sources of similar flavouring compounds.

Most liquorice is used as a flavouring agent for tobacco, particularly American blend cigarettes, to which liquorice lends a natural sweetness and a distinctive flavour and makes it easier to inhale the smoke by creating bronchodilators, which open up the lungs.<ref name=Cook/><ref name="Boeken v. Phillip Morris Inc 1640">Boeken v. Phillip Morris Inc., 127 Cal. App. 4th 1640, 1673, 26 Cal. Rptr. 3d 638, 664 (2005).</ref> Liquorice flavours are also used as candies or sweeteners, particularly in some European and Middle Eastern countries. Liquorice extracts have a number of medical uses, and they are also used in herbal and folk medications. Excessive consumption of liquorice (more than 2&nbsp;mg/kg/day of pure glycyrrhizinic acid, a liquorice component) may result in adverse effects, and overconsumption should be suspected clinically in patients presenting with otherwise unexplained hypokalemia and muscle weakness.<ref name="SAGE Publications">{{cite web|last1=Omar|first1=Hesham R|last2=Komarova,|first2=Irina|last3=El-Ghonemi,|first3=Mohamed|last4=Ahmed|first4=Fathy|last5=Rashad|first5=Rania|last6=Abdelmalak|first6=Hany D|last7=Yerramadha|first7=Muralidhar Reddy|last8=Ali|first8=Yaseen|last9=Camporesi|first9=Enrico M|title=How much is too much? in Licorice abuse: time to send a warning message from Therapeutic Advances in Endocrinology and Metabolism|url=http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3498851/|website=http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov|publisher=SAGE Publications|accessdate=13 January 2015}}</ref>

Etymology

The word liquorice is derived (via the Old French licoresse) from the Greek γλυκύρριζα (glukurrhiza), meaning "sweet root",<ref>γλυκύρριζα, Henry George Liddell, Robert Scott, A Greek-English Lexicon, on Perseus</ref> from γλυκύς (glukus), "sweet"<ref>γλυκύς, Henry George Liddell, Robert Scott, A Greek-English Lexicon, on Perseus</ref> + ῥίζα (rhiza), "root",<ref>ῥίζα, Henry George Liddell, Robert Scott, A Greek-English Lexicon, on Perseus<</ref><ref>liquorice, on Oxford Dictionaries</ref> the name provided by Dioscorides.<ref>google books Maud Grieve, Manya Marshall - A modern herbal: the medicinal, culinary, cosmetic and economic properties, cultivation and folk-lore of herbs, grasses, fungi, shrubs, & trees with all their modern scientific uses, Volume 2 Dover Publications, 1982 & Pharmacist's Guide to Medicinal Herbs Arthur M. Presser Smart Publications, 1 April 2001 2012-05-19</ref> It is usually spelled liquorice in British usage, but licorice in the United States and Canada. It is called erk-soos (عرق سوس) in Arabic, athimadhuram (అతిమధురం) in Telugu, jyeshthamadhu (ಜ್ಯೇಷ್ಠಮಧು) in Kannada, ملیٹھی in Urdu, athimadhuram (அதிமதுரம்) in Tamil, irattimadhuram (ഇരട്ടിമധുരം) in Malayalam, yastimadhu (यष्टिमधु) in Sanskrit, mulethi (मुलेठी) in Hindi, Vel Mee (වැල් මී) in Sinhalese, jethimadh (જેઠીમધ) in Gujarati, and jyeshthamadh (ज्येष्ठमध) in Marathi.

Description

It is a herbaceous perennial, growing to 1 m in height, with pinnate leaves about {{convert|7|–|15|cm|in|abbr=on}} long, with 9–17 leaflets. The flowers are {{convert|0.8|–|1.2|cm|in|frac=12|abbr=on}} long, purple to pale whitish blue, produced in a loose inflorescence. The fruit is an oblong pod, {{convert|2|-|3|cm|in|frac=12|abbr=on}} long, containing several seeds.<ref name=rhs>Huxley, A., ed. (1992). New RHS Dictionary of Gardening. ISBN 0-333-47494-5</ref> The roots are stoloniferous.<ref>Brown, D., ed. (1995). "The RHS encyclopedia of herbs and their uses". ISBN 1-4053-0059-0</ref>

Chemistry

The scent of liquorice root comes from a complex and variable combination of compounds, of which anethole is up to 3% of total volatiles. Much of the sweetness in liquorice comes from glycyrrhizin, which has a sweet taste, 30–50 times the sweetness of sugar. The sweetness is very different from sugar, being less instant, tart, and lasting longer.

The isoflavene glabrene and the isoflavane glabridin, found in the roots of liquorice, are phytoestrogens.<ref>{{Cite journal | last1 = Somjen | first1 = D. | last2 = Katzburg | first2 = S. | last3 = Vaya | first3 = J. | last4 = Kaye | first4 = A. Cultivation and uses

Liquorice, which grows best in well-drained soils in deep valleys with full sun, is harvested in the autumn two to three years after planting.<ref name=rhs/> Countries producing liquorice include Iran, Afghanistan, the People’s Republic of China, Pakistan, Iraq, Azerbaijan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, and Turkey.<ref name=Mafco2010>M & F Worldwide Corp., Annual Report on Form 10-K for the Year Ended December 31, 2010.</ref>

The world's leading manufacturer of liquorice products is M&F Worldwide, which manufactures more than 70% of the worldwide liquorice flavours sold to end users.<ref>M & F Worldwide Corp., Annual Report on Form 10-K for the Year Ended December 31, 2001.</ref>

Tobacco

Most liquorice is used as a flavouring agent for tobacco. For example, M&F Worldwide reported in 2011 that about 63% of its liquorice product sales are to the worldwide tobacco industry for use as tobacco flavour enhancing and moistening agents in the manufacture of American blend cigarettes, moist snuff, chewing tobacco, and pipe tobacco.<ref name=Mafco2010/> American blend cigarettes made up a larger portion of worldwide tobacco consumption in earlier years,<ref>Erik Assadourian, Cigarette Production Drops, Vital Signs 2005, at 70.</ref> and the percentage of liquorice products used by the tobacco industry was higher in the past. M&F Worldwide sold approximately 73% of its liquorice products to the tobacco industry in 2005.<ref>M & F Worldwide Corp., Annual Report on Form 10-K for the Year Ended December 31, 2005.</ref> A consultant to M&F Worldwide's predecessor company stated in 1975 that it was believed that well over 90% of the total production of liquorice extract and its derivatives found its way into tobacco products.<ref name=Cook>Marvin K. Cook, The Use of Licorice and Other Flavoring Material in Tobacco (April 10, 1975).</ref>

Liquorice provides tobacco products with a natural sweetness and a distinctive flavour that blends readily with the natural and imitation flavouring components employed in the tobacco industry. It represses harshness and is not detectable as liquorice by the consumer.<ref name=Cook/> Tobacco flavourings such as liquorice also make it easier to inhale the smoke by creating bronchodilators, which open up the lungs.<ref name="Boeken v. Phillip Morris Inc 1640"/> Chewing tobacco requires substantially higher levels of liquorice extract as emphasis on the sweet flavour appears highly desirable.<ref name=Cook/>

Food and candy

{{See also|Liquorice (confectionery)}} Liquorice flavour is found in a wide variety of candies or sweets. In most of these candies, the taste is reinforced by aniseed oil so the actual content of liquorice is very low. Liquorice confections are primarily purchased by consumers in the European Union.<ref name=Mafco2010/>

In the Netherlands, where liquorice candy (drop) is one of the most popular forms of sweets, only a few of the many forms that are sold contain aniseed, although mixing it with mint, menthol, or with laurel is quite popular. Mixing it with ammonium chloride (salmiak) is also popular. The most popular liquorice, known in the Netherlands as zoute drop (salty liquorice), actually contains very little salt, i.e., sodium chloride.<ref>[1] the online Dutch food composition database]</ref> The salty taste is probably due to ammonium chloride and the blood pressure-raising effect is due to glycyrrhizin. Strong, salty sweets are popular in Nordic countries.

Pontefract in Yorkshire was the first place where liquorice mixed with sugar began to be used as a sweet in the same way it is in the modern day.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.aboutfood.co.uk/places/yorkshire_guide.html|publisher=AboutFood.com|title= Right good food from the Ridings|date=25 October 2007}}</ref> Pontefract cakes were originally made there. In County Durham, Yorkshire, and Lancashire, it is colloquially known as 'Spanish', supposedly because Spanish monks grew liquorice root at Rievaulx Abbey near Thirsk.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.thenorthernecho.co.uk/news/918508.where_liquorice_roots_go_deep/|title=Where Liquorice Roots Go Deep|publisher=Northern Echo|accessdate=9 December 2008}}</ref>

Liquorice root chips

Dried sticks of liquorice root

In Italy (particularly in the south), Spain, and France, liquorice is popular in its natural form. The root of the plant is simply dug up, washed, dried, and chewed as a mouth freshener. Throughout Italy, unsweetened liquorice is consumed in the form of small black pieces made only from 100% pure liquorice extract; the taste is bitter and intense. In Calabria a popular liqueur is made from pure liquorice extract.

Liquorice is also very popular in Syria and Egypt, where it is sold as a drink, in shops as well as street vendors. It is used for its expectorant qualities in folk medicine in Egypt.

Dried liquorice root can be chewed as a sweet. Black liquorice contains about 100 calories{{dubious|date=July 2015}} per ounce (15&nbsp;kJ/g).

Liquorice is used by brewers to flavour and colour porter classes of beers, and the enzymes in the root also stabilize the foam heads produced by beers brewed with it.<ref>Licorice - HowStuffWorks<!-- Bot generated title --></ref>

Medicine

{{See also|Glycyrrhizin}} {{See also|Enoxolone}} Glycyrrhizin has also demonstrated antiviral, antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, hepatoprotective, and blood pressure-increasing effects in vitro and in vivo, as is supported by the finding that intravenous glycyrrhizin (as if it is given orally very little of the original drug makes it into circulation) slows the progression of viral and autoimmune hepatitis.<ref>{{cite journal|last=Chien|first=CF|author2=Wu, YT |author3=Tsai, TH |title=Biological analysis of herbal medicines used for the treatment of liver diseases.|journal=Biomedical Chromatography|date=January 2011|volume=25|issue=1-2|pages=21–38|doi=10.1002/bmc.1568|pmid=21204110}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|last=Yasui|first=S|author2=Fujiwara, K |author3=Tawada, A |author4=Fukuda, Y |author5=Nakano, M |author6= Yokosuka, O |title=Efficacy of intravenous glycyrrhizin in the early stage of acute onset autoimmune hepatitis.|journal=Digestive Diseases and Sciences|date=December 2011|volume=56|issue=12|pages=3638–47|doi=10.1007/s10620-011-1789-5|pmid=21681505}}</ref> In one clinical trial liquorice demonstrated promising activity, when applied topically, against atopic dermatitis.<ref>{{cite journal|last=Reuter|first=J|author2=Merfort, I |author3=Schempp, CM |title=Botanicals in dermatology: an evidence-based review.|journal=American Journal of Clinical Dermatology|date=2010|volume=11|issue=4|pages=247–67|doi=10.2165/11533220-000000000-00000|pmid=20509719}}</ref> Additionally, liquorice may be effective in treating hyperlipidaemia (a high amount of fats in the blood).<ref>{{cite journal|last=Hasani-Ranjbar|first=S|author2=Nayebi, N |author3=Moradi, L |author4=Mehri, A |author5=Larijani, B |author6= Abdollahi, M |title=The efficacy and safety of herbal medicines used in the treatment of hyperlipidemia; a systematic review.|journal=Current pharmaceutical design|date=2010|volume=16|issue=26|pages=2935–47|pmid=20858178 |doi=10.2174/138161210793176464}}</ref> Liquorice has also demonstrated efficacy in treating inflammation-induced skin hyperpigmentation.<ref>{{cite journal|last=Callender|first=VD|coauthors=St Surin-Lord, S; Davis, EC; Maclin, M|title=Postinflammatory hyperpigmentation: etiologic and therapeutic considerations.|journal=American Journal of Clinical Dermatology|date=April 2011|volume=12|issue=2|pages=87–99|doi=10.2165/11536930-000000000-00000|pmid=21348540}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|last=Leyden|first=JJ|author2=Shergill, B |author3=Micali, G |author4=Downie, J |author5= Wallo, W |title=Natural options for the management of hyperpigmentation.|journal=Journal of the European Academy of Dermatology and Venereology|date=October 2011|volume=25|issue=10|pages=1140–5|doi=10.1111/j.1468-3083.2011.04130.x|pmid=21623927}}</ref> Liquorice may also be useful in preventing neurodegenerative disorders and dental caries.<ref>{{cite journal|last=Kannappan|first=R|author2=Gupta, SC |author3=Kim, JH |author4=Reuter, S |author5= Aggarwal, BB |title=Neuroprotection by spice-derived nutraceuticals: you are what you eat!|journal=Molecular Neurobiology|date=October 2011|volume=44|issue=2|pages=142–59|doi=10.1007/s12035-011-8168-2|pmid=21360003|url=http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3183139/pdf/nihms307525.pdf|format=PDF|pmc=3183139}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|last=Gazzani|first=G|author2=Daglia, M |author3=Papetti, A |title=Food components with anticaries activity.|journal=Current Opinion in Biotechnology|date=April 2012|volume=23|issue=2|pages=153–9|doi=10.1016/j.copbio.2011.09.003|pmid=22030309}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|last=Messier|first=C|author2=Epifano, F |author3=Genovese, S |author4= Grenier, D |title=Licorice and its potential beneficial effects in common oro-dental diseases.|journal=Oral Diseases|date=January 2012|volume=18|issue=1|pages=32–9|doi=10.1111/j.1601-0825.2011.01842.x|pmid=21851508}}</ref>

The antiulcer, laxative, antidiabetic, anti-inflammatory, immunomodulatory, antitumour and expectorant properties of liquorice have been investigated.<ref>{{cite journal|last=Shibata|first=S|title=A drug over the millennia: pharmacognosy, chemistry, and pharmacology of licorice.|journal=Yakugaku Zasshi|date=October 2000|volume=120|issue=10|pages=849–62|pmid=11082698}}</ref>

The compound glycyrrhizin (or glycyrrhizic acid), found in liquorice, has been proposed as being useful for liver protection in tuberculosis therapy, but evidence does not support this use, which may in fact be harmful.<ref name=Liu2008>{{cite journal |author=Liu Q, Garner P, Wang Y, Huang B, Smith H |title=Drugs and herbs given to prevent hepatotoxicity of tuberculosis therapy: systematic review of ingredients and evaluation studies |journal=BMC Public Health |volume=8 |issue= |page=365 |year=2008 |pmid=18939987 |pmc=2576232 |doi=10.1186/1471-2458-8-365 |type=Systematic review}}</ref>

Folk medicine

In traditional Chinese medicine, liquorice (甘草) is believed to "harmonize" the ingredients in a formula and to carry the formula to the 12 "regular meridians".<ref>{{cite book | last = Bensky | first = Dan | title = Chinese Herbal Medicine: Materia Medica, Third Edition |publisher = Eastland Press |year = 2004| isbn= 0-939616-42-4|display-authors=etal}}</ref> Liquorice has been traditionally known and used as medicine in Ayurveda for rejuvenation.<ref>{{Cite book|title = Ayurveda: Its Principles & Philosophies|last = Balakrishna|first = Acharya|publisher = Divya prakashan|year = 2006|isbn = 8189235567|location = New Delhi, India|page = 206}}</ref>

Toxicity

Its major dose-limiting toxicities are corticosteroid in nature, due to the inhibitory effect its chief active constituents, glycyrrhizin and enoxolone, have on cortisol degradation and include oedema, hypokalaemia, weight gain or loss, and hypertension.<ref>{{cite journal|last=Olukoga|first=A|author2=Donaldson, D |title=Liquorice and its health implications.|journal=The Journal of the Royal Society for the Promotion of Health|date=June 2000|volume=120|issue=2|pages=83–9|doi=10.1177/146642400012000203|pmid=10944880}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|last=Armanini|first=D|author2=Fiore, C |author3=Mattarello, MJ |author4=Bielenberg, J |author5= Palermo, M |title=History of the endocrine effects of licorice.|journal=Experimental and Clinical Endocrinology & diabetes|date=September 2002|volume=110|issue=6|pages=257–61|doi=10.1055/s-2002-34587|pmid=12373628}}</ref>

The United States Food and Drug Administration believes that foods containing liquorice and its derivatives (including glycyrrhizin) are safe if not consumed excessively. Other jurisdictions have suggested no more than 100&nbsp;mg to 200&nbsp;mg of glycyrrhizin per day, the equivalent of about {{convert|70|to|150|g|abbr=on}} of liquorice.<ref name="SAGE Publications"/>
