It is a staple dessert in any Portuguese home during the holidays.<ref> A Portuguese Christmas Retrieved 12 August 2013 </ref>

History

{{lang|pt|Bolo rei}} recipe is from France which finds its way to Portugal during the 19th century when {{lang|pt|Confeitaria Nacional}}<ref>Bolo-Rei: The King of Portuguese Christmas Cakes Retrieved 12 August 2013 </ref> opened as Portuguese monarchy’s official bakery in 1829. The {{lang|pt|Confeitaria}} was the first to introduce this recipe to the country.<ref> The Battle for Lisbon’s best pastry Retrieved 12 August 2013 </ref>

The cake itself is round with a large hole in the centre,<ref>A Foodie’s Guide to Christmas in Europe Retrieved 12 August 2013 </ref> resembling a crown covered with crystallized and dried fruit.

The {{lang|pt|bolo rei}} is baked from a soft, white dough, with raisins, various nuts, and crystallized fruit. Also included is the characteristic fava bean, and tradition dictates that whoever finds the fava has to pay for the {{lang|pt|bolo rei}} next year.<ref> Bolo Rei Portuguese Kings Cake – A treat for your Christmas table recipe Retrieved 12 August 2013 </ref> A small prize (usually a small metal toy) was also included within the cake. The inclusion of the prize has been discontinued since mid-90s.

Milestones

In 2009, a giant {{lang|pt|Bolo rei}} measuring about {{convert|70|m}} and weighing around {{convert|200|kg}} was baked for the town of Olhão by pastry chef Felipe Martins of Pastelaria Kubidoce.<ref> Giant Bolo Rei offered to the people of Olhão Retrieved 12 August 2013 </ref>
