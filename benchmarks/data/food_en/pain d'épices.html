{{lang|fr|Pain d'épices}} or {{lang|fr|pain d'épice}} ({{Language with name/for||French|"spice bread"}}, sometimes incorrectly translated as gingerbread) is a French cake or quick bread. Its ingredients, according to {{lang|fr|Le Dictionnaire de l'Académie française}} (1694), were "rye flour, honey and spices".<ref name="toussaintsamat" /><ref name="toussaintsamat" /> In Alsace, a considerable tradition incorporates a pinch of cinnamon.

According to Maguelonne Toussaint-Samat, the commercial production of {{lang|fr|pain d'épices}} was a specialty of Reims, based on a recipe of a pastry cook from Bourges and made popular when Charles VII and his mistress Agnes Sorel expressed their liking for it.<ref name="toussaintsamat" /> The honey used was the dark buckwheat honey of Brittany. In 1571, the Corporation of Spice Bread Makers of Reims were chartered separately from the pastry cooks; in 1596 the Parisian makers of {{lang|fr|pain d'épices}} were given their own charter. The Reims {{lang|fr|pain d'épices}} industry was decimated by World War I.<ref name="meilleur" /> The {{lang|fr|pain d'épices}} of Dijon outpaced its older competitors in the Napoleonic era, and the bread is now considered one of the specialties of that city.

{{lang|fr|Pain d'épices}} was originally a sourdough bread without added leavening; it was left in a wooden trough to rest in a cool place for months, during which the honeyed rye flour experienced fermentation. When ready the dough was cooked in loaf moulds. The modern product usually rises with baking soda, or with baking powder, developed in the nineteenth century.

Because traditional {{lang|fr|pain d'épices}} is sweetened entirely with honey, honey merchants in France often stock loaves of it for sale. {{lang|fr|La Collective des Biscuits et Gâteaux de France}} reserves the name {{lang|fr|pain d'épices pur miel}} ({{Language with name/for||French|"pure honey spice bread"|links=no}}) for {{lang|fr|pain d'épices}} sweetened only with honey.<ref name="repertoire" /><ref name="codesdusage" />
