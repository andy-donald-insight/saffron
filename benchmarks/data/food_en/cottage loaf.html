Cottage loaves are a traditional type of bread originating in England.

A cottage loaf is characterised by its shape, which is essentially that of two round loaves, one on top of the other, with the upper one being rather smaller: the shape is similar to that of the French brioche and the pain chapeau of Finistère.<ref name=ocf>Davidson, A. The Oxford companion to food, OUP, 2006, p.99</ref>

The origins of the name and shape are unknown but possibly extend back hundreds of years.<ref>{{cite book| last = Cauvain| first =Stanley P. |author2=Linda S. Young| isbn = 0-8493-1221-3| title = Baking problems solved| publisher = CRC Press| year = 2001| url = http://books.google.com/books?vid=ISBN0849312213&id=EY_IEkv4CDYC&pg=PA270&lpg=PA270&ots=PYBTbS0yiv&dq=%22Cottage+loaf%22+bread&sig=Rhryr843TNV9fIr9h0oaFh0z13Y| page = 270}}</ref> Elizabeth David, who described the cottage loaf in her English Bread and Yeast Cookery, surmised that the shape may have arisen as a way of saving 'floor space' in old-fashioned bread ovens.<ref name="ocf"/> The name, however, did not first appear in writing until the mid 19th century.<ref name=ayto>Ayto, J. The glutton's glossary: a dictionary of food and drink terms, Routledge, 1990, p.80</ref> It was formerly possible to find an oblong version, known as a "cottage brick", and common in the London area.<ref name="ayto"/>

Cottage loaves, while formerly common, are now rarely found in bakeries, as they are relatively time-consuming and difficult to make, and in common with other round loaves are less convenient for slicing.
