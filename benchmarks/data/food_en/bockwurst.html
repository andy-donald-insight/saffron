Bockwurst

Bockwurst is a German sausage which was first mentioned 1827 in Bavaria.<ref>{{Literatur| Titel=Bayrisches Wörterbuch. Sammlung von Wörtern und Ausdrücken| Autor=Johann Andreas Schmeller| Jahr=1827| Verlag=Cotta| Ort=Stuttgart und Tübingen| Band=Band 1| Seiten=151| Online={{Google Buch|BuchID=uxpbAAAAQAAJ|Seite=151|Linktext=Volltext}}| Zugriff=2014-10-15}}</ref> Nevertheless, an "urban legend" in Berlin claims that it was invented in 1889 by restaurant owner R. Scholtz of Berlin.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.berlinische-monatsschrift.de/lexikon/FrKr/s/Skalitzer_strasse.htm|title=Skalitzer Straße|work=berlinische-monatsschrift.de|accessdate=29 March 2015}}</ref> It is one of the most popular varieties within Germany, and can be found abroad. The sausage is traditionally made from ground veal and pork (tending more towards veal, unlike bratwurst). Bockwurst is flavored with salt, white pepper and paprika. Other herbs, such as chives and parsley, are often also added and, in Germany, bockwurst is often smoked as well. Bockwurst was originally eaten with bock beer and it is usually served with mustard. A natural casing sausage, it is usually cooked by simmering although it may also be grilled. Boiling should be avoided as the casing may split open and the Bockwurst may look unappetizing and lose flavor to the cooking water.

Bockwurst made in America, also from veal and pork, bears more resemblance to the Bavarian Weisswurst in color and taste, albeit parsley is rarely used in this version.
