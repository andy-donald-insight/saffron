A {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}} ({{IPA-pl|zapʲɛˈkanka}}; plural: {{lang|pl|zapiekanki}}, {{IPA-pl|zapʲɛˈkankʲi|pron}}) is an open-face sandwich made of half of a baguette or other long roll of bread, topped with sautéed white mushrooms, cheese and optionally other ingredients, and toasted until the cheese melts. Served hot with ketchup, it is a popular street food in Poland. With its origin dating back to the 1970s, the {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}} is associated with the austere times of Poland's Communist regime, but it has enjoyed renewed demand in the 21st century, which has also brought a wider range of varieties.

Etymology

The Polish word {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}} comes from the verb {{lang|pl|zapiekać}}, which means "to bake a dish so that its ingredients combine and a crispy, browned crust forms on top,"{{efn|In Polish: {{lang|pl|piec potrawę w taki sposób, że jej składniki łączą się ze sobą, a na wierzchu powstaje chrupiąca, przyrumieniona warstwa.}}{{sfn|Wielki słownik języka polskiego PAN}}}} and may refer to any of various casseroles and other foods prepared in this manner.{{sfn|Słownik języka polskiego PWN}} The sense of bread-based street food, as used in this article, probably originated in the 1970s together with the rise of the dish itself.

Preparation and varieties

A {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}} topped with ketchup, mustard and chopped, browned onions

A classic no-frills {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}} is made from one half of a baguette, or any other long roll of white bread, cut lengthwise, as for a submarine sandwich. It may be up to {{convert|50|cm|in}} long.{{sfnp|Bush|2012}} The bread is topped with sliced, sautéed white mushrooms and grated cheese to form an open-face sandwich, which is then toasted until the bread becomes crisp and the cheese melts. Hard, mature yellow cheese with high fat content that melts well in heat, such as Gouda, Edam, Emmental, Tilsit or Cheddar, is best for this purpose; Polish smoked sheep milk cheese, such as oscypek, is also a popular choice.{{sfnp|Chojnacka|2013}} A zapiekanka is best served hot. The typical garnish is tomato ketchup, usually splattered on the cheese in a generous amount.

{{lang|pl|Zapiekanki}} are available throughout Poland in many varieties and levels of quality. Frozen ones, reheated in a microwave oven, are usually soggy and tasteless. Some oven-baked {{lang|pl|zapiekanki}}, on the other hand, are available with a lavish choice of additional ingredients and sauces, which has earned them the moniker of "Polish pizza".{{sfnp|Bush|2012}} Varieties include "diablo" with bacon, pickled cucumbers and spicy sauce; "Gypsy" with ham and sweet and sour sauce; "Greek" with olives and feta cheese; and "Hawaiian" with pineapple and barbecue sauce; patrons may also choose their own combinations. While the {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}} is primarily a street food, home-made versions also exist, such as the "student's {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}}", made from bread, cheese and whatever else is at hand at the moment.{{sfnp|Kraig|Sen|2013}}

History

A microwaved {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}}

{{lang|pl|Zapiekanki}} first appeared in the streets of Polish towns in the 1970s. Under {{lang|pl|Edward Gierek}}'s Polish United Workers' Party leadership, Poland's Communist authorities allowed a degree of private enterprise in the catering industry. This move led to quick proliferation of small family-owned foodservice establishments, known in Polish as {{lang|pl|mała gastronomia}}, or "small gastronomy". Their spread continued during the food shortages of the following decade. They usually took the form of stands or travel trailers serving {{lang|pl|zapiekanki}} along with simple dishes of Polish cuisine, such as {{lang|pl|kiełbasa}} sausage, boiled ham hock or tripe soup, and American fast food staples, like hot dogs, hamburgers and French fries.{{sfnp|Chwalba|2008}}{{sfnp|TVP Info|2015}} The American journalist Anne Applebaum, who first came to Poland in 1988, described the {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}} of that time as "a pizzalike substance" and "a poor relative of its distant Italian cousin", "a mushy white sandwich roll" with "a few overcooked mushrooms" beneath "melted cheese and a squeeze of ketchup", which she ate nonetheless because little else was available.{{sfnp|Applebaum|2013}}

{{lang|pl|Zapiekanka}} stalls in {{lang|pl|Plac Nowy, Kazimierz, Kraków}}, at night

Demand for {{lang|pl|zapiekanki}} fell with the reintroduction of market economy in the 1990s, but remained on the menus of some of those "small gastronomy" establishments that survived the competition with large fast food chains. Some {{lang|pl|zapiekanka}} stands even attained cult following, such as those located in {{lang|pl|Plac Nowy}} (New Square) in the {{lang|pl|Kazimierz}} district of Kraków. Once a Jewish neighborhood, {{lang|pl|Kazimierz}} declined into desolation following the Holocaust, but experienced a revival after Steven Spielberg shot his 1993 film, Schindler's List, on location there.{{sfnp|Vorhees|2010}}{{rp|133}} The district became a tourist attraction and then, a nightlife hub. The dodecagonal brick building constructed in 1900 in the middle of the square, which used to house a kosher butcher's shop, now serves as an outdoor food court operating into the wee hours, with numerous stalls serving all kinds of {{lang|pl|zapiekanki}} to partygoers made hungry by a night of revelry.{{sfnp|Vorhees|2010}}{{rp|17,76}} {{clear}}
