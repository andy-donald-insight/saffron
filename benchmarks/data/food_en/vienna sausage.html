A Vienna sausage ({{lang-de|Wiener Würstchen, Wiener}}, Viennese/Austrian German: Frankfurter Würstel or Würstl, Swiss German Wienerli, Swabian: Wienerle or Saitenwurst) is a kind of sausage most frequently made from chicken, beef, or pork in chicken broth. It describes different products in different places. The word Wiener means Viennese in German;<ref>{{cite web|url=http://dictionary.reverso.net/german-english/Wiener%20%7B1%7D|title=Wiener {1} translation English - German dictionary - Reverso|work=reverso.net}}</ref> in Austria the term Wiener is uncommon for this food item, which instead identifies a type of cold cut.

The ingredients, preparation, size and taste can vary widely by both manufacturer and region of sale.

Europe

In some European countries, cooked and often smoked wiener sausages bought fresh from supermarkets, delicatessens and butcher shops may be called by a name (such as in German or French) which translates in English as "Vienna sausage." Traditionally, they are made from spiced ham. Wieners sold as Vienna sausage in Europe have a taste and texture very much like North American "hot dogs" or "frankfurters", but are usually longer and somewhat thinner, with a very light, edible casing. European Vienna sausage served hot in a long bun with condiments is often called a "hot dog", referring not to the wiener itself, but to the long sandwich as a whole.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.reteteculinare.ro/dictionar/C/9.html |title=Dictionar Culinar - Retete Culinare |publisher=Reteteculinare.ro |date=2015-04-30 |accessdate=2015-10-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mynetfair.com/fr/showroom/104/?1=1&formID=1270540775&p=1&currentThemesID=1042&showCompanies=1&sustained=1&showCompanyTypes=2&showCompanyTypes=0 |title=· mynetfair |publisher=Mynetfair.com |date= |accessdate=2015-10-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|author=Yann Picand, Dominique Dutoit |url=http://dictionnaire.sensagent.com/saucisse+de+vienne/fr-fr/ |title=saucisse de vienne : définition de saucisse de vienne et synonymes de saucisse de vienne (français) |publisher=Dictionnaire.sensagent.com |date= |accessdate=2015-10-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|author=BàS / n° 200602 (p.16-17) |url=http://www.bonasavoir.ch/recherche.php?offset=100&id=1948&type=dossier&rubrique=Dossier+de+la+semaine |title=Bon à savoir > Services > Recherche > Grasse saucisse de Vienne |publisher=Bonasavoir.ch |date= |accessdate=2015-10-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.leshop.ch/leshop/Main.do/direct/fr/Supermarche/-17487/-10080/76579 |title=Le premier supermarché en ligne de Suisse |publisher=LeShop.ch |date= |accessdate=2015-10-08}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.leshop.ch/leshop/Main.do/direct/fr/Supermarche/-17487/-10077/274344 |title=Le premier supermarché en ligne de Suisse |publisher=LeShop.ch |date= |accessdate=2015-10-08}}</ref>

North America

In North America the term "Vienna sausage" has most often come to mean only smaller and much shorter smoked and canned wieners, rather than hot dogs.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/vienna%20sausage |title=Vienna Sausage &#124; Definition of Vienna sausage by Merriam-Webster |publisher=Merriam-webster.com |date= |accessdate=2015-10-08}}</ref> North American vienna sausages are made from pork wieners, finely ground to a paste consistency and mixed with salt and spices, most notably mustard, then stuffed into a long casing, sometimes smoked and always thoroughly cooked, after which the casings are removed as with hot dogs. The sausages are then cut into short segments for canning and further cooking. They are also available plain (in a gelatin, similar to aspic) or with a variety of flavorings, such as smoke, chili or barbecue sauces.

In some regions (especially the South) these canned Vienna Sausages are colloquially referred to as "Veye-EEnna" or "Vie-ee-nee" (vɑI-iː-nə / vɑI-iː-ni) sausages, or simply "vie-ee-nees." They are a common staple in lunches for skilled trade workers (such as construction trades) due to their ability to be eaten without being heated, their portability and stable shelf life in hot climates. They are often paired with saltine or Ritz Crackers.<ref>{{cite book|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=FGz5nz1VD3QC&lpg=PA51&dq=ritz%20cracker%20and%20vienna%20sausage&pg=PA50#v=onepage&q=ritz%20cracker%20and%20vienna%20sausage&f=false |title=I Want to Be Left Behind: Finding Rapture Here on Earth - Brenda Peterson - Google Books |publisher=Books.google.com |date= |accessdate=2015-10-08}}</ref>

In popular culture

Homer Simpson, patriarch of the Simpson family on the popular sitcom of the same name, expressed his love for canned Vienna sausages in the season three episode of The Simpsons titled "Black Widower". Homer said: "You can't go wrong with cocktail weenies. They taste as good as they look and they come with this delicious red sauce. It looks like ketchup, tastes like ketchup, but brother, it ain’t ketchup."
