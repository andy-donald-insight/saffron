Pork jowl (alternately called jowl bacon or, especially in the Southern United States, hog jowl) is cured and smoked cheeks of pork. Hog jowl is a staple of soul food,<ref name="Gillespie2009">{{cite book|last=Gillespie|first=Carmen|title=Toni Morrison: A Literary Reference to Her Life and Work|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=Qo5h9LqqanAC&pg=PA343|accessdate=28 July 2012|year=2009|publisher=Infobase Publishing|isbn=9781438108575|page=343}}</ref> but is also used outside the United States; the Italian variant is called guanciale.<ref name="nytimes">{{cite news | url=http://www.nytimes.com/2011/09/14/dining/jowciale-pork-jowl-with-a-backwoods-whiff.html | title=Pork Jowl With a Backwoods Whiff | newspaper=New York Times | date=September 13, 2011 | accessdate=July 27, 2012 | author=Fabricant, Florence}}</ref><ref name="May2005">{{cite book|last=May|first=Tony|title=Italian Cuisine: The New Essential Reference to the Riches of the Italian Table|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=0tAafwz2YcQC&pg=PA11|accessdate=28 July 2012|date=2005-06-01|publisher=Macmillan|isbn=9780312302801|page=11}}</ref>

Uses

Jowl bacon can be fried and eaten as a main course, similar to streaky bacon, such as in a traditional full English breakfast. Often, it is used as a seasoning for beans, black-eyed peas or with cooked with leafy green vegetables such as collard greens or turnip greens in a traditional Southeastern meal.<ref name="HedgepethFindley2008">{{cite book|last1=Hedgepeth|first1=William|last2=Findley|first2=John|last3=Clayton|first3=Al|title=The Hog Book|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=Ly2SYt07jDgC&pg=PA23|accessdate=28 July 2012|year=2008|publisher=University of Georgia Press|isbn=9780820332734|page=23}}</ref><ref name="about">{{cite web | url=http://littlerock.about.com/b/2010/12/31/hog-jowls-and-pork-explaining-southern-new-years-traditions.htm | title=Hog Jowls and Pork: Explaining Southern New Year's Traditions | publisher=About.com | date=December 31, 2010 | accessdate=July 27, 2012 | author=Galiano, Amanda}}</ref> A Southern US tradition of eating black-eyed peas and greens with either pork jowls or fatback on New Year's Day to ensure prosperity throughout the new year goes back hundreds of years.<ref name="ut">{{cite news | url=http://www.utsandiego.com/uniontrib/20061230/news_lz1n30nowread.html | title=Eating hog jowls may bring luck, at high price | newspaper=Union-Tribune | date=December 30, 2006 | accessdate=July 27, 2012 | author=Credeur, Mary Jane}}</ref>

Jowl meat may also be chopped and used as a garnish, similar to bacon bits,<ref name="latimes">{{cite news | url=http://www.latimes.com/features/food/la-fo-gold-20120728,0,1308232.story | title=Counter Intelligence: Next Door by Josie | newspaper=Los Angeles Times | date=July 27, 2012 | accessdate=July 27, 2012 | author=Gold, Jonathan}}</ref> or served in sandwich form.<ref name="observ">{{cite news | url=http://www.newsobserver.com/2012/07/26/2220109/little-hens-agrarian-accent-leaves.html | title=Little Hen's agrarian accent leaves a mouth-watering experience | newspaper=News Observer | accessdate=July 27, 2012 | author=Cox, Greg}}</ref>

Pork jowl can be used as a binding ingredient in pork liver sausages such as liverwurst and braunschweiger.

Because pork jowl is cured, like many other cuts of pork, it has been a traditional wintertime food as it is able to be stored for long periods of time without refrigeration.

Health risks

Like most cured pork products, pork jowls are high in nitrates and sodium, which give the potential for health risks, particularly for those with a family history of heart disease.<ref name="ut" />
