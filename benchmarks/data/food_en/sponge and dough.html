Method

A sponge ferment is usually a sticky process that uses part of the flour, part or all of the water, and part or all of the yeast of a total- or straight-dough formula. Highly liquid sponges of batter consistencies are mixed with a whip, spoon, or fork. Lower hydration, stiffer sponges are lightly mixed or kneaded just until the dough begins to develop. The sponge is allowed to rest and ferment for a period of time in an environment of a desired temperature and humidity. When the sponge's fermentation time has elapsed or it has reached a desired volumetric growth characteristic, the final dough's ingredients are added. The gluten is developed in the mixing or kneading process, and it may then be processed through further work and rest cycles before being proofed then baked.

The sponge's fermentation time depends on its temperature and that of the surrounding area, the ingredients used, and the percentage amount of yeast. It ferments in a humid environment at {{convert|74|-|78|°F|°C}}, where it may rise and expand to 4-5 times its original volume, when it falls it has reached 66-70% of its allotted time.<ref name="xktisbn0-8138-1942-3">{{cite book |author=Ramaswamy, H.; Marcotte, M |title=Food processing: principles and applications |publisher=Blackwell Pub |location=Cambridge, MA |year=2004 |page=194 |isbn=0-8138-1942-3}}</ref>

Purpose

The sponge method is used for 3 different reasons: taste, texture and chemistry.

The flavour that is created is dependent on the ingredients used and the fermenting yeast. Just like sourdough, the longer the ferment, the greater the taste difference.

Sponge doughs were used before bread improvers were invented. Texture is partly a byproduct of the chemistry going on in the fermentation, which does several important things such as activate the different enzymes (protease and amylase) needed to leaven bread. Modern grain-harvesting practices have reduced the naturally-occurring enzymes that grains had in former times, a result of no-longer-used grain-storage processes,<ref name="pp39-41isbn0-89529-221-1">{{cite book |author=Murray, Maynard; Howell, Edward |title=Enzyme nutrition: the food enzyme concept |publisher=Avery Pub. Group |location=Wayne, N.J |year=1985 |pages=39–41 |isbn=0-89529-221-1 |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=h9EgG8O7GgIC&pg=PA39#v=onepage&q&f=false |accessdate=2012-01-29}}</ref> so today small amounts of enzymes are routinely added to flour by manufacturers,<ref name="p345isbn0-8138-0187-7">{{cite book |author=Nanna A. Cross; Corke, Harold; Ingrid De Leyn; Nip, Wai-Kit |title=Bakery products: science and technology |publisher=Blackwell |location=Oxford |year=2006 |page=345 |isbn=0-8138-0187-7}}</ref> often in the form of malted barley or sprouted grain.

Proteases, dependent on their time of action and concentration levels,<ref name="pp204-206isbn0-7514-0249-4">{{cite book |author=Woods, L. F, J.; Tucker, G. S. L. |title=Enzymes in food processing |publisher=Blackie Academic & Professional |location=London |year=1995 |pages=204–206 |isbn=0-7514-0249-4}}</ref> soften the gluten in the dough, hydrolyzing peptide bonds,<ref name="p136isbn0-442-00353-6">{{cite book |author=Stauffer, Clyde E. |title=Functional additives for bakery foods |publisher=Van Nostrand Reinhold |location=New York |year=1990 |page=136 |isbn=0-442-00353-6}}</ref> increasing dough extensibility which allows the protein matrix to stretch out as the mix expands, thus leading to increased baked volumes and better structure.<ref name="pp109-111isbn3-527-31689-2">{{cite book |author=Wolfgang Aehle |title=Enzymes in Industry: Production and Applications |publisher=Wiley-VCH |location=Weinheim |year=2007 |pages=109–111 |isbn=3-527-31689-2 |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=9RGwwbLuIIQC&pg=PA109#v=onepage&q&f=false |accessdate=2012-01-29}}</ref>
