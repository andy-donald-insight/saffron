Lacinato kale (called cavolo nero, literally "black kale", in Italian and often in English) is a variety of kale with a long tradition in Italian cuisine, especially that of Tuscany. It is also known as Tuscan kale, Tuscan cabbage, Italian kale, dinosaur kale, black kale, flat back cabbage, palm tree kale, or black Tuscan palm.<ref name=goin>{{cite book|last1=Goin|first1=Suzanne|last2=Gelber|first2=Teri|title=Sunday Suppers at Lucques: Seasonal Recipes from Market to Table |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=F8i3E4emYJIC&lpg=PT236&dq=cavolo%20nero%20lacinato%20kale&pg=PT236#v=onepage&q=cavolo%20nero%20lacinato%20kale&f=false|year=2005|publisher=Random House Digital, Inc.|isbn=|page=236}}</ref><ref name=thorness>{{cite book|last1=Thorness|first1=Bill|title=Edible Heirlooms: Heritage Vegetables for the Maritime Garden |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=xCobUshBhpoC&lpg=PA90&dq=lacinato%20kale%20gardening&pg=PA90#v=onepage&q=lacinato%20kale%20gardening&f=false|year=2009|publisher=Skipstone|isbn=978-1-59485-142-1|page=90}}</ref> Lacinato kale has been grown in Tuscany for centuries,<ref name=appleman>{{cite book|last1=Appleman|first1=Nate|last2=Lindgren|first2=Shelley|last3=Leahy|first3=Kate|title=A16: Food + Wine|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=1gIWoYep8-kC&pg=PA230|year=2008|publisher=Random House|isbn=978-1-58008-907-4|page=230}}</ref> and is one of the traditional ingredients of minestrone<ref>{{cite book|last1=Brennan|first1=Georgeanna|last2=Koons|first2=Todd|last3=Frankeny|first3=Frankie|title=Great Greens: Fresh, Flavorful, and Innovative Recipes|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=fRBbX-G3XW4C&pg=PA30|year=2003|publisher=Chronicle|isbn=978-0-8118-3907-5|page=30}}</ref> and ribollita.

Description

Lacinato kale grows 2 to 3 feet tall<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.drweil.com/drw/u/ART03040/Tuscan-Kale.html|title=Tuscan Kale - Dr. Weil's Garden}}</ref> and has dark blue-green leaves with an "embossed texture"; its taste is described as "slightly sweeter and more delicate&nbsp;... than curly kale."<ref>{{cite book|last1=Murray|first1=Michael T.|last2=Pizzorno|first2=Joseph|last3=Pizzorno|first3=Lara|title=The Encyclopedia of Healing Foods|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=LLFLfbiWpqgC&pg=PA210|year=2005|publisher=Simon and Schuster|isbn=978-0-7434-7402-3|page=210}}</ref> The lacinato variety is sometimes called "dinosaur kale" because its bumpy leaves are said to resemble what dinosaur skin is thought to have looked like.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.farmerd.com/product/575/seeds|title=Lacinato Dinosaur Kale|date=2012-10-30|publisher=Farmer D Organics|accessdate=10 October 2012}}</ref> Because of its taste, "slightly bitter [and] earthy",<ref>{{cite book|last1=Middleton|first1=Susie|last2=Fink|first2=Ben|title=Fast, Fresh, and Green: More Than 90 Delicious Recipes for Veggie Lovers|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=LDrdwuVSMP8C&pg=PT166|year=2010|publisher=Chronicle|isbn=978-0-8118-6566-1|page=166}}</ref> it has been called "the darling of the culinary world".<ref>{{cite book|last=Soler|first=Ivette|title=The Edible Front Yard: The Mow-Less, Grow-More Plan for a Beautiful, Bountiful Garden|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=plbMQy5V5I0C&pg=PA49|year=2011|publisher=Timber Press|isbn=978-1-60469-199-3|page=49}}</ref>

Preparation and dishes

Lacinato kale, like most other kale varieties, is usually blanched first, and then sautéed with other, flavorful ingredients; in Campanian cuisine, anchovies are often added.<ref name=appleman/> It is commonly used in pastas and soups, but can also be eaten raw, in a salad.<ref>{{cite book|last=Ross|first=Jenny|title=Raw Basics: Incorporating Raw Living Foods Into Your Diet Using Easy and Delicious Recipes|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=kk2alR8WZ3oC&pg=PA18|year=2011|publisher=Hay House|isbn=978-1-4019-3166-7|page=18}}</ref>

In Tuscan cuisine, lacinato kale is often used in ribollita ("twice cooked"), a thick, hearty soup made up of ingredients cooked for a meal the day before.<ref>{{cite book|last=Dickie|first=John|title=Delizia!: The Epic History of the Italians and Their Food|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=nB6NtvQhYDYC&pg=PA285|year=2008|publisher=Simon and Schuster|isbn=978-0-7432-7799-0|page=285}}</ref>

This kale is also used in other cuisines. In Montenegro and Croatia, it is known under the name raštan, raštika, or crno zelje (literally, black cabbage), and used as a side in winter dishes. In Dutch, it is called zwarte kool (black cabbage).

Cultivation

Lacinato kale dates to the 18th century in Italy.<ref name="staub" /> This cultivar is popular among gardeners because of its color and texture,<ref name="staub">{{cite book|last1=Staub|first1=Jack E.|last2=Buchert|first2=Ellen|title=75 Exciting Vegetables for Your Garden|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=ZfSmSmC0WhMC&pg=PA120|year=2005|publisher=Gibbs-Smith|isbn=978-1-58685-250-4|page=120}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|last=Ryrie|first=Charles|title=The Country Garden|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=vbImwF8GsNsC&q=Lacinato&dq=Lacinato&hl=en&ei=yZjlTbWoN4HpgAeTsLmnBg&sa=X&oi=book_result&ct=result&resnum=6&ved=0CDwQ6AEwBTiqAQ|year=2003|publisher=Reader's Digest|isbn=978-0-7621-0391-1|page=111}}</ref> and was listed amongst the plants Thomas Jefferson recorded in his 1777 garden at Monticello.<ref name="tj">{{cite book|last=Jefferson|first=Thomas |authorlink=Thomas Jefferson|editor=Edwin Morris Betts|title=Thomas Jefferson's Garden Book|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=kDZy5EovSc4C&lpg=PA71&dq=cavolo%20nero%20gardening&pg=PA71#v=onepage&q&f=false|accessdate=June 1, 2011|origyear=1999|year=2002|publisher=Thomas Jefferson Memorial Foundation, UNC Press|isbn=978-1-882886-11-1|page=71}}</ref> The plant grows to a height of two feet, with blistered leaves often over one foot in length each and two to four inches wide.<ref name="staub" /> The "straplike"<ref name="idiot">{{cite book|last1=McLaughlin|first1=Chris|title=The Complete Idiot's Guide to Heirloom Vegetables |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=r3IaTHvsFQEC&lpg=PT139&dq=lacinato%20kale%20gardening&pg=PT139#v=onepage&q&f=false|publisher=Penguin|isbn=978-1-61564-052-2|year=2010|page=139}}</ref> leaves are typically harvested from the bottom of the stem, leaving the remainder of the plant looking like a palm tree.<ref name="staub" />
