Blue Agave (Agave tequilana)

Because agave nectar dissolves quickly, it can be used as a sweetener for cold beverages such as cocktails, smoothies, and iced tea.

Agave nectar (more accurately called agave syrup) is a sweetener commercially produced from several species of agave, including Agave tequilana (blue agave) and Agave salmiana.<ref>{{cite journal |url=http://www.thenibble.com/reviews/nutri/cookies/agave-ice-cream.asp |first=Karen |last=Hocman |title=Agave Nectar a.k.a. Agave Syrup |journal=The Nibble |date=August 2009}}</ref><ref name="agave">{{cite web |url=http://www.ethnoleaflets.com//leaflets/agave.htm |title=Blue Agave and Its Importance in the Tequila Industry |publisher=Ethnobotanical Leaflets |author=Gary M. Mohr, Jr. |date=3 Oct 1999 |accessdate=2010-01-04 }}</ref> Agave syrup is sweeter than honey and tends to be less viscous. Most agave syrup comes from Mexico and South Africa.

Agave syrup has been marketed as a "healthful"<!-- not HEALTHY --> sweetener, but this fact has been the subject of criticism due to its very high fructose content (which is even more than high fructose corn syrup in its fructose content by weight) and its potential to lead to insulin resistance and significantly increased triglyceride levels (a risk factor for heart disease).<ref name="agavesyrup">{{cite web |url=http://www.huffingtonpost.com/dr-jonny-bowden/debunking-the-blue-agave_b_450144.html |title=Debunking the Blue Agave Myth |publisher=HuffingtonPost.com |author=Dr. Jonny Bowden |date=15 Feb 2010 |accessdate=2014-01-06 }}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.thewellnesswarrior.com.au/2011/05/why-i-was-wrong-to-recommend-agave-nectar/|title=Why I was wrong to recommend agave nectar|first=Jess|last=Ainscough|date=8 May 2011|publisher=The Wellness Warrior}}</ref>

Production

To produce agave syrup from the Agave americana and A. tequilana plants, the leaves are cut off the plant after it has aged seven to fourteen years. The juice is then extracted from the core of the agave, called the piña.<ref name="agave" /> The juice is filtered, then heated to break the complex components (the polysaccharides) into simple sugars.<sup>[Need to cite reference for production process]</sup> The main polysaccharide is called inulin or fructosan and is mostly fructose. This filtered juice is then concentrated to a syrupy liquid, slightly thinner than honey. Its color varies from light- to dark-amber, depending on the degree of processing.

Agave salmiana is processed differently from Agave tequiliana. As the plant develops, it starts to grow a stalk called a quiote.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.ncsu.edu/jcraulstonarboretum/horticulture/interpretive_signs/interpretive_signs_other_topics.php?UniqueID=14 |title=Monocarpic Behavior in Agaves|publisher=J. C. Raulston Arboretum, North Carolina State University |accessdate=2010-01-04 |date=June 19, 2009}}</ref> The stalk is cut off before it fully grows, creating a hole in the center of the plant that fills with a liquid called aguamiel. The liquid is collected daily. The liquid is then heated, breaking down its complex components into fructose and glucose and preventing it from fermenting into pulque.

An alternative method used to process the agave juice without heat is described in a United States patent for a process that uses enzymes derived from the mold Aspergillus niger to convert the inulin-rich extract into fructose.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.patentstorm.us/patents/5846333.html | title=Method of producing fructose syrup from agave plants (United States Patent 5846333) | date=1998-12-08}}</ref> Aspergillus niger, a fungus commonly used in industrial fermentations,<ref name=industrial_mycology1991>{{cite book|last=Onions|first=A.H.S., Allsopp D., Eggins H.O.W.|title=Smith's Introduction to industrial mycology.|year=1991|publisher=Cambridge University Press|location=Cambridge, UK|isbn=9780521427821|edition=7th}}</ref> is "generally recognized as safe" (GRAS) by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.cfsan.fda.gov/~rdb/opa-gras.html |title=Inventory of GRAS Notices: Summary of all GRAS Notices |date=2008-10-22 |work=US FDA/CFSAN |accessdate=2008-10-31| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20081011163648/http://www.cfsan.fda.gov/%7Erdb/opa-gras.html| archivedate= 11 October 2008 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref>

Composition

Agave nectar consists primarily of fructose and glucose. One source gives 47% fructose and 16% glucose;<ref>"Volcanic Nectar Blue Agave" Global Goods Inc. (Retrieved 2013)</ref> another gives 56% fructose and 20% glucose.<ref>{{cite journal | url=http://www.uni-giessen.de/fbr09/food/download/Publikationen/J%20agric%20food%20chem%202005%2053%209722-9729_jf051433u.pdf | title=Mass Spectrometric Detection and Formation of D-Amino Acids in Processed Plant Saps, Syrups, and Fruit Juice Concentrates | author=Ralf Patzold and Hans Bruckner | journal=J. Agric. Food Chem | year=2005 | volume=53 |issue=25 | pages=9722–9729 | doi=10.1021/jf051433u| accessdate=2 November 2010}}</ref> These differences probably reflect variation from one vendor of agave nectar to another.

Culinary use

Agave syrup is 1.4 to 1.6 times sweeter than sugar<ref>{{cite news |url=http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052748704335904574497622806733800.html |title=Looking at Health Claims of Agave Nectar |newspaper=The Wall Street Journal |date=October 27, 2009 |author=Johannes, Laura |accessdate=2010-01-04| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20100102234657/http://online.wsj.com/article/SB10001424052748704335904574497622806733800.html| archivedate= 2 January 2010 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref> and is often substituted for sugar or honey in recipes.<ref>"Agave Nectar Recipes," Global Goods Inc and Cook'n With Agave♙</ref> In cooking, it is commonly used as a vegan alternative to honey for those who choose to exclude animal products from their diets.<ref name=LiveStrong_VeganSweetener>{{cite web |accessdate=2010-10-03 |url=http://www.livestrong.com/article/203571-vegan-sugar-substitutes/ |title=Vegan Sugar Substitutes |work=LiveStrong.com| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20101013030310/http://www.livestrong.com/article/203571-vegan-sugar-substitutes/| archivedate= 13 October 2010 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref> Agave syrup dissolves quickly and so it can be used as a sweetener for cold beverages such as iced tea. It is added to some breakfast cereals as a binding agent.<ref>{{cite journal|last=Chomka|first=Stefan|date=30 July 2007|title=Dorset Cereals|journal=The Grocer|publisher=William Reed Business Media|location=Crawley, England|url=http://www.thegrocer.co.uk/articles.aspx?page=independentarticle&ID=120966|accessdate=16 January 2011}}</ref>

Agave syrups are sold in light, amber, dark, and raw varieties. Light agave syrup has a mild, almost neutral flavor, and is therefore sometimes used in delicate-tasting dishes and beverages. Amber agave syrup has a medium-intensity caramel flavor and is therefore used in dishes and drinks with stronger flavors. Dark agave syrup has stronger caramel notes and imparts a distinct flavor to dishes, such as some desserts, poultry, meat, and seafood dishes. Both amber and dark agave syrups are sometimes used "straight out of the bottle" as a topping for pancakes, waffles, and French toast. The dark version is unfiltered and therefore contains a higher concentration of the agave plant's minerals.<ref>{{cite book |title=Anna Getty's Easy Green Organic |last1=Getty |first1=Anna |others=Dan Goldberg and Ron Hamad, photographs |year=2010 |publisher=Chronicle Books |location=San Francisco |isbn=0-8118-6668-8 |page=141 |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=LVkmCNXBzwwC&pg=PA141#v=onepage&q&f=false |accessdate=25 November 2011}}</ref> Raw agave syrup also has a mild, neutral taste.{{Dubious|date= July 2013}} It is produced at temperatures below {{convert|118|F|C}} to protect the natural enzymes, so this variety could be considered an appropriate sweetener for raw foodists.<ref>Ania Catalano. Baking With Agave Nectar: Over 100 Recipes Using Nature's Ultimate Sweetener, 2008, Celestial Arts, p. ix.</ref>

Health effects

The impact of agave syrup on blood sugar (as measured by its glycemic index and glycemic load) is comparable to fructose,<ref name=AboutAgave_GI>{{cite web|accessdate=2010-10-03|url=http://www.allaboutagave.com/agave-nectar-and-the-glycemic-index.php|title=Agave Nectar and the Glycemic Index|publisher=All About Agave| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20100917061715/http://allaboutagave.com/agave-nectar-and-the-glycemic-index.php| archivedate= 17 September 2010 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref><ref name=AboutAgave_vsHoney>{{cite web|accessdate=2010-10-03|url=http://www.allaboutagave.com/agave-vs-liquid.php|title=Agave Nectar vs. Liquid Sugars|publisher=All About Agave| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20100917061727/http://allaboutagave.com/agave-vs-liquid.php| archivedate= 17 September 2010 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref> which has a much lower glycemic index and glycemic load than table sugar (sucrose).<ref name=AboutAgave_vsSugar>{{cite web|accessdate=2010-10-03|url=http://www.allaboutagave.com/agave-vs-granular.php|title=Agave Nectar vs. Granular Sugars|publisher=All About Agave| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20100917061538/http://allaboutagave.com/agave-vs-granular.php| archivedate= 17 September 2010 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal| url=http://www.mendosa.com/gilists.htm| title=Revised International Table of Glycemic Index (GI) and Glycemic Load (GL) Values—2002 | author=David Mendosa| accessdate=2 November 2010| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20101110004946/http://www.mendosa.com/gilists.htm| archivedate= 10 November 2010 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref>

However, consumption of large amounts of fructose can be deleterious and can trigger fructose malabsorption, metabolic syndrome,<ref name="PMC552336">{{cite journal |author=Basciano H, Federico L, Adeli K |title=Fructose, insulin resistance, and metabolic dyslipidemia |journal=Nutrition & Metabolism |volume=2 |issue=5 |pages= |year=2005 |pmc=552336 |doi=10.1186/1743-7075-2-5 |pmid=15723702}}</ref> hypertriglyceridemia, decreased glucose tolerance, hyperinsulinemia, and accelerated uric acid formation.<ref name="metabolism of fructose -pmid8213607">{{cite journal |author=Mayes, PA |title=Intermediary metabolism of fructose |journal=Am J Clin Nutr. |volume=58 |issue=5 Suppl|pages=754S–765S|date=Nov 1993 |pmid=8213607 |doi= }}</ref><ref name="fructose increases uric acid -pmid10954012">{{cite journal |author=Buemann B, Toubro S, Holst JJ, Rehfeld JF, Bibby BM, Astrup A |title=D-tagatose, a stereoisomer of D-fructose, increases blood uric acid concentration |journal=Metabolism |volume=49 |issue=8|pages=969–76 |date=Aug 2000 |pmid=10954012 |doi= 10.1053/meta.2000.7724|pmid=8213607}}</ref><ref name="Yet another reason to avoid fructose -davis20081206">{{cite web |author=Davis, W |title=Yet another reason to avoid fructose |work=The Heart Scan Blog | date=December 6, 2008|url=http://heartscanblog.blogspot.com/2008/12/yet-another-reason-to-avoid-fructose.html | accessdate=2 November 2010| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20101010124813/http://heartscanblog.blogspot.com/2008/12/yet-another-reason-to-avoid-fructose.html| archivedate= 10 October 2010 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref>

Due to its high fructose levels, agave syrup has the potential to lead to insulin resistance and significantly increased triglyceride levels (a risk factor for heart disease).<ref name="agavesyrup"/>
