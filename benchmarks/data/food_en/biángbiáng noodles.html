Biángbiáng noodles ({{zh|s= 面|t= 麵|p=Biángbiáng miàn}}), also known as ({{zh|s=油泼扯面|t=油潑扯麵|p=Yóupō chěmiàn}}), are a type of noodle popular in China's Shaanxi province. The noodles, touted as one of the "ten strange wonders of Shaanxi" ({{zh|c=陕西十大怪}}), are described as being like a belt, owing to their thickness and length. The "Noodle King" chain in Beijing ({{zh|c= 面北京连锁店}}) serves biáng biáng noodles.

Description

The noodle is broad and hand-made. It was part of a poor-man's meal in the countryside, but has recently become popular in trendy restaurants due to its unique character name.{{Citation needed|date=October 2010}}

Use in dishes

Dishes with this noodle are often topped with lots of red hot peppers for the cold winter in Shaanxi.

Chinese character for biáng

{{multiple images| | direction = horizontal | width = 100 | image1 = Biáng (regular script).svg | width1 = | alt1 = | caption1 = The character for biáng in calligraphic regular script. | image2 = Biáng.svg | width2 = | alt2 = | caption2 = The character for biáng in a Song font. }}

Made up of 58 strokes, the Chinese character for "biáng" is one of the most complex Chinese characters in contemporary usage, although the character is not found in modern dictionaries or even in the Kangxi dictionary.

The character is composed of {{lang|zh|言}} (speak; 7 strokes) in the middle flanked by {{lang|zh|幺}} (tiny; 2×3 strokes) on both sides. Below it, {{lang|zh|馬}} (horse; 10 strokes) is similarly flanked by {{lang|zh|長}} (grow; 2×8 strokes). This central block itself is surrounded by {{lang|zh|月}} (moon; 4 strokes) to the left, {{lang|zh|心}} (heart; 4 strokes) below, {{lang|zh|刂}} (knife; 2 strokes) on the right, and {{lang|zh|八}} (eight; 2 strokes) above. These in turn are surrounded by a second layer of characters, namely {{lang|zh|宀}} (roof; 3 strokes) on the top and {{lang|zh|辶}} (walk; 4 strokes) curving around the left and bottom.

Phonetic substitution

The Chinese character for "biáng" cannot be entered into computers. Therefore, phonetic substitutes like {{zh|c=彪彪面|p=biāobiāo miàn}} or {{zh|c=冰冰面|p=bīngbīng miàn}}) are often used.

The character is described by the following ideographic description sequence:<ref name="irg">See Unicode Technical Report #45 and associated data File, UTC-00791. The file references this Wikipedia article as a primary source and a reason for inclusion.</ref>

<span style="font-size:larger">⿺辶⿳穴⿲月⿱⿲幺言幺⿲長馬長刂心</span>

Unicode

The character has not been added to Unicode yet, but is being considered by the IRG for inclusion in the CJK Unified Ideographs Extension F block.<ref name="irg" />

Origin of the character

Restaurant specializing in Biang Biang noodles

A Xi'an biang biang mian restaurant.

The origins of the biang biang noodles and the character biáng are unclear. In one version of the story, the character biáng was invented by the Qin Dynasty Premier Li Si. However, since the character is not found in the Kangxi Dictionary, it may have been created much later than the time of Li Si. Similar characters were found used by Tiandihui.

In the 2007 season of the TVB show The Web ({{zh|c=一網打盡}}), the show's producers tried to find the origin of the character by contacting university professors, but they could not verify the Li Si story or the origin of the character. It was concluded that the character was invented by the noodle store{{clarify|July 20111|date=July 2011}}.

One theory is that there was no such character or meaning for this word in the beginning, and the word actually came from the sound people make from chewing such delicious noodles, "biang biang biang".

According to an article on China Daily, the word "biang" actually refers to the sound made by the chef when he creates the noodles by pulling the dough and slapping it on the table.<ref>{{cite web|title=China Daily: Biangbiang Shaanxi street food|url=http://www.chinadaily.com.cn/food/2012-11/18/content_15939056.htm|accessdate=19 November 2012}}</ref>
