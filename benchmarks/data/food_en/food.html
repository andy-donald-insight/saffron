FOOD was an important artist-run restaurant in SoHo, New York. FOOD was founded by artists Carol Goodden, Tina Girouard<nowiki> </nowiki>and Gordon Matta-Clark. FOOD was considered one of the first important restaurants in SoHo.<ref name=":32">{{Cite web|title = Carol Goodden & Gordon Matta-Clark's Food|url = http://www.brokencitylab.org/blog/carol-goodden-gordon-matta-clarks-food/|accessdate = 29 May 2015|website = Broken City Lab|first = |date = 29 October 2009|last = Justin}}</ref> Other individuals who were involved with FOOD included Suzanne Harris and Rachel Lew.<ref name=":14" /> FOOD was a place where artists in SoHo, especially those who were later involved in Avalanche magazine and the Anarchitecture group, could meet and enjoy food together.<ref name=":14" /> FOOD was considered to be both a business and an artistic "intervention in an urban setting."<ref name=":14" /> It has also been called a "landmark that still resonates in the history and mythology of SoHo in the 1970s."<ref>{{Cite web|title = Food, Gordon Matta-Clark|url = http://www.eai.org/title.htm?id=761|website = Electronic Arts Intermix|accessdate = 4 June 2015}}</ref>

History

Before they founded FOOD, Goodden and Matta-Clark were already cooking for artists in New York and were already known for throwing dinner parties for friends, mainly hosted at their loft.<ref name=":62">{{Cite journal|url = https://artmarketbaruch.files.wordpress.com/2012/03/food.pdf|title = The Banquet Years: FOOD, A SoHo Restaurant|last = Waxman|first = Lori|date = 2008|journal = Gastronomica: The Journal of Food and Culture|doi = |pmid = |access-date = 30 May 2015|volume = 8|issue = 4|pages = 24–33}}</ref> In 1971, they roasted an entire pig under the Brooklyn Bridge.<ref name=":22">{{Cite news|url = http://tmagazine.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/05/10/food-matters-when-eating-and-art-became-one/?_r=0|title = Food Matters {{!}} When Eating and Art Became One|last = Kahn|first = Howie|date = 10 May 2013|work = New York Times|access-date = 29 May 2015|via = }}</ref> The roast was known as the Brooklyn Bridge Event.<ref name=":62" /> The Event was accompanied by outdoor sculpture and celebrated the opening of the Alternative Gallery Space on Greene Street.<ref name=":5">{{Cite web|title = Guest Post Series: Bethsheba Goldstein Interviews Carol Goodden about the Origins of FOOD|url = http://sohomemory.com/2012/06/30/guest-post-series-bethsheba-goldstein-interviews-carol-goodden-about-food/|accessdate = 29 May 2015|date = 30 June 2012|website = The SoHo Memory Project|last = Goldstein|first = Bethsheba}}</ref> The sculptures at the pig roast had been curated by Alanna Heiss of the Museum of Modern Art (MOMA.)<ref name=":5" /> Music for the roast was done by Richard Landry and the Phillip Glass Ensemble.<ref name=":5" />

Matta-Clark suggested to Goodden the idea for a restaurant, which she found appealing.<ref name=":5" /> They chose a location in SoHo on the corner of Prince and Wooster streets.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Giving FOOD its due: "FOOD 1971/2013"|url = http://www.artslant.com/ny/articles/show/34937|website = ArtSlant|accessdate = 29 May 2015|date = 9 May 2013|last = Goodman|first = Matthew Shen}}</ref> There was a struggling restaurant there called Comidas Criollas and Goodden negotiated the lease from them.<ref name=":5" /> At that time, SoHo was considered a cultural desert, with struggling businesses.<ref name=":122">{{Cite news|url = http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB10001424127887324266904578459020800238706|title = The Original Artisanal Food|last = Battaglia|first = Andy|date = 2 May 2013|work = The Wall Street Journal|access-date = 1 June 2015|via = }}</ref> Goodden had access to an inheritance which she was able to use as the initial investment for the restaurant.<ref>{{Cite journal|url = http://www.joaap.org/6/lovetowe/schaafsma.html|title = Other Options: A Closer Look at FOOD|last = Schaafsma|first = Ben|date = November 2008|journal = Journal of Aesthetics and Protest|doi = |pmid = |access-date = 3 June 2015|issue = 6}}</ref> Gooden and Matta-Clark wanted to create a space where they could help their friends find jobs while giving people a place to eat in what would later be known as SoHo.<ref name=":02">{{Cite web|title = Why Food (the Restaurant) Is the Talk of the 2013 Frieze Art Fair|url = http://www.vulture.com/2013/05/food-restaurant-at-frieze-art-fair.html|website = Vulture|accessdate = 29 May 2015|last = Swanson|first = Carl|date = 5 May 2013|publisher = New York Media, LLC}}</ref> Matta-Clark and Girouard began to renovate the place.<ref name=":8">{{Cite news|url = http://www.nytimes.com/2007/02/21/dining/21soho.html?pagewanted=all&_r=0|title = When Meals Played the Muse|last = Kennedy|first = Randy|date = 1 February 2007|work = The New York Times|access-date = 3 June 2015|via = }}</ref> FOOD opened on October 1971 at 127 Prince Street.<ref name=":9">{{Cite book|title = FOOD, an Exhibition by White Columns|last = |first = |publisher = Walter Konig|year = 1999|isbn = 3887891333|location = Koln, Germany|pages = |language = English, German|url = http://www.publiccollectors.org/FOOD.pdf|editor-last = Bußmann|editor-first = Klaus|editor-last2 = Muller|editor-first2 = Markus|access-date = 3 June 2015}}</ref> During the renovations, Matta-Clark was inspired to so some of his original "cutting" art works.<ref name=":14" />

FOOD was intended to be a simple project designed to bring the artistic community together.<ref name=":9" /> Artists were invited to be guest chefs, as well as working at the restaurant on a regular basis.<ref name=":8" /> There was no ordering of many different dishes at FOOD, diners ate what was offered on that day.<ref name=":102">{{Cite web|title = Food Curated by Catherine Morris|url = http://gallery400.uic.edu/exhibitions/food/related_pages/exhibition-checklist-expanded|website = Gallery 400|accessdate = 1 June 2015}}</ref> The menu was simple and affordable.<ref name=":7" /> Meals included items like raw mackerel in wasabi sauce, Creole-style stuffed tongue and anchovy onion pie.<ref name=":62"/> FOOD was considered to be an "active and dynamic site" which served nearly one hundred people daily in 1972.<ref name=":14">{{Cite web|title = The Story of FOOD|url = http://www.cca.qc.ca/en/study-centre/1838-the-story-of-food|website = Canadian Centre for Architecture (CCA)|accessdate = 4 June 2015|date = 2011|last = Clintberg|first = Mark}}</ref>

In 1972 Matta-Clark created a 43-minute documentary of the restaurant.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Gordon Matta-Clark - Food (1972)|url = http://www.ubu.com/film/gmc_food.html|website = UbuWeb Film & Video|accessdate = 3 June 2015}}</ref> He was often seen as the center of the energy surrounding FOOD restaurant.<ref name=":15">{{Cite journal|url = http://www.artnet.com/magazine/features/smyth/smyth6-4-04.asp|title = Gordon Matta-Clark|last = Smyth|first = Ned|date = 4 June 2004|journal = Artnet|doi = |pmid = |access-date = 4 June 2015}}</ref>

FOOD only lasted about three years with the original founders.<ref name=":1" /> After Matta-Clark lost interest in the project and Goodden was left to carry on mostly on her own.<ref name=":7" /> Eventually, the restaurant was handed over to new operators who ran FOOD until the 1980s.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|title = Why Food (the Restaurant) Is the Talk of the 2013 Frieze Art Fair|url = http://www.vulture.com/2013/05/food-restaurant-at-frieze-art-fair.html|website = Vulture|accessdate = 29 May 2015|last = Swanson|first = Carl|date = 5 May 2013|publisher = New York Media, LLC}}</ref> However, FOOD did not retain its artistic roots under the new management.<ref name=":72">{{Cite web|title = When Artists Lived In SoHo: A Look Back at the Restaurant FOOD by Gordon Matta-Clark and Carol Goodden|url = http://untappedcities.com/2013/12/12/when-artists-lived-in-soho-a-look-back-at-the-restaurant-food-by-gordon-matta-clark-and-carol-goodden/|website = Untapped Cities|accessdate = 30 May 2015|last = Scavone|first = Enzo|date = 12 December 2013}}</ref>

Legacy

performers, such as Donald Judd, Robert Rauschenberg and John Cage created meals at FOOD.<ref name=":1" /> The cooking and the meals themselves were a kind of a performance art,<ref name=":3">{{Cite web|title = Carol Goodden & Gordon Matta-Clark's Food|url = http://www.brokencitylab.org/blog/carol-goodden-gordon-matta-clarks-food/|accessdate = 29 May 2015|website = Broken City Lab|first = |date = 29 October 2009|last = Justin}}</ref> especially the soup.<ref name=":22"/> Gooden felt that soup could be used as a sort of "painting" for the table.<ref name=":4">{{Cite web|title = Frieze Frame: FOOD 1971|url = http://sohomemory.com/2013/04/27/frieze-frame-food-1971/|website = The SoHo Memory Project|accessdate = 29 May 2015|date = 27 April 2013}}</ref> Matta-Clark developed a meal that he based on bones called Matta-Bones<ref name=":11">{{Cite web|url = http://www.theparisreview.org/blog/2012/07/25/112-greene-street/|title = 112 Greene Street|date = 25 July 2012|accessdate = 1 June 2015|website = The Paris Review|publisher = |last = Barliant|first = Claire}}</ref> which cost $4.<ref name=":1" /> After the meal, the bones were used to create necklaces for the diner to wear.<ref name=":02"/> Matta-Bones<nowiki> </nowiki>once served over 100 people and after they ate, Richard Peck scrubbed the bones clean in the kitchen after which Hisachika Takahashi, an assistant to Scavone|first = Enzo|date = 12 December 2013}}</ref> called Alive.<ref name=":11" /> Perhaps the most unusual dinner was never realized: Mark di Suvero wished to serve meals through the windows of the restaurant using a crane and directing diners to eat with tools such as screwdrivers and hammers.<ref name=":16">{{Cite web|url = http://thegourmand.co.uk/on-screen/matta-clarks-food|title = Matta FOOD was noted for using fresh and seasonal foods, which was a "vaguely countercultural" idea for the time.<ref name=":8" /> FOOD was one of the first New York restaurants to serve sushi<ref name=":12">{{Cite news|url = http://www.wsj.com/articles/SB10001424127887324266904578459020800238706|title = The Original Artisanal Food|last = Battaglia|first = Andy|date = 2 May 2013|work = The Wall Street Journal|access-date = 1 June 2015|via = }}</ref> which had been suggested by Takahashi.<ref name=":8" /> Another unusual feature of FOOD was that it was one of the first places to serve vegetarian meals.<ref name=":12" /> FOOD also "championed" the use of the open kitchen which is seen in many modern restaurants.<ref name=":13">{{Cite web|title = Gordon Matta-Clark's Art Restaurant Resurrected|url = http://www.phaidon.com/agenda/art/articles/2013/may/02/gordon-matta-clarks-art-restaurant-resurrected/|website = Phaidon|accessdate = 4 June 2015|date = 2 May 2013}}</ref>

The art, the new ideas and the famous names that surrounded FOOD have helped keep FOOD alive.<ref name=":16" /> Other famous artists who were frequently seen at FOOD included the members of Mabou Mines, the Phillip Glass group and dancers of Grand Union.<ref name=":15" />

FOOD 1971/2013

The Frieze Art Fair recognized the contributions of FOOD to the artistic community in 2013 by inviting several chefs from the original FOOD to participate at the fair.<ref name=":22"/> Goodden and Girouard both contributed to the tribute of FOOD with Goodden preparing her famous soups and Girouard paying homage to the pig roast under the Brooklyn Bridge.<ref name=":13" /> FOOD 1971/2013 was created by curator, Ceclia Alemani and conceived of as a special project for the fair.<ref name=":16" />
