A Chicago-style hot dog, Chicago Dog, or Chicago Red Hot is an all-beef frankfurter<ref name="eat" /><ref>{{cite web| last = Sweet | first = Lynn. | authorlink = Lynn Sweet | title = Chicago hot dogs at the White House | work =Chicago Sun-Times | date = 2010-06-10 | url = http://www.suntimes.com/news/sweet/2376622,CST-NWS-hotdog10.article | quote = Chicago-style hot dogs are steamed | accessdate = 2010-08-01}}</ref> on a poppy seed bun,<ref name="buns">{{cite web| last = Zeldes | first = Leah A. | authorlink = | title = It takes big buns to hold Chicago hot dogs | work = Dining Chicago | publisher = Chicago's Restaurant & Entertainment Guide, Inc. | date = 2010-07-13 | url = http://www.diningchicago.com/blog/2010/07/13/it-takes-big-buns-to-hold-chicago-hot-dogs/ | doi = | accessdate = 2010-07-31}}</ref> originating from the city of Chicago, Illinois. The hot dog is topped with yellow mustard, chopped white onions, bright green sweet pickle relish, a dill pickle spear, tomato slices or wedges, pickled sport peppers and a dash of celery salt.<ref name="eat" /><ref>{{cite news |first=Charles |last=Leroux |title=Chicago hot dogs |url=http://www.chicagotribune.com/entertainment/chi-wonders-hotdogs,0,3842862.story |work=Chicago Tribune |publisher=Tribune Co |date=2005-08-30 |accessdate=2007-04-28}}</ref><ref name="Toledo Blade">{{cite news |first=Kathie |last=Smith |title=Chicago's food history|url=http://toledoblade.com/apps/pbcs.dll/article?AID=/20070501/ART06/70501015/-1/ART |work=Toledo Blade |publisher=Block Communications |date=2007-05-01 |accessdate=2007-05-01}}</ref><ref name="Fluky's recipe">{{cite web|url=http://www.flukys.com/html/recipe.html |title=How to Make Your own "Chicago Style Hot Dogs" |accessdate=April 28, 2007 |author=Fluky's}}</ref> The complete assembly of a Chicago hot dog is said to be "dragged through the garden" due to the many toppings.<ref name="Zeldes">{{cite news |first=Leah A |last=Zeldes |authorlink = |title=How to Eat Like a Chicagoan |url=http://www.chicagorestaurant.com/show_article.php?aID=13 |work=Chicago's Restaurant Guide |publisher=Chicago's Restaurant Guide |date=2002-09-30 |accessdate=2002-09-30 |archiveurl= https://web.archive.org/web/20021001023605/http://www.chicagorestaurant.com/show_article.php?aID=13 |archivedate= 2002-10-01}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|title=Chicago-style Hot Dogs and Hot Dog Stands |publisher=h2g2 |accessdate = February 4, 2010|date = July 19, 2007|url =http://h2g2.com/edited_entry/A885468}}</ref> The method for cooking the hot dog itself varies depending on the vendor's preference. Most often they are steamed, water-simmered, or less often grilled over charcoal (in which case they are referred to as "char-dogs").

The canonical recipe<ref name="eat" /> does not include ketchup, and there is a widely shared, strong opinion among many Chicagoans and aficionados that ketchup is unacceptable.<ref name="Recipe Detail: Chicago Style Hot Dog">{{cite web|url=http://www.wttw.com/main.taf?erube_fh=wttw&wttw.submit.CPRecipieDetail=1&wttw.RecipieID=26 |title=Recipe Detail: Chicago Style Hot Dog}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|title=The Slow Food guide to Chicago: Restaurants, markets, bars |last=Gibson |first=Kelly |authorlink= |author2=Portia Belloc Lowndes |year=2008 |publisher=Chelsea Green Publishing |location= |isbn=978-1-931498-61-6 |pages=238 |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Mg1wcViP7vgC&pg=PA238&dq=ketchup#v=onepage&q=ketchup&f=false |quote= ...no self-respecting Chicagoan would think of using ketchup as a condiment... |accessdate=February 18, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|title=Fodor's Chicago 2010 |year=2009 |publisher=Fodor's |location=New York |isbn=978-1-4000-0860-5 |page=189 |url=https://books.google.com/?id=e1vL7GVGBRAC&pg=PA189&dq=Chicago+%22hot+dog%22+ketchup#v=onepage&q=Chicago%20%22hot%20dog%22%20ketchup |quote= Make sure to never add ketchup to your Chicago-style hot dog: a major no-no among hot dog aficionados. |accessdate=February 18, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.neverputketchuponahotdog.com/|title=Never Put Ketchup On A Hot Dog, by Bob Schwartz - Home Page|work=neverputketchuponahotdog.com|accessdate=22 November 2015}}</ref><ref>Adams, Cecil (August 30, 1991). History

Many sources attribute the distinctive collection of toppings on a Chicago-style wiener to historic Maxwell Street and the "Depression Sandwich" reportedly originated by Fluky's in 1929.<ref name="eat" /><ref name="Zeldes"/> The founders of Vienna Beef frankfurters—the most common brand served today, first sold at the 1893 World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago<ref name="zeldes">{{cite web| last = Zeldes | first = Leah A. | authorlink = | title = And the wieners are ... Variations

The "dragged through the garden" style is heavily promoted by Vienna Beef and Red Hot Chicago, the two most prominent Chicago hot dog manufacturers,<ref name="makers">{{Cite web | last = Zeldes | first = Leah A. | authorlink = | title = Hot dog makers around town | work = Chicago Sun-Times | publisher = | date = June 21, 2011 | url = http://www.suntimes.com/photos/galleries/5870444-417/hot-dog-makers-around-town.html | doi = | accessdate = 2012-07-17}}</ref> but exceptions are common, with vendors adding cucumber slices or lettuce,<ref name="eat" /> omitting poppyseeds or celery salt, or using plain relish or a skinless hot dog.<ref name="sun-times">{{Cite web | last = Zeldes | first = Leah A. | authorlink = | title = Even without trimmings, Chicago-style hot dog in league of its own | work = Chicago Sun-Times | publisher = | date = June 21, 2011 | url = http://www.suntimes.com/photos/galleries/5869576-417/even-without-trimmings-chicago-style-hot-dog-in-league-of-its-own.html | doi = | accessdate = 2012-07-17}}</ref> Several popular hot dog stands serve a simpler version: a steamed natural-casing dog with only mustard, onions, plain relish and sport peppers, wrapped up with hand-cut french fries,<ref name="eat" /> while the historic Superdawg drive-ins notably substitute a pickled tomato for fresh. Many vendors, including Portillo's, offer a Chicago-style dog with cheese sauce, known as a cheese-dog.

Preparation

Chicago-style hot dogs are cooked in hot water or steamed before adding the toppings.<ref name="eat" /><ref name="Fluky's recipe"/> A less common style is cooked on a charcoal grill and referred to as a "char-dog". Char-dogs are easily identifiable because very often the ends of the dog are sliced in crisscross fashion before cooking, producing a distinctive cervelat-style "curled-x" shape as the dog cooks.<ref name="Meathead 2009"/> Some hot dog stands, such as the Wieners Circle,<ref name="Chicagoist 2012"/> only serve char-dogs.<ref>"Five Guys Offers More Than Burgers. Tribune.com. {{Deadlink|date=November 2015}}</ref>

The typical beef hot dog weighs 1/8 of a pound or {{convert|2|oz|g}} and the most traditional type features a natural casing, providing a distinctive "snap" when bitten.<ref name="Toledo Blade"/><ref name="wiener">{{cite web| last = Zeldes | first = Leah A. | authorlink = | title = Know your wiener! | work = Dining Chicago | publisher = Chicago's Restaurant & Entertainment Guide, Inc. | date = 2010-07-08 | url = http://www.diningchicago.com/blog/2010/07/08/know-your-wiener/ | doi = | accessdate = 2010-07-31}}</ref>

The buns are a high-gluten variety made to hold up to steam warming, typically the S. Rosen's Mary Ann brand from Alpha Baking Company.<ref name="buns" />
