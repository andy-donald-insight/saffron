Raisin bread is a type of bread made with raisins and flavored with cinnamon. It is "usually a white flour or egg dough bread".<ref name="Bricklin">{{cite book|editor=Mark Bricklin|title=Prevention Magazine's Nutrition Advisor: The Ultimate Guide to the Health-Boosting and Health-Harming Factors in Your Diet|year=1994|publisher=Rodale|isbn=978-0-87596-225-2|page=80}}</ref> Aside from white flour, raisin bread is also made with other flours, such as oat flour or whole wheat flour. Some recipes include honey, brown sugar, eggs, or butter.<ref>{{cite book|author1=Mark Bricklin|author2=The Editors of Prevention Magazine|title=Prevention Magazine's Nutrition Advisor: The Ultimate Guide to the Health-Boosting and Health-Harming Factors in Your Diet|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=iPFokL0MuswC&pg=PA80|date=15 August 1994|publisher=Rodale|isbn=978-0-87596-225-2|page=80}}</ref> Variations of the recipe include the addition of walnuts,<ref>{{cite news|title=Delia skims the goalpost|newspaper=The Independent on Sunday|date=25 June 2000}}</ref> hazelnuts,<ref>{{cite news|last=Miers|first=Thomasina|title=Party season's big dippers|newspaper=The Times|date=15 December 2007}}</ref> pecans<ref>{{cite news|last=Richardson|first=Belinda|title='We could be in the lounge bar of an ocean-going liner'|newspaper=The Daily Telegraph|date=25 June 2005}}</ref> or, for a dessert, rum or whisky.<ref>{{cite news|title=10 top spots near the shops|newspaper=The Times|date=15 December 2007}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last=Ferrier|first=Clare|title=The Royal Oak, Brookland|newspaper=The Daily Telegraph|date=13 September 2008}}</ref>

Raisin bread is eaten in many different forms, including being served toasted for breakfast ("raisin toast") or made into sandwiches.<ref name="Hensperger">{{cite book|last=Hensperger|first=Beth|title=The Bread Lover's Bread Machine Cookbook|year= 2000|publisher=Harvard Common Press|isbn=978-1-55832-156-4|page=449}}</ref> Some restaurants serve raisin bread with their cheeseboards.<ref>{{cite news|last=Mclean|first=Neil|title=If this is a diet, count me in|newspaper=The Sunday Times|date=27 June 2004}}</ref>

History

Its invention has been attributed to Henry David Thoreau<ref>Dolis, J. (2005) Tracking Thoreau: double-crossing nature and technology p.32. Fairleigh Dickinson Univ Press ISBN 0-8386-4045-1 Retrieved January 2012</ref>{{#tag:ref|Walter Harding wrote in his biography of Henry Thoreau that the man had created raisin bread. Author Ken Jennings writes: "It seems the eminent Professor Harding was taken in by, of all things, a story in a 1943 Ladies' Home Journal article, which got its delicious, raisiny facts from a longstanding legend in Thoreau's hometown of Concord, Massachusetts... Ultimately Harding recanted his claims in a 1990 Thoreau Society Bulletin titled 'Thoreau and Raisin Bread.{{'"}}<ref name="Jennings2006" />|group="nb"}} in Concord, Massachusetts lore, but there have been published recipes for bread with raisins since 1671.<ref name="Jennings2006">{{cite book|author=Ken Jennings|title=Brainiac: Adventures in the Curious, Competitive, Compulsive World of Trivia Buffs|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=tXi_rfgUWCcC&pg=PT168|date=September 12, 2006|publisher=Random House Publishing Group|isbn=978-1-58836-552-1|page=168}}</ref> Since the 15th century, breads made with raisins were made in Europe. In Germany stollen was a Christmas bread. Kulich was an Easter bread made in Russia and panettone was made in Italy.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.sunmaid.com/history-of-raisins-and-dried-fruit.html | title=History of Raisins and Dried Fruit | publisher=Sun Maid | accessdate=November 26, 2013}}</ref> The earliest citation for "raisin bread" in the Oxford English Dictionary is dated to an 1845 article in Blackwood's Edinburgh Magazine.<ref name=OED>{{cite web|title=raisin, n.|url=http://www.oed.com/view/Entry/157621|work=Oxford English Dictionary|publisher=Oxford University Press|accessdate=26 November 2013}}</ref> In England, raisin bread became a common element of high tea from the second half of the 19th century.<ref>{{cite news|last=Wilson|first=Bee|title=There's nothing 'high' about high tea|newspaper=The Times|date=9 March 2002}}</ref> In the 1920s, raisin bread was advertised as "The Bread Of Iron", due to the high iron content of the raisins.<ref>{{cite news|title=The Bread of Iron (advertisement)|url=http://oregonnews.uoregon.edu/lccn/sn83045782/1921-09-18/ed-1/seq-56.pdf|accessdate= November 26, 2013|newspaper=The Sunday Oregonian|date=September 18, 1921|location=Portland, Oregon}}</ref> The bread became increasingly popular among English bakers in the 1960s.<ref>{{cite news|last=Woodland|first=John|title=Price blow to raisin traders in UK|newspaper=The Times|date=20 October 1967}}</ref>

Varieties

A loaf of raisin challa

European versions of raisin bread include the Estonian "kringel"<ref>{{cite news|last=Brûlé|first=Tyler|title=Things to do, places to go|newspaper=The Financial Times|date=27 December 2008}}</ref> and the Slovakian "vianocka".<ref>{{cite news|last=Gill|first=Jaime|title=A winter affair|newspaper=The Guardian|date=22 November 2008}}</ref> A similar food is raisin challah, a traditional Jewish food for Shabbat and holidays.<ref name="GlazerGlazer2011">{{cite book|author1=Phyllis Glazer|author2=Miriyam Glazer|title=The Essential Book of Jewish Festival Cooking|date=March 29, 2011|publisher=HarperCollins|isbn=978-0-06-204121-0|page=127}}</ref> It has been suggested that Garibaldi biscuits were based on a raisin bread that was eaten by the troops of Italian general Giuseppe Garibaldi.<ref name=Ind0707>{{cite news|last=Vallely|first=Paul|title=Garibaldi: The First Global Action Hero|newspaper=The Independent|date=30 June 2007}}</ref>

Production

The United States Code of Federal Regulations specifies standards that raisin bread produced in the country must meet. This includes a requirement for the weight of the raisins to be equal to 50% of the weight of flour used.<ref name=GPO>{{cite web|title=Section 136.160 - Raisin bread, rolls, and buns|url=http://www.gpo.gov/fdsys/pkg/CFR-2005-title21-vol2/xml/CFR-2005-title21-vol2-sec136-160.xml|work=Code of Federal Regulations|accessdate=26 November 2013|date=1 April 2005}}</ref> Raisin bread is one of five types of bread for which federal standards have been outlined.<ref>{{cite book|title=Kiplinger's Personal Finance|date=May 1982|publisher=Kiplinger Washington Editors, Inc.|page=40|chapter=Taking the wraps off bread|issn=15289729}}</ref>

In cosmology

The ways in which individual raisins move during rising and baking of the bread is often used as an analogy to explain the expansion of the universe.<ref>{{cite web|title=What does it mean when they say the universe is expanding?|url=http://www.loc.gov/rr/scitech/mysteries/universe.html|work=Everyday Mysteries: Fun Science Facts from the Library of Congress|publisher=The Library of Congress|accessdate=November 26, 2013|date=August 23, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|title=Tests of Big Bang: Expansion|url=http://map.gsfc.nasa.gov/universe/bb_tests_exp.html|work=WMAP's Universe|publisher=National Aeronautics and Space Administration|accessdate=November 26, 2013|author=NASA/WMAP Science Team|date=March 25, 2013}}</ref>
