Salt, pepper, and sugar are commonly placed on Western restaurant tables.

A condiment is a spice, sauce, or other food preparation that is added to food to impart a particular flavor, to enhance its flavor,<ref>Merriam-Webster: Definition of condiment</ref> or in some cultures, to complement the dish. The term originally described pickled or preserved foods, but has shifted meaning over time.<ref>Smith, pp. 144–146</ref>

Many condiments are available packaged in single-serving packets, like mustard or ketchup, particularly when supplied with take-out or fast-food meals. They are usually applied by the diner, but are sometimes added prior to serving; for example, in a sandwich made with ketchup, mustard or mayonnaise. Some condiments are used during cooking to add flavor or texture to the food; barbecue sauce, teriyaki sauce, soy sauce, and marmite are examples.

The term condiment comes from the Latin condimentum, meaning "spice, seasoning, sauce" and from the Latin condere, meaning "preserve, pickle, season".<ref name="Nealon">Nealon</ref>

Definition

Tray of condiments and spices

The exact definition of what is and is not a condiment varies. Some definitions include spice and herbs, including salt and pepper,<ref>Collins: Definition Condiment</ref> using the term interchangeably with seasoning.<ref name="Farrell, p. 291">Farrell, p. 291</ref> Others restrict the definition to including only "prepared food compound[s], containing one or more spices", which are added to food after the cooking process, such as mustard, ketchup or mint sauce.<ref name="Farrell, p. 291"/>

History

Condiments were known in Ancient Rome, Ancient India, Ancient Greece and Ancient China, and were often used to improve the taste of spoiling food; before food preservation techniques were widespread, pungent spices and condiments were used to make the food more palatable.<ref>Farrell, p. 297</ref> The Romans made the condiments garum and liquamen by {{clarify span|crushing and fermenting in salt|reason=crushing and fermenting what?|date=May 2015}} with the meat of various fish, leading to a flourishing condiment industry.<ref name="Nealon"/> Apicius, a cookbook based on 4th and 5th century cuisine, contains a section based solely on condiments.<ref name="Nealon"/>

Condiment market in the United States

The condiment market refers to the marketing and consumer purchase of condiments.

In the United States, condiment market was valued at USD 5.6 billion in 2010 and is estimated to grow to USD 7 billion by 2015.<ref name="BB1">{{cite web|url=http://www.businessweek.com/magazine/content/10_42/b4199096744538.htm|title=Spreading the Love|last=Sax|first=David|date=October 7, 2010<!--, 5:00PM EST-->|work=Bloomberg Businessweek|accessdate=9 October 2010}}</ref> The condiment market is the second largest in specialty foods behind that of cheese.<ref name="BB1"/>
