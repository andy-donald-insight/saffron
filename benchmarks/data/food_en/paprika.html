Paprika is a spice made from air-dried fruits of the chili pepper family of the species Capsicum annuum. Although paprika is often associated with Hungarian cuisine, the chilies from which it is made are native to the New World, introduced to the Old World from the Americas. The seasoning is also used to add color and flavor to many types of dishes.

The trade in paprika expanded from Iberia to Africa and Asia,<ref>Peppers: The Domesticated Capsicums - Google Books. Books.google.co.uk. Retrieved on September 20, 2013.</ref> and ultimately reached Central Europe through the Balkans, then under Ottoman rule, which explains the Hungarian origin of the modern English term. In Spanish, paprika has been known as pimentón since the 16th century, when it became a typical ingredient of the western region of Extremadura.<ref name="UTexas">Peppers: The Domesticated Capsicums, pp. 5 and 73</ref> Despite its presence in Central Europe since the beginning of Ottoman conquests, it did not become popular in Hungary until the late 19th century.<ref name="glutton's glossary">The Glutton's Glossary: A Dictionary of Food and Drink Terms, p. 205</ref>

Paprika can range from mild to hot, and flavors also vary from country to country, but almost all plants grown produce the sweet variety.<ref name="memoir"/> Sweet paprika is mostly composed of the pericarp, with more than half of the seeds removed, whereas hot paprika contains some seeds, placentas, calyces, and stalks.<ref name="UTexas"/>

Etymology and history

The plant that makes the Hungarian version of the spice was grown from 1529 by the Turks at Buda<ref name="etymdict">{{cite web|url=http://www.etymonline.com/index.php?term=paprika |title=Online Etymology Dictionary |publisher=Etymonline.com |date= |accessdate=November 4, 2011}}</ref> (now part of the capital of Hungary, Budapest). The first recorded use of the word "paprika" in English is from 1896,<ref name="etymdict"/> although an earlier reference to Turkish paprika was published in 1831.<ref name="Encyclopaedia">{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=0e0IAAAAQAAJ&pg=PA476&dq=%22paprika%22&hl=en&sa=X&ved=0CDsQ6AEwBGoVChMIwLnOjtvKyAIVxBo-Ch1v7A0z#v=onepage&q=%22paprika%22&f=false|title=Encyclopedia Americana, p. 476|author=Lieber F|publisher=Oxford University|date=1831}}</ref> The word derives from the Hungarian or Turkish word paprika, a diminutive of the Serbian and Croatian word papar (meaning "pepper"),<ref>A Magyar Nyelv Történeti-Etimológiai Szótára (Historical-Etymological Dictionary of the Hungarian Language) (1976, Budapest, Akadémiai Kiadó), 3:93. "paprika 1748...&nbsp;Szerb-horvát eredetű&nbsp;... Ez a szb.-hv. pàpar 'bors'&nbsp;..." (paprika 1748&nbsp;... Serbo-Croatian originally&nbsp;... This is the Serbo-Croatian pàpar 'pepper'&nbsp;... [followed by an explanation of the Hungarian suffix -ka]).</ref> which in turn came from the Latin piper or modern Greek piperi.<ref name="etymdict"/> Paprika and similar words, peperke, piperke, and paparka, are used in various Slavic languages in the Balkans for bell peppers.<ref name="UTexas"/>

The word "paprika" entered a large number of languages, in many cases probably via German.<ref name="SpicePages">{{cite web|url=http://gernot-katzers-spice-pages.com/engl/Caps_ann.html|title=Paprika (Capsicum annuum L.)|author=Katzer, Gernot|date=May 27, 2008|accessdate=December 1, 2012}}</ref> European languages use a similar word, while examples from other languages include the Japanese papurika.<ref name="SpicePages" />

Central European paprika was hot until the 1920s when a Szeged breeder found one plant that produced sweet fruit, then grafted it onto other plants.<ref name="memoir">Paprika: A Spicy Memoir from Hungary, p. 202</ref>

Usage

Paprika pepper farmer in Tanzania

Paprika is produced in various places including Hungary, Serbia, Spain and some regions of the United States.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.foodreference.com/html/artpaprika.html |title=Paprika&nbsp;— Food Facts |publisher=Food Reference |date= |accessdate=November 4, 2011}}</ref> It is used as an ingredient in a broad variety of dishes throughout the world. It is principally used to season and color rices, stews, and soups, such as goulash, and in the preparation of sausages as an ingredient mixed with meats and other spices. In the United States, paprika is frequently sprinkled on foods as a garnish, but the flavor is more effectively produced by heating it gently in oil.<ref>{{cite web|last=Hyde |first=Brenda |url=http://oldfashionedliving.com/paprika.html |title=Classic Spice Blends: Paprika |publisher=Oldfashionedliving.com |date= |accessdate=November 4, 2011}}</ref>

Spanish paprika (pimentón) is available in three versions&nbsp;— mild (pimentón dulce), moderately spicy (pimentón agridulce), and very spicy (pimentón picante). Some Spanish paprika, including pimentón de la Vera has a distinct smoky flavor and aroma, as it is dried by smoking, typically using oak wood.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://spanishfood.about.com/od/essentialingredients/a/paprika.htm |title=Spanish Paprika&nbsp;— Pimentón |publisher=Spanishfood.about.com |date=March 2, 2011 |accessdate=November 4, 2011}}</ref>

Hungary is a major source of commonly used paprika. It is available in grades (in America) ranging as:

  • Special quality (különleges) the mildest, very sweet with a deep bright red color
  • Delicate (csípősmentes csemege)&nbsp;– color from light to dark red, a mild paprika with a rich flavor
  • Exquisite delicate (csemegepaprika)&nbsp;– similar to delicate, but more pungent
  • Pungent exquisite delicate (csípős csemege, pikáns)&nbsp;– an even more pungent version of delicate
  • Rose (rózsa)&nbsp;– pale red in color with strong aroma and mild pungency
  • Noble sweet (Édesnemes)&nbsp;– the most commonly exported paprika; bright red and slightly pungent
  • Half-sweet (félédes)&nbsp;– a blend of mild and pungent paprikas; medium pungency
  • Strong (erős)&nbsp;– light brown in color, the hottest paprika<ref>{{cite web|author=Tom |url=http://blog.thespicehouse.com/grades-of-paprika/ |title=Grades of Paprika &#124; The Spice House Blog |publisher=|date=October 31, 2008 |accessdate=November 4, 2011}}</ref>

Hungarian paprika is often specified in recipes, because it is unique. It is bright red and said to be sweeter than the same paprika grown in other soils and climates. Other paprika types have their unique niche, so it is important to use the type of paprika specified in recipes (if specified), unless it is used in small quantities. In "paprikash" (paprika gravy, a combination of broth, paprika, and sour cream), paprika is used by the tablespoonful. In such instances, Hungarian paprika is preferred.

"The Hungarian varieties are more robust and considered superior. The Spanish varieties are sweeter and milder. Most tables in Hungary are set with salt and hot paprika (not black pepper) shakers. One particular variety, the 'rose', known for its sweet aroma and brilliant color, is prized above all others. Hungarian agricultural authorities fiercely guard their plants and seeds and twice as much acreage is devoted to peppers as any other crop."<ref>Chefs Corner | Red Cat Restaurants | New York City. Red Cat Restaurants. Retrieved on September 20, 2013.</ref>

"Due to the favourable climate and geographical conditions, Hungarian paprika has a bright red colour and a distinctive rich flavour that allowed Hungary to become one of the leading paprika producers in the world&nbsp;... Kalocsa and Szeged in the southern part of Hungary are the heart of paprika production in Hungary. These regions have the highest amount of sunny hours a year and paprika plants need lots of sunshine to get ripe and sweet."<ref>Hungarian Paprika-Facts, History & Recipes. Budapest-tourist-guide.com. Retrieved on September 20, 2013.</ref>

The Netherlands is a major production and distribution source of paprika, as well, especially grown in greenhouses.

In Moroccan cuisine, paprika (tahmira) is usually found augmented by the addition of a small amount of olive oil blended into it.

Paprika can also be used with henna to bring a reddish tint to hair when coloring it. Paprika powder can be added to henna powder when prepared at home.

The color of paprika is primarily due to zeaxanthin.

Nutrition

According to information published by the United States Department of Agriculture, 1 tbsp (6.8 g) of paprika has this nutritional content:<ref>NDL/FNIC Food Composition Database Home Page. Nal.usda.gov. Retrieved on September 20, 2013.</ref>

  • Calories: 19
  • Fat: 0.88 g
  • Carbohydrates: 3.67 g
  • Fiber: 2.4 g
  • Protein: 0.96 g
