Michetta {{IPA-it|miˈketta|}} (also known as rosetta {{IPA-it|roˈzetta|}} "small rose") is an Italian white bread, recognizable from its bulged shape.

Other similarly prepared types of Italian breads include the maggiolino {{IPA-it|maddʒoˈliːno|}} ("cockchafer") and tartaruga {{IPA-it|tartaˈruːɡa|}} ("turtle").

History

Michetta originated in Lombardy, northern Italy, during the Austrian rule. Functionaries of the Austrian empire introduced a number of food products, including the Kaisersemmel, a type of bread with segments resembling a small rose.

However, due to the higher humidity, the micheta produced in Milan were less fragrant and became harder in a single day.{{citation needed|date=October 2014}} To solve the problem, Lombard bakers removed the internal crumb{{what?|date=October 2014}} and obtained a softer version of the original, which also helped it last longer. The new type of bread was called michetta, from the Lombard version of Kaisersemmel, micca,<ref>{{cite book|first=Francesco |last=Cherubini|title=Vocabolario milanese italiano|year= 1840|volume=III}}</ref> a term originally meaning "crumb."
