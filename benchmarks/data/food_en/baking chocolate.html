Baking chocolate, also referred to as bitter chocolate,<ref name="Mushet Table Caruso 2008 p. 39"/> cooking chocolate<ref name="Risley 2009 p. 370"/> and unsweetened chocolate,<ref name="Patrick-Goudreau 2007 p. 241"/> is a type of chocolate that is prepared or manufactured for baking.<ref name="Mushet Table Caruso 2008 p. 39"/> It is used as an ingredient in desserts and in baked goods. It is typically prepared in unsweetened,<ref name="Mushet Table Caruso 2008 p. 39"/> bitter-sweet<ref name="Risley 2009 p. 370"/> semi-sweet<ref name="Gonzalez 1998 p. 2"/> and sweet varieties.<ref name="Gardens 2013 p. 18"/> It may be prepared with chocolate liquor or cocoa solids. Recipes that include unsweetened baking chocolate typically use a significant amount of sugar.<ref name="Gardens 2013 p. 18"/> Bittersweet baking chocolate "must contain 35 percent chocolate liquor or higher."<ref name="Gardens 2013 p. 18"/> Most baking chocolates have at least a 50% cocoa content, with the remaining content usually being "almost all sugar."<ref name="Mushet Table Caruso 2008 p. 39"/> Sweet varieties may be referred to as "sweet baking chocolate" or "sweet chocolate."<ref name="Phillips 2008 p. 162"/> Sweet baking chocolate contains more sugar than bittersweet<ref name="Gardens 2013 p. 18"/> and semi-sweet varieties, and semi-sweet varieties contain more sugar than bittersweet varieties.<ref name="Phillips 2008 p. 162"/> Sweet and semi-sweet baking chocolate is prepared with a chocolate liquor content between 15 and 35 percent.<ref name="Gardens 2013 p. 18"/>

Modern manufactured baking chocolate is typically formed from chocolate liquor into bars<ref name="Mushet Table Caruso 2008 p. 39"/> and chocolate chips. Manufacturers may process the chocolate and then form it into bulk-sized ten-pound bars, which are then sold to confectioners and bakers.<ref name="Risley 2009 p. 370"/> Baking chocolate may be of a lower quality compared to other types of chocolate, and may have part of the cocoa butter replaced with other fats that do not require tempering.<ref name="Gisslen 2012 p. 88"/> This type of baking chocolate may be easier to handle compared to those that have not had their cocoa butter content lowered.<ref name="Gisslen 2012 p. 88"/> Lower quality baking chocolate may not be as flavorful compared to higher-quality chocolate, and may have a different mouthfeel.<ref name="Gisslen 2012 p. 88"/>

Varieties

The table below denotes the four primary varieties of baking chocolate.

Type Content Sources
Unsweetened Contains no sugar, and contains 99% chocolate liquor or cocoa solids <ref name="Mushet Table Caruso 2008 p. 39"/><ref name="Gardens 2013 p. 18"/><ref name="Holmberg Cooking Magazine 2009 p. 4"/>
Bittersweet Usually has less sugar and more chocolate liquor compared to semi-sweet varieties. <ref name="Mushet Table Caruso 2008 p. 39"/><ref name="Phillips 2008 p. 162"/><ref name="Holmberg Cooking Magazine 2009 p. 4"/><ref name="Marcus 2013 p. 367"/>
Semi-sweet Has less sugar than sweet varieties. In Europe, a regulation exists stating that semi-sweet varieties must contain more sugar and less chocolate liquor compared to bittersweet varieties. No such regulation exists in the United States, and due to this, semi-sweet and bittersweet varieties can vary in sweetness and chocolate liquor content. In the U.S., bittersweet varieties are even sometimes sweeter than semi-sweet varieties. <ref name="Mushet Table Caruso 2008 p. 39"/>
Sweet Has the most sugar <ref name="Gardens 2013 p. 18"/>

Manufacturers

Manufacturers of baking chocolate include Baker's Chocolate,<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/> Callebaut, Ghirardelli, Guittard, Lindt, Menier, Scharffen Berger and Valrhona,<ref name="Risley 2009 p. 370"/> among others.

Baker's Chocolate

{{main|Baker's Chocolate (brand)}}

A 1919 advertisement for Baker's Cocoa

Baker's Chocolate is an American brand of baking chocolate<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/> that was first named Walter Baker & Company. It is considered to be the oldest chocolate manufacturer in the United States.<ref name="Sammarco 2011 p. 58"/> The company was established in 1765 in Dorchester, Massachusetts,<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/><ref name="Sammarco 2011 p. 9"/> when a physician named Dr. James Baker met John Hannon. The original brand name was "Hannon’s Best Chocolate," which was "manufactured for almost fifteen years" and was sold with a money-back guarantee if the consumer was unsatisfied with the product.<ref name="Sammarco 2011 p. 9"/> The name was changed in 1780 after Hannon's wife, Elizabeth Gore Hannon, sold the company to Baker in 1780, after Hannon never returned from a 1779 sailing trip to the West Indies to purchase cocoa beans.<ref name="Sammarco 2011 p. 9"/> At the time, it was rumored that Hannon intended to leave his wife, and thus deserted her.<ref name="Sammarco 2011 p. 9"/> Original versions of the brand were not prepared for baking, and before 1865, the company purveyed three grades of drinking chocolate, which were "Best Chocolate", "Common Chocolate" and "Inferior Chocolate".<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/> The inferior grade was mostly sold to West Indian and American slaves.<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/> Around the mid 1800s, the company began to significantly expand, and later significantly increased its advertising in American newspapers.<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/> In 1896, Baker's Chocolate was advertising in around 8,000 newspapers in the United States.<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/> The company also advertised using signage and cards in grocery stores, in novels, in street cars and using billboards.<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/> Around the late 1800s, the company began promoting the notion of using chocolate as an ingredient in desserts and for baking.<ref name="Goldstein Mintz Krondl Rath 2015 p. 45"/>
