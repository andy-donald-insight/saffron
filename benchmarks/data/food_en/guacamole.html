Guacamole ({{IPA-es|wakaˈmole|lang}}; <small>or</small> {{IPA-es|ɡwakaˈmole||GuacamolePronunciation.ogg}}; can informally be referred to as "guac" in North America <ref name="Oxford Dictionary">{{cite web | title = Oxford Dictionary |url = http://www.oxforddictionaries.com/us/definition/american_english/guac}}</ref>) is an avocado-based dip or salad first created by the Aztecs in what is now Mexico.<ref name="zeldes">{{cite web | last = Zeldes | first = Leah A. | authorlink = | title = Eat this! Guacamole, a singing sauce, on its day | work = Dining Chicago | publisher = Chicago's Restaurant & Entertainment Guide, Inc. | date = November 4, 2009 | url = http://blog.diningchicago.com/2009/11/04/eat-this-guacamole-a-singing-sauce-on-its-day/ | doi = | accessdate = November 5, 2009 }}</ref> In addition to its use in modern Mexican cuisine it has also become part of American cuisine as a dip, condiment and salad ingredient.<ref name="beard">{{cite book|last1=Beard|first1=James|authorlink1=James Beard|last2=Bittman|first2=Mark |title=Beard on Food: The Best Recipes and Kitchen Wisdom from the Dean of American Cooking|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=s9Q7PTP-GdgC&pg=PA86|accessdate=March 14, 2012|date=September 4, 2007|publisher=Bloomsbury Publishing USA|isbn=978-1-59691-446-9|pages=86–87}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|last=Smith|first=Andrew F. |title=The Oxford companion to American food and drink|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=AoWlCmNDA3QC&pg=PT171 |accessdate=March 14, 2012 |date=May 1, 2007 |publisher=Oxford University Press |isbn=978-0-19-530796-2 |pages=144–146}}</ref>

Ingredients

Guacamole dip is traditionally made by mashing ripe avocados and sea salt with a molcajete (mortar and pestle). Some recipes call for tomato, onion, garlic, lemon or lime juice, chili or cayenne pepper, cilantro or basil, jalapeño, and/or additional seasonings. Some recipes call for sour cream as the main ingredient. Guacamole is also a word for avocado in some areas in Latin America.{{citation needed|date=September 2015}}

On July 2, 2013, the New York Times published a guacamole recipe that included the addition of English peas.<ref>CLARK, MELISSA (July 2, 2013), "Green Pea Guacamole", The New York Times. Retrieved November 6, 2015.</ref> Two years later, on July 1, 2015, the newspaper posted a link to the article on Twitter account with the caption, "Add green peas to your guacamole. Trust us."<ref>https://twitter.com/nytimes/status/616303020574441472?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw</ref&gt; The post sparked overwhelmingly negative feedback from their readers and followers, which prompted the media to pick-up on the story,<ref>Lo Dico, Joy (July 2, 2015), "Mind your peas and Qs over guacamole." Evening Standard. :16</ref> calling the incident "Guacamolegate."<ref name=zillman>Zillman, Claire (July 14, 2015). "Adding peas to guacamole is actually terrible for the environment," Fortune p. 1.</ref> Even President Barack Obama weighed in, tweeting that—while he respected the newspaper—peas didn't belong in guacamole.<ref>https://twitter.com/POTUS/status/616338528138608640?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw</ref&gt;

Due to the presence of polyphenol oxidase in the cells of avocado, exposure to the oxygen in the air causes an enzymatic reaction develops melanoidin pigment, turning the sauce brown.<ref name=hartel1>Hartel, 2009, p. 43</ref>This result is generally considered unappetizing, and there are several methods (some anecdotal) that are used to counter this effect.<ref name=hartel1/>

History

Aztecs made Guacamole dip by at least the 16th century.<ref name="zeldes" /> A Spanish-English pronunciation guide from 1900 lists guacamole as a "salad of alligator pear".<ref>Edward Gray, A New Pronouncing Dictionary of the Spanish and English Languages, Part 1 (D. Appleton, 1900) 349.</ref>

Later marketing tried to create a "luau" or Pacific Island image of the avocado in the 1960s, and a Spanish or Mediterranean image in the 1970s.{{citation needed|date=July 2015}} Guacamole has pushed avocado sales in the US to 30 million pounds on two days a year: Super Bowl Sunday and Cinco de Mayo.<ref>{{cite book |last= Charles |first= Jeffrey |editor1-last= Belasco |editor1-first= Warren |editor2-last= Scranton |editor2-first= Philip |title= Food nations: selling taste in consumer societies |url= http://books.google.com/books?id=p9wPoDHQR-IC |accessdate= September 20, 2011 |year= 2002 |publisher= Routledge |isbn= 0-415-93077-4 |pages= 131–154 |chapter= 8. Searching for gold in Guacamole: California growers market the avocado, 1910–1994 }}</ref>{{citation needed|date=July 2015}}{{dubious|date=July 2015}}

Etymology and pronunciation

The name comes from an Aztec dialect via Nahuatl āhuacamolli {{IPA-nah|aːwakaˈmolːi|}}, which literally translates to "avocado sauce", from āhuacatl {{IPA-nah|aːˈwakat͡ɬ|}} ("avocado") + molli {{IPA-nah|ˈmolːi|}} ("sauce", literally "concoction").<ref name="zeldes" /> In Mexican Spanish it is pronounced {{IPA-es|wakaˈmole|}}, in American English it is sometimes pronounced {{IPAc-en|ɡ|w|ɑː|k|ə|ˈ|m|oʊ|l|iː}}, and in British English sometimes {{IPAc-en|ˌ|w|æ|k|ə|ˈ|m|oʊ|l|iː}}. The name of the Guatemalan version has the final "e" omitted ({{IPA-es|wakaˈmol|lang}}).{{citation needed|date=September 2015}} Early recipes from the California Avocado Advisory Board (Calavo), published in the 1940s, were accompanied with a pronunciation suggestion: "say huakamole".{{citation needed|date=September 2015}}

Similar foods

Mantequilla de pobre

Mantequilla de pobre ({{Language with name/for||Spanish|"poor-man's butter"}}) is a mixture of avocado, tomato, oil, and citrus juice. Despite its name, it predates the arrival of dairy cattle in the Americas, and thus was not originally made as a butter substitute.<ref name="beard" />

Guacsause

Thinner and more acidic,<ref>{{cite news |title= Caracas Calling |url= http://www.nypress.com/article-9759-caracas-calling.html |newspaper= New York Press |publisher = Manhattan Media |date = July 13, 2004 |accessdate= March 4, 2010}}</ref> or thick and chunky,<ref>{{cite news |title= Guasacaca&nbsp;— Venezuelan-style Guacamole |url= http://southamericanfood.about.com/b/2009/07/02/guasacaca-venezuelan-style-guacamole.htm |website= About.com |date = July 2, 2009 |accessdate= October 6, 2013}}</ref> guasacaca is a Venezuelan avocado-based sauce; it is made with vinegar,<ref>{{cite journal |last= Serpa |first= Diego |year= 1968 |title= Avocado Culture in Venezuela |journal= California Avocado Society 1968 Yearbook |volume= 52 |pages= 153–168 |issn= 0096-5960|url= http://www.avocadosource.com/CAS_Yearbooks/CAS_52_1968/CAS_1968_PG_153-168.pdf |format= PDF |accessdate= March 4, 2010}}</ref> and is served over parrillas (grilled food), arepas, empanadas, and various other dishes. It is common to make the guasacaca with a little hot sauce instead of jalapeño, but like a guacamole, it is not usually served as a hot sauce itself.

Salat avocado

Salat avocado ({{lang-he|סלט אבוקדו}}) is a rural Israeli avocado salad, with lemon juice and chopped scallions (spring onions) with salt and black pepper added, was introduced by farmers who planted avocado trees on the coastal plain in the 1920s. Avocados have since become a winter delicacy and are cut into salads as well as being spread on bread today also with pita and flat bread.<ref>Ansky, pg. 50</ref> usually eaten in the villages of the coastal plain. It is also common today to add cumin before adding the lemon juice as well as feta cheese or safed cheese.
