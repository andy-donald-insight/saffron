Harissa sauce

Harissa ({{lang-ar|هريسة}} | harîsa - Word Origin : from Arabic) is a North African hot chili pepper paste, the main ingredients of which are roasted red peppers, serrano peppers and other hot chili peppers and spices and herbs such as garlic paste, coriander seed, or caraway as well as some vegetable or olive oil for preservation. It is most closely associated with Tunisia, Libya and Algeria<ref name="Morse">{{cite book

History and details

As with the European cuisine, chili peppers were imported into Maghrebian cuisine via the Columbian Exchange,<ref name="Morse"/> presumably during the Spanish occupation of Tunisia between 1535 and 1574.<ref name="Marks2008">{{cite book|author=Gil Marks|title=Olive Trees and Honey: A Treasury of Vegetarian Recipes from Jewish Communities Around the World|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=Lb3MVYVp_9sC&pg=PT797|year=2008|publisher=Wiley|isbn=0-544-18750-4||location="Northwest African Chili Paste (Harissa)"}}</ref> Recipes for harissa vary according to the household and region. Variations can include the addition of cumin, red peppers, garlic, coriander, and lemon juice. In Saharan regions, harissa can have a smoky flavor.{{citation needed|date=June 2014}} Prepared harissa is also sold in jars, cans, bottles, tubes, plastic bags and other containers.

Harissa is sometimes described as "Tunisia's main condiment",<ref name="Civitello2011">{{cite book|author=Linda Civitello|title=Cuisine and Culture: A History of Food and People|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=KwtE4v_qS4EC&pg=PT244|year=2011|publisher=John Wiley & Sons|isbn=978-1-118-09875-2|page=244}}</ref> even "the national condiment of Tunisia",<ref name="Harris1998">{{cite book|author=Jessica B. Harris|title=The Africa Cookbook: Tastes of a Continent|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=7rmIDVcoHNoC&pg=PA137|year=1998|publisher=Simon and Schuster|isbn=978-0-684-80275-6|page=137}}</ref> or at least as "the hallmark of Tunisia's fish and meat dishes".<ref name="Cavendish2006">{{cite book|author=Marshall Cavendish|title=World and Its Peoples|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=j894miuOqc4C&pg=PA1282|year=2006|publisher=Marshall Cavendish|isbn=978-0-7614-7571-2|page=1282}}</ref> In Tunisia, harissa is used as an ingredient in a meat (goat or lamb) or fish stew with vegetables, and as a flavoring for couscous. It is also used for lablabi, a chickpea soup usually eaten for breakfast. In Algeria, harissa is commonly added to soups, stews, and couscous.<ref name="Edelstein2010">{{cite book|author=Sari Edelstein|title=Food, Cuisine, and Cultural Competency for Culinary, Hospitality, and Nutrition Professionals|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=Tl9Pcq25s8AC&pg=PA345|year=2010|publisher=Jones & Bartlett Publishers|isbn=978-1-4496-5968-4|page=345}}</ref> In some{{which|date=June 2014}} European countries it is used sometimes as a breakfast spread for tartines or rolls.{{citation needed|date=June 2014}} Harissa paste can also be used as a rub for meat<ref>{{cite web

Tunisia is the biggest exporter of pre-made harissa.<ref>Tunisian Harissa Export cepex.nat.tn {{dead link|date=June 2014}}</ref> In 2006, the Tunisian production of harissa was 22,000 tonnes, incorporating about 40,000 tonnes of peppers.<ref>{{cite book|author=Oxford Business Group|title=The Report: Tunisia 2008|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=fRa1SlekJHUC&pg=PA195|publisher=Oxford Business Group|isbn=978-1-902339-93-1|page=195}}</ref> Tunisian harissa is often made with chilis grown around Nabeul and Gabès, which are relatively mild, scoring 40,000-50,000 on the Scoville scale.<ref name="WheelerClammer2010">{{cite book|author1=Donna Wheeler|author2=Paul Clammer|author3=Emilie Filou|title=Tunisia|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=7RO1b2LvcisC&pg=PA53|year=2010|publisher=Lonely Planet|isbn=978-1-74179-001-6|page=53}}</ref> Another significant producer is Algeria's Annaba Province,<ref name="Group2008">{{cite book|author=Oxford Business Group|title=The Report: Algeria 2008|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=zPz9FHXJVLUC&pg=PA230|year=2008|publisher=Oxford Business Group|isbn=978-1-902339-09-2|page=230}}</ref> which is also a significant consumer.<ref name="Albala2011">{{cite book|author=Ken Albala|title=Food Cultures of the World Encyclopedia|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=NTo6c_PJWRgC&pg=PA7|year=2011|publisher=ABC-CLIO|isbn=978-0-313-37626-9|page=7}}</ref> According to cookbook author Martha Rose Shulman, pre-made harissa tastes rather different from that served in Tunisian and expat restaurants.<ref name="Shulman2014">{{cite book|author=Martha Rose Shulman|title=The Simple Art of Vegetarian Cooking: Templates and Lessons for Making Delicious Meatless Meals Every Day|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=EMdWAwAAQBAJ&pg=PA254|year=2014|publisher=Rodale|isbn=978-1-62336-130-3|page=254}}</ref>

The exposure of the population from a young age to harissa is blamed<ref>{{cite journal |vauthors=Bouvier G, Hergenhahn M, Polack A, Bornkamm GW, de Thé G, Bartsch H |title=Characterization of macromolecular lignins as Epstein-Barr virus inducer in foodstuff associated with nasopharyngeal carcinoma risk |journal=Carcinogenesis |volume=16 |issue=8 |pages=1879–85 |year=1995 |pmid=7634418 |url=http://carcin.oxfordjournals.org/cgi/pmidlookup?view=long&pmid=7634418 }}</ref> for the increased prevalence of nasopharynx cancer (NPC) in Tunisia.<ref name="Abdesslam2009">{{cite book|author=Boutayeb Abdesslam|title=Social Determinants, Health Equity and Human Development|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=kzRLW7dk_k8C&pg=PA70|year=2009|publisher=Bentham Science Publishers|isbn=978-1-60805-066-6|page=70}}</ref> The exact pathway is unknown, but laboratory experiments have shown that some ingredients in harissa may reactivate the Epstein-Barr virus, a viral cause of NPC.<ref name="Busson2013"> {{cite book|author=Pierre Busson |title=Nasopharyngeal Carcinoma: Keys for Translational Medicine and Biology |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=zW5MAAAAQBAJ&pg=PA30 |year=2013|publisher=Springer|isbn=978-1-4614-5947-7 |page=30 }}</ref>
