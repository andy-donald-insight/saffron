{{Refimprove|date=June 2011}} {{Infobox prepared food | name = New York–style pizza | image = File:NYPizzaPie.jpg|250px | caption = | alternate_name = | country = United States | region = New York City | creator = | course = | type = Pizza | served = | main_ingredient = Pizza dough, tomato sauce, mozzarella | variations = | calories = | other = }} {{pizza}} {{American cuisine}} New York–style pizza is a style of pizza characterized by large hand-tossed thin-crust pies, often sold in wide slices to-go. It has a crust which is crisp along its edge yet soft and pliable enough beneath its toppings to be folded in half to eat.<ref name="Encyclopedia of NYC"/> This style evolved from a type that originated in New York City in the early 1900s, and today refers to the style of pizza eaten in the states of New York, New Jersey, and Connecticut. Regional variations exist throughout the Northeast and elsewhere in the U.S.

History

The first pizzeria in America was founded by Gennaro Lombardi in New York City's Little Italy in 1905.<ref name="LonelyPlanet">{{cite book |title=New York City 7 |last=Otis |first=Ginger Adams |authorlink= |year=2010 |publisher=Lonely Planet |location= |isbn=1741795915 |page=256 |pages= |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=btN9Lh8v3OQC&pg=PA256&dq=New+York-style+pizza&hl=en&sa=X&ei=mLG3UMq2LonVigKogoHADg&ved=0CEkQ6AEwBg#v=onepage&q=New%20York-style%20pizza&f=false |accessdate=November 2012}}</ref> An immigrant pizzaiolo from Naples, he opened a grocery store in 1897 which eight years later was licensed to sell pizza by New York State.<ref name="LonelyPlanet"/> An employee, Antonio Totonno Pero, began making pizza, which sold for five cents a pie. Many people, however, could not afford a whole pie and instead would offer what they could in return for a corresponding sized slice,{{citation needed|date=October 2014}} which was wrapped in paper tied with string. In 1924, Totonno left Lombardi's to open his own pizzeria on Coney Island, called Totonno's.

The original pizzerias in New York used coal brick ovens and baked their pizza with the cheese on the bottom and sauce on top.{{citation needed|date=October 2014}} By 2010 over 400 pizza restaurants existed in New York City, with hundreds more of varied cuisine also offering the dish.<ref name="Encyclopedia of NYC"/>

Characteristics

New York–style pizza is traditionally hand-tossed,<ref name="The Pizza Gourmet"/> consisting in its basic form of a light layer of tomato sauce<ref name="LonelyPlanet"/> and genuine mozzarella cheese; additional toppings are placed atop the cheese.<ref name="The Pizza Gourmet">{{cite book |title=The Pizza Gourmet: Simple Recipes for Spectacular Pizza |last=MacKenzie |first=Shea |authorlink= |year=1995 |publisher=Penguin |location= |isbn=089529656X |page=81 |pages= |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=wDMRCfDkO8AC&pg=PA81&dq=New+York-style+pizza&hl=en&sa=X&ei=mLG3UMq2LonVigKogoHADg&ved=0CEMQ6AEwBA#v=onepage&q=New%20York-style%20pizza&f=false |accessdate=November 2012}}</ref> Pies are large, typically around 18&nbsp;inches (45&nbsp;cm) in diameter, and commonly cut into 8 slices. These large wide slices<ref name="Monday">{{cite book |title=Today Is Monday in New York |last=Downing |first=Johnette |authorlink= |author2=Kadair, Deborah Ousley |year=2011 |publisher=Pelican Publishing |location= |isbn=158980886X |page= |pages=(unlisted) |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=ctxhsDfeV0EC&pg=PT32&dq=New+York-style+pizza&hl=en&sa=X&ei=BMK3UM7TFqGGiQKY64CQAQ&ved=0CFIQ6AEwCTgK |accessdate=November 2012}}</ref> are often eaten as a fast food or "street snack" while folded in half, as their flexibility sometimes makes them unwieldy to eat flat. Eating it folded also allows its oil and any toppings to collect more manageably.

New York–style pizza gets its distinguishing crust from the high-gluten bread flour with which it is made. Minerals present in New York City water are also credited with giving the dough in metro area pies their characteristic flavor.<ref name="Monday"/><ref>Gilbert, Sara. "New York Pizza: is the water the secret?". Slashfood. Weblogs, Inc.September 26, 2005.</ref> Some out-of-state pizza makers even transport the water cross-country for the sake of authenticity.<ref>Cornwell, Rupert. "New York's 'Champagne Tap Water' Under Threat". The Independent UKJuly 21, 2006.</ref><ref>Wayne, Gary. "Mulberry Street Pizzeria". Seeing Stars in Hollywood. 2008.</ref>

Regional variations

New York–style pizza is most prevalent in New York, New Jersey, and Connecticut, but can be found throughout the Northeastern region.<ref name="The Pizza Gourmet"/> Outside this area, many pizzas described as "New York style,"<ref name="Encyclopedia of NYC">{{cite book |title=The Encyclopedia of New York City (2nd edition) |last=Jackson |first=Kenneth T.; (et al.) |authorlink= |year=2010 |publisher=Yale University Press |location= |isbn=0300182570 |page= |pages=(unlisted) |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=lI5ERUmHf3YC&pg=PT4684&dq=New+York-style+pizza&hl=en&sa=X&ei=mLG3UMq2LonVigKogoHADg&ved=0CFIQ6AEwCQ |accessdate=November 2012}}</ref> including those of major pizza chains such as Pizza Hut, generally do not fall within the variations commonly accepted as genuine in its native area.
