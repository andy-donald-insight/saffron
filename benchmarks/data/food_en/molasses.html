Blackstrap molasses

Molasses,<ref>{{cite web |url=https://dictionary.cambridge.org/us/dictionary/british/molasses |title=molasses Definition in the Cambridge English Dictionary |work=cambridge.org}}</ref> or black treacle (British, for human consumption; known as molasses otherwise), is a viscous by-product of the refining of sugarcane or sugar beets into sugar. <!-- Please do not change the following sentence without first checking the cited source. If you disagree with the source, it is your responsibility to find an alternate. -->The word comes from the Proto-Indo-European *mélid". Cognates include Ancient Greek μέλι (méli) (honey), Latin mel, Portuguese melaço.<ref>"Molasses" at Dictionary.com</ref> Molasses varies by amount of sugar and method of extraction, and age of plant.

Sweet sorghum syrup may be colloquially called "sorghum molasses" in the American South.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.npr.org/2012/09/12/160946531/sorghum-travels-from-the-south-to-the-mainstream |title=Sorghum Travels From The South To The Mainstream |last=Rapuano |first=Rina |website=npr.org |date=September 12, 2012 |accessdate=May 22, 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.uky.edu/Ag/CCD/introsheets/swsorghumintro.pdf |title=Sweet Sorghum for Syrup |last=Bitzer |first=Morris |publisher=N.p.: University of Kentucky |year=2002 |format=PDF |accessdate=22 May 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://rcrec-ona.ifas.ufl.edu/pdf/publications/molasses-general-considerations..pdf |title=Molasses - General Considerations |last=Curtin |first=Leo V. |publisher=Institute of Food and Agricultural Sciences and University of Florida, n.d. |accessdate=May 22, 2014 |format=PDF}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.in.gov/isdh/files/Guidance_on_Sorghum_final_3-18-2008pdf_entered_6_2012.pdf |title=Ventilated |date=March 19, 2008 |work=Indiana State Department of Health - Division of Consumer Protection - Food Protection Program Guidance on Sorghum Production, March 19, 2008 (2008): 1-6. |accessdate=May 22, 2014 |format=PDF}}</ref>

Cane molasses

A bottle of molasses

Cane molasses is a common ingredient in baking and cooking.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.brerrabbit.com/cooking-with-molasses/ |title=Cooking with Molasses - Brer Rabbit Molasses Recipes - Easy Baking Recipes |work=Brer Rabbit}}</ref>{{Where|date=May 2014}}

To make molasses, sugar cane is harvested and stripped of leaves. Often the fields of cane are set on fire to burn off the leaves and drive out the snakes that seem to enjoy this habitat. Its juice is extracted usually by cutting, crushing or mashing. The juice is boiled to concentrate it, promoting sugar crystallization. The result of this first boiling is called "first syrup", and it has the highest sugar content. First syrup is usually referred to in the Southern states of the US as "cane syrup", as opposed to molasses. "Second molasses" is created from a second boiling and sugar extraction, and has a slight bitter taste.

Blackstrap molasses

The third boiling of the sugar syrup yields dark, viscous blackstrap molasses, known for its robust flavor.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://spiritfoods.net/health-benefits-of-blackstrap-molasses/ |title=Health Benefits of Blackstrap Molasses |publisher=Spiritfoods |accessdate=6 September 2012}}</ref> The majority of sucrose from the original juice has been crystallised and removed. The calorific content of blackstrap molasses is mostly due to the small remaining sugar content.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.whfoods.com/genpage.php?tname=nutrientprofile&dbid=85 |title=Blackstrap Molasses In-depth nutrient analysis |publisher=The World's Healthiest Foods |accessdate=6 September 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.barryfarm.com/nutri_info/sugars/molasses_bs.html |title=Molasses, Blackstrap |publisher=Barry Farm Foods |accessdate=23 June 2012}}</ref> Unlike highly refined sugars, it contains significant amounts of vitamin B6 and minerals, including calcium, magnesium, iron, and manganese; one tablespoon provides up to 20% of the recommended daily value of each of those nutrients. Blackstrap is also a good source of potassium.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://nutritiondata.self.com/facts/sweets/5574/1 |title=Molasses, blackstrap |publisher=NutritionData |accessdate=27 August 2012}}</ref> Blackstrap molasses has long been sold as a dietary supplement.

Blackstrap molasses is significantly more bitter than "regular" molasses. It is sometimes used in baking. This residual product of sugar refining is used for producing ethanol and as an ingredient in cattle feed and as fertilizer.

The term "black-strap" or "blackstrap" is an Americanism dating from 1875 or before.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://chroniclingamerica.loc.gov/lccn/sn83032041/1875-03-26/ed-1/seq-3/;words=molasses+black-strap |title=Local and Personal |newspaper=The Cambria Freeman (1867-1938) |location=Ebensburg, Pa. |date=March 26, 1875 |page=3 |accessdate=2015-10-24}}</ref> Its first known use is in a book by detective Allan Pinkerton in 1877.<ref>{{cite book |title=The Molly Maguires and the Detectives, 1905 ed. |url=http://books.google.com/books?id=2FgWAAAAYAAJ |last=Pinkerton |first=Allan |authorlink=Allan Pinkerton |year=1877 |publisher=G. W. Dillingham Co. |location=New York |isbn= |page= |accessdate=2009-07-08}}</ref> In North India it is known by Urdu word Raab.

Sugar beet molasses

Molasses made from sugar beets differs from sugarcane molasses. Only the syrup left from the final crystallization stage is called molasses; intermediate syrups are called high green and low green, and these are recycled within the crystallization plant to maximize extraction. Beet molasses is 50% sugar by dry weight, predominantly sucrose, but contains significant amounts of glucose and fructose. Beet molasses are limited in biotin (vitamin H or B<sub>7</sub>) for cell growth; hence, it may be supplemented with a biotin source. The non-sugar content includes many salts, such as calcium, potassium, oxalate, and chloride. It contains betaine and the trisaccharide raffinose. These are as a result of concentration from the original plant material or chemicals in processing, and make it unpalatable to humans. Hence it is mainly used as an additive to animal feed (called "molassed<!--Note: "molasses" is the correct spelling--> sugar beet feed") or as a fermentation feedstock.{{Citation needed|date=August 2010}}

It is possible to extract additional sugar from beet molasses through molasses desugarization. This exploits industrial-scale chromatography to separate sucrose from non-sugar components. The technique is economically viable in trade-protected areas, where the price of sugar is supported above market price. As such, it is practiced in the US<ref>"Chromatographic Separator Optimization" at Amalgamated Research Inc.</ref> and parts of Europe. Molasses is also used for yeast production.

Unsulphured molasses

Many kinds of molasses on the market come branded as unsulphured (using the original British spelling of sulfur). Many foods, including molasses, were treated with sulfur dioxide as a preservative, helping to kill off molds and bacteria. Sulfur dioxide is also used as a bleaching agent, and helped to lighten the color of molasses. Most brands have veered away from sulphured molasses, due to its relatively stable natural shelf life, the off flavor that can arise from using sulfur dioxide, and the fact that sulfur dioxide in high doses can be toxic.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.culinarylore.com/food-science:why-does-my-mollases-say-unsulphured |title=Why Does my Mollases say Unsulphured? Was Sulphur Removed From it? |date=October 8, 2012 |website=CulinaryLore.com |publisher= |last=T |first=Eric |accessdate=August 20, 2015}}</ref>

Other forms

Yomari - rice flour breads filled with chaku

In Middle Eastern cuisine, molasses is produced from carob, grapes, dates, pomegranates, and mulberries. In Nepal it is called chaku<ref>{{cite web |url=http://news.xinhuanet.com/english/photo/2014-01/07/c_133025086.htm |title=Locals prepare molasses for festival in Nepal |accessdate=29 September 2014}}</ref> (Nepal Bhasa: चाकु) and used in the preparation of Newari condiments such as yomari.

Other uses

Food products and additives

Bhapa Pitha, a popular Bangladeshi style rice cake, is often sweetened with molasses.

Molasses can be used:

  • In dark rye breads
  • In some cookies and pies
  • In barbecue sauces
  • In beer styles such as stouts and porters
  • The principal ingredient in the distillation of rum
  • As a humectant in jerky processing
  • An iron supplement
  • An additive in livestock feeds
  • An ingredient in fishing groundbait
  • A source for yeast production
  • The main ingredient in the production of citric acid
  • An additive in tobacco smoked in a hookah, shisha, or narghile (found in the brands The King, Al Fakher Tobacco, Cedars Tobacco, Mazaya, Nakhla, Tangiers, Salloum, and Hookafina Blak){{citation needed|date=July 2015}}

Chemical

  • The carbon source for in situ remediation of chlorinated hydrocarbons
  • Blended with magnesium chloride and used for de-icing
  • A stock for ethanol fermentation to produce an alternative fuel for motor vehicles

Industrial

  • As a minor component of mortar for brickwork<ref>{{cite book |title=A Manual on Lime and Cement, Their Treatment and Use in Construction |last=Heath |first=Arthur Henry |publisher=Mackaye Press |date=1893 |url=https://books.google.co.uk/books?id=JR2fdsniyZsC&q=molasses |accessdate=2015-10-24}}</ref>
  • Mixed with glue to case ink rollers on early printing presses

Horticultural

  • As a soil additive to promote microbial activity<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.bfa.com.au/Portals/0/BFAFiles/AUT05-bioactive-materials.pdf |format=PDF |title=Bioactive materials for sustainable soil management |website=bfa.com.au}}</ref>
