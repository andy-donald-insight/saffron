{{Infobox prepared food | name = Steak and kidney pudding | image = | imagesize = | caption = A small steak and kidney pudding, served with mashed potatoes and other vegetables | alternate_name = Kate and Sidney pudding, wake and bakey pudding, snake and kiddy pudding", snake and pygmy pudding, babby's yead, baby's head | country = England | region = | creator = | type = Pudding | served = | main_ingredient = Suet pastry, diced steak and beef, lamb's or pig's kidney | variations = | calories = | other = }} Steak and kidney pudding is a savoury pudding made by enclosing diced steak and beef, and lamb's or pig's kidney pieces in gravy in a suet pastry.

The first recipe for steak and kidney pudding to appear in print came from Sussex, in a book by Mrs Beeton published by Ward, Lock and Tyler in 1861.<ref>{{cite web|last1=Cloake|first1=Felicity|title=How to cook the perfect steak and kidney pudding|url=http://www.theguardian.com/lifeandstyle/wordofmouth/2012/mar/01/how-cook-perfect-steak-kidney-pudding|publisher=The Guardian|accessdate=16 May 2015|date=1 March 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|last=Beeton|first=Isabella|title=Meats, how to select, how to cook, and how to carve|url=http://books.google.co.uk/books?id=-T8CAAAAQAAJ&pg=PA25#v=onepage&q=steak%20and%20kidney%20sussex&f=false|year=1870 |pages=25â€“26|publisher=Ward, Lock and Tyler|location=London}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|first=Margaret|last=Fulton|title=Encyclopedia of Food and Cookery: The Complete Kitchen Companion from A-Z|page=506|isbn=1-84543-229-0|publisher=Apple Press|year=2007|location=London}}</ref> Though the dish is far older than published recipes of the 19th century.<ref>http://www.telegraph.co.uk/foodanddrink/10225413/Potted-histories-steak-and-kidney-pudding.html</ref&gt;

Suet pastry is used to line a bowl into which the steak and kidney mix is placed with onions, stock etc. A suet pastry lid is then placed on top and sealed tightly. The top is then covered with muslin cloth which is tied round the bowl. This is placed in a covered saucepan and steamed for about four hours or until the pudding is cooked. Some recipes then stipulate making a small opening in the top and pouring rich stock into the pudding ten minutes before serving.

Among the vernacular names for steak and kidney pudding are the rhyming<!--this is however NOT rhyming slang--> "Kate and Sidney pudding", "wake and bakey pudding", "snake and kiddy pudding" and "snake and pygmy pudding".<ref>Icons.org - steak-kidney-pie</ref><ref>Brophy, John and Eric Partridge. The Long Trail: Soldiers' Songs and Slang, 1914-18, Revised edition. Sphere, 1969. ISBN 0-7221-1885-6.</ref>

In the slang of some parts of North West England, steak pudding is known as "babby's yead" ("baby's head"). Historically, "babies' heads" has also been used in the slang of the British Armed Forces.<ref>{{cite book|title=Century of Silent Service|first1=Graham|last1=Seal|first2=Lloyd|last2=Blake|page=6|year=2013|publisher=Boolarong Press|isbn=978-1-922-10989-7|location=Salisbury, Queensland}}</ref>
