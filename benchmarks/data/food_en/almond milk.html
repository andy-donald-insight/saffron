Almond milk

Almond milk is a plant milk with a creamy texture and nutty taste. It contains neither cholesterol nor lactose, and is often consumed by the lactose-intolerant and others who want to avoid dairy products, including vegans. Commercial almond milk comes in sweetened, unsweetened, plain, vanilla and chocolate flavors, and is usually enriched with vitamins. It can also be made at home using a blender, almonds and water.<ref name=pros>{{cite web | url=http://www.adelaidenow.com.au/ipad/the-pros-and-cons-of-almond-milk/story-fn6jaj16-1225984312290 | title=The pros and cons of almond milk | last=Larmer | first=Christina | date=2011-01-09 | publisher=Adelaide Now | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref>

In the US sales of almond milk overtook soy milk in 2013 and in the UK in 2014, where sales increased from 36 million liters in 2011 to 92 million in 2013.<ref>Rebecca Burn-Callander, "How the UK is going crazy for almond milk", The Daily Telegraph, 17 November 2014.</ref>

History

In the Middle Ages, almond milk was known in both the Islamic world and Christendom. As a nut (the "fruit of a plant"), it is suitable for consumption during Lent. Almond milk was a staple of medieval kitchens because cow's milk could not keep for long without spoiling.<ref name="Bynum">{{Citation|url=https://books.google.co.il/books?id=9DUgmJGxZyEC |title=Holy Feast and Holy Fast: The Religious Significance of Food to Medieval Women |publisher=University of California Press |author=Bynum, W.C. |year=1988 |isbn=978-0-520-06329-7 |page=41}}</ref>

Historically, almond milk was also called amygdalate after the Latin name for the almond. It was consumed over a region extending from the Iberian Peninsula to East Asia.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.vegparadise.com/highestperch31.html | title=Vegetarians in Paradise/Almond History, Almond Nutrition, Almond Recipe | publisher=vegparadise.com | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref> Le Viandier, a 14th-century recipe collection, contains a recipe for almond milk and recommends its use as a substitute for animal milk during fast days.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.theage.com.au/articles/2004/05/03/1083436518647.html | title=Nuts-and-bolts brekkie | last=Chiodo | first=Tony | date=2004-05-04 | publisher=The Age | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref>

In the United States, almond milk remained a fairly niche health food item until the early 2000s, when its popularity began to increase. In 2011 alone, almond milk sales increased by 79%.<ref>{{cite web | url=https://finance.yahoo.com/news/almond-white-milk-dairy-alternatives-174100672.html | title=With Almond as the New White Milk, Dairy Alternatives Make Further Inroads | work=Marketwire | author=David Sprinkle | date=2012-01-19 | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref> In 2013, it surpassed soy milk as the most popular plant-based milk in the U.S.<ref name="soy milk fades">{{cite web | url=http://www.businessweek.com/articles/2013-08-21/soy-milk-fades-as-americans-opt-for-drinkable-almonds | title=Soy Milk Fades as Americans Opt for Drinkable Almonds | first=Venessa | last=Wong | date=2013-08-21 | publisher=BusinessWeek | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref> Popular brands of almond milk include Blue Diamond's Almond Breeze and WhiteWave Foods' Silk PureAlmond.<ref name="soy milk fades" />

Nutrition

Almond paste, used as a quick base to prepare almond milk

Almonds are rich in nutrients including fiber, vitamin E, magnesium, selenium, manganese, zinc, potassium, iron, phosphorus, tryptophan, copper, and calcium.<ref name=pros/><ref>{{cite web | url=http://almondmilk.net/almond-milk-nutrition/ | title=Nuts-and-bolts brekkie | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref> "The UK Institute of Food Research found finely ground almonds contain potential probiotic properties that could help boost digestive health by increasing the levels of certain beneficial bacteria in the stomach".<ref name="almond">{{cite web | last=Larmer | first=Christina | title=Almond Milk | url=http://www.bodyandsoul.com.au/nutrition/nutrition+tips/almond+milk+,10031 | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref>

Commercially-sold almond milk has less protein than cow's milk and other animal milk substances.<ref name="almond" /> For children with atopic dermatitis under two years of age, almond milk is not a suitable replacement for breast milk, cow's milk, or hydrolyzed formulas due to the low protein.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22624441 | title=Severe malnutrition resulting from use of rice milk in food elimination diets for atopic dermatitis | author=Keller MD, Shuker M, Heimall J, Cianferoni A. | journal=Isr Med Assoc J | date=Jan 2012 | volume=14 | issue=1 | pages=40â€“2 | publisher=PubMed | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref>

Health

Almond milk is a popular alternative to dairy milk. It contains less protein than dairy milk. Some commercial almond milks contain added calcium. Features of almond milk include:

  • low in calories compared to dairy milk;
  • no cholesterol or saturated fat;
  • low sodium content;
  • high calcium levels, which may reduce the risk of developing arthritis and osteoporosis;
  • 50 percent recommended daily value of vitamin E;
  • unsweetened almond milk has a low glycemic index, reducing risk of diabetes, and
  • no lactose, making it suitable for lactose-intolerant people.<ref>http://www.lifehack.org/articles/lifestyle/benefits-almond-milk.html</ref><!--needs a better source-->

Production

The basic method of modern domestic almond milk production is to grind almonds in a blender with water, then strain out the almond pulp (flesh) with a strainer, cheesecloth,<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.thekitchn.com/how-to-make-almond-milk-at-home-cooking-lessons-from-the-kitchn-189996 | title=How to Make Almond Milk at Home | last=Christensen | first=Emma | date=2013-05-28 | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref> or nut milk bag. Almond milk can also be made by adding water to almond butter.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.makealmondmilk.com/make-almond-milk-using-almond-butter/ | title=Make Almond Milk Using Almond Butter | date=August 2013 | accessdate=2014-10-14}}</ref>

In commercially-produced almond milk, 1 liter has approximately the same nutritional content as 16 almonds.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Lay off the almond milk, you ignorant hipsters|url = http://www.motherjones.com/tom-philpott/2014/07/lay-off-almond-milk-ignorant-hipsters|website = Mother Jones|accessdate = 2015-10-25|last = Philpott|first = Tom}} The calculation is 1 oz (28g) of almonds in 48 oz (1.42 liters) of almond milk. At 1.2g per almond, that translates to about 16 almonds per liter.</ref> c
