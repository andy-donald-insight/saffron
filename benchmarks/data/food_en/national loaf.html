The National Loaf was a bread made from wholemeal flour with added calcium and vitamins, introduced in Britain during World War II.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.yourdiscovery.com/reality/1940s_house/kitchen/index.shtml |title=The 1940s House: The Kitchen |publisher=Discovery Communications, Inc |year=2010 |accessdate=2012-03-15}}</ref> Introduced in 1942, the loaf (similar to today's brown bread) was made from wholemeal flour to combat wartime shortages of white flour. The loaf was abolished in October 1956.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.bakersfederation.org.uk/20th_century.aspx |title=The 20th Century |publisher=Federation of Bakers |year=2007 |accessdate=2012-03-15}}</ref><ref name="Oddy2003">{{cite book|author=Derek J. Oddy|title=From plain fare to fusion food: British diet from the 1890s to the 1990s|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=hLlYiS8xwF0C&pg=PA171|accessdate=15 March 2012|year=2003|publisher=Boydell Press|isbn=978-0-85115-934-8|page=171}}</ref>

The National Loaf was grey, mushy and unappetising; only one person in seven preferred it to white bread, which became unavailable. The government insisted on it because it saved space in shipping food to Britain.<ref>{{cite book|author=Richard W. Lacey|title=Hard to Swallow: A Brief History of Food|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=gAg1gyCK6kwC&pg=PA108|year=1994|publisher=Cambridge University Press|pages=108â€“9}}</ref><ref> Angus Calder, The people's war: Britain 1939-45 (1969) pp 276-77</ref> Eleanor Roosevelt, the American First Lady, visiting Buckingham Palace in 1942, noted that "We were served on gold and silver plates, but our bread was the same kind of war bread every other family had to eat."<ref>{{cite book|author=Edna Healey|title=The Queen's House: A Social History of Buckingham Palace|url=http://books.google.com/books?id=XISeDvSS_rAC&pg=PT275|year=2012|publisher=Pegasus Books|page=275}}</ref>
