A crostata is an Italian baked tart or pie, also known as coppi in Naples and sfogliate in Lombardy.{{sfn|Capatti|Montanari|1999|p=60–61}} The earliest known use of crostata in its modern sense can be traced to the cookbooks Libro de Arte Coquinaria (Art of Cooking) by Martino da Como, published circa 1465,{{sfn|Scappi|1570|p=252}} and Cuoco napolitano (Neapolitan recipes), published in the late 15th century containing a recipe (number 94) titled Crostata de Caso, Pane, etc..{{sfn|Scully|2000|p=65}}<!-- note Scappi source refers to recipe de crostata piscium, recipe 133 in MS 319 at bibliotheque municipale of Châlons-sur-Marne, which may precede either of these uses -->

A crostata is a "rustic free-form version of an open fruit tart"{{sfn|Corley|2011|p=129}} that may also be baked in a pie plate.{{sfn|Adams|Rivard|2002|p=122}}

Historically, it also referred to an "open-faced sandwich or canapé" because of its crusted appearance,{{sfn|Scappi|1570|p=252}} or a chewet, a type of meat pie.{{sfn|Weekley|1967|p=402}}

Etymology

The name derives from the Latin word crustāta, the feminine past participle of crustāre (to encrust), and ultimately from the noun crusta (crust).{{sfn|Skeat|1911|p=125}} The French term croustade derives from it, from which the English term custard derives.{{sfn|Skeat|1911|p=125}} The word crostata appeared in the earliest Italian dictionaries, included in the 1612 dictionary Vocabolario degli accademici della Crusca (compiled from 1591-1608){{sfn|Sessa|2001}} by the Accademia della Crusca and the Scuola Normale Superiore di Pisa,{{sfn|Accademia della Crusca and Scuola Normale Superiore di Pisa|1612|location=Crostata}} and the 1617 dictionary Il memoriale della lingua italiana: ridotto in ordine d'alfabeto per commodità del lettore by Giacomo Pergamino, in which it was defined as a type of torta.{{sfn|Pergamino|1617|p=145}}

Description

Traditionally, a crostata consisted of a base, usually three layers, of friable dough "flavoured with clarified fat and butter".{{sfn|Capatti|Montanari|1999|p=60}} Today, shortcrust pastry is used instead. It is differentiated from a torta by its filling: a crostata has an inconsistent chunky filling, whereas a torta has a consistent filling made of blended ingredients.{{sfn|Capatti|Montanari|1999|p=60}} There are "endless variations"{{sfn|Worthington|2012}} of both sweet and savoury crostata,{{sfn|Adams|Rivard|2002|p=122}} the sweet ones usually served as a dessert.

Sweet variations use fruit preserves as a filling, typically apricot, cherry, peach or nectarine, or berries.{{sfn|Worthington|2012}} The crostata can also be blind-baked and then filled with pastry cream (crema pasticciera) topped with pieces of fresh fruit; this is called crostata di frutta. In his 1570 cookbook Opera dell'arte del cucinare, Bartolomeo Scappi included a recipe for a crostata of plums and sour cherries,{{sfn|Capatti|Montanari|1999|p=60}} and others for quince, and pears. A modern version is crostata alla nutella, which has Nutella as the filling.{{sfn|Giallo Zafferano: Crostata all Nutella}}

Ingredients for a savoury crostata may include meat, fish, or vegetables,{{sfn|Capatti|Montanari|1999|p=60}} which are pre-cooked.{{sfn|Adams|Rivard|2002|p=122}} Opera dell'arte del cucinare included a recipe for a "crostata of crabmeat and shrimp", and also stated that to instead make a torta, the shrimp and crab should be crushed.{{sfn|Capatti|Montanari|1999|p=60}} A popular sweet variant, especially in central Italy, is crostata di ricotta, made with ricotta mixed with sugar and lemon zest, and which may additionally include cocoa or raisins.{{sfn|Cushing}}{{sfn|Rocco}}{{sfn|Giallo Zafferano: Crostata di ricotta}}

Scappi included many recipes for crostata in Opera dell'arte del cucinare. For meat and seafood based crostata, there were recipes using pork jowls or prosciutto,{{sfn|Scappi|1570|p=254}} crayfish, anchovies, or oysters. Other savoury crostata recipes included a crostata with creamy cheese referred to as a butirata,{{sfn|Scappi|1570|p=254}} those with truffles or field mushrooms,{{sfn|Scappi|1570|p=463–464}} one with artichoke or cardoon hearts,{{sfn|Scappi|1570|p=463–464}} and one with "the viscera of any sort of turtle".{{sfn|Scappi|1570|p=523}}
