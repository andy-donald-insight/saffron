Pandan cake is a light, fluffy or sponge cake<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=j9bD37IuSscC&pg=PA127|title=What Herb Is That?|publisher=|page=127|accessdate=29 December 2014}}</ref> of Indonesian and Malaysian origins with Dutch influence flavoured with the juice of Pandanus amaryllifolius leaves.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=GmqEAwAAQBAJ&pg=PA615|title=The World Cookbook|publisher=|page=615|accessdate=29 December 2014}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.laweekly.com/squidink/2010/05/26/cheap-sweets-pandan-chiffon|title=Cheap Sweets: Pandan Chiffon|date=22 December 2014|work=LA Weekly|accessdate=29 December 2014}}</ref> The cakes are light green in tone<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=GLa2Gn1YRd4C&pg=PA288|title=A World of Cake|publisher=|page=288|accessdate=29 December 2014}}</ref> due to the chlorophyll in the leaf juice. It also sometimes contains green food colouring to further enhance its colour. The cakes are sometimes not made with the leaf juice but instead simply flavoured with Pandanus extract, in which case colouring is added if a green colour is desired.<ref>{{cite web | url = http://articles.latimes.com/2011/may/05/food/la-fo-chiffoncakerec4-20110505 | title = Recipe: Pandan chiffon cake with coconut glaze | date = May 5, 2011 |work = Los Angeles Times| accessdate = November 5, 2011}}</ref>

It is popular in Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines, Singapore, Vietnam, Laos, Thailand, Sri Lanka, Hong Kong and China. It is also known as pandan chiffon.

Names in different languages

  • Indonesian: bolu pandan
  • Malay: kek pandan
  • Vietnamese: Bánh phú sĩ
  • Chinese: 班蘭蛋糕-baan1 laan4 daan6 gou1
